# Project Constitution

This document governs how the Resonance project is designed, documented, and decided. It has authority over all other documents. When another document conflicts with the constitution, the constitution wins until it is formally amended here.

## Purpose

Resonance is a science-fiction game franchise whose world is rendered primarily through sound and whose foundation is accessibility. This constitution exists so the project can grow without losing its identity, and so any contributor or assistant can understand the rules quickly.

## Single source of truth

The GitHub repository is the single source of truth. Every approved design decision belongs inside the Markdown documentation, not only in chat. A decision that lives only in a conversation is not yet real. To make it real, edit the relevant living document and record the change in the Decision Log.

## Core philosophy

These pillars guide every decision.

- Audio is gameplay.
- Story teaches mechanics.
- Accessibility is foundational.
- Perspective creates replayability.
- Knowledge is progression.
- The AI is a character.
- Technology exists to serve people.
- Curiosity is always rewarded.
- Every meaningful decision gains something while sacrificing something.
- Players should feel remembered.

## What the project explores

Resonance explores real themes through interactive gameplay rather than lectures. These themes include identity, adaptation, disability, history, trust, memory, privacy, survival, artificial intelligence, and ethics.

## Accessibility as foundation

Accessibility is the foundation of the universe, not a feature added at the end. No player may be locked out of the game because of disability. Every gameplay mechanic must be playable through multiple input and output methods. The Accessibility Bible defines the details, and no design may violate it.

## Design principles

These principles are the definitive statement of how Resonance is designed. They govern every game in the franchise. Each design-note bible and specialist bible must remain consistent with them.

### What Resonance always does

- Renders the world through sound first, so it can be understood with the eyes closed.
- Treats accessibility as standard equipment that every player benefits from.
- Teaches its mechanics through story, place, and consequence rather than through lectures.
- Rewards curiosity, patience, and care.
- Lets the world continue to live whether or not any particular player is present.
- Gives every meaningful choice something gained and something surrendered.
- Remembers the player, so that actions leave lasting traces in the world.

### What Resonance never does

Resonance never sacrifices a person's ability to play for the sake of spectacle, and never treats a human trait as a punchline. The complete, binding list of prohibitions is recorded below under Things we will never do, and it governs the whole franchise.

### Accessibility philosophy

Accessibility is a right, not a difficulty setting. No information may depend on a single sense, and no path may depend on a single input method. Accessible play is the primary design target, and every other consideration is shaped around it rather than bolted on afterwards.

### Community philosophy

The world is a living community of people, not a backdrop for the player. Neighbours are born, grow, work, celebrate, grieve, and remember on their own schedules. The player is a resident among them, welcomed but never central to their existence.

### Narrative philosophy

Story and mechanics are one thing. Narrative exists to give gameplay meaning, and gameplay exists to give narrative weight. Exposition is avoided in favour of environmental storytelling, overheard life, and knowledge that is discovered rather than announced.

### Player philosophy

The player is a participant, not a protagonist the universe revolves around. Players are never the chosen one, never the sole solution to a crisis, and never able to overwrite the world's canon. Their agency is real, and it is exercised within a world that has its own life.

### Scientific philosophy

The science is internally consistent and takes its own rules seriously. Discoveries follow evidence, and understanding is refined over time. When knowledge changes, it changes because the world revealed more, not because the record was quietly rewritten.

### Representation philosophy

Characters are whole people. Identity, disability, culture, and history shape a character without ever being the entirety of that character. No group is reduced to a single trait, and no species is treated as though it holds only one culture.

### World-building philosophy

The universe is built from the acoustic identity outward. Every place, object, creature, and technology has a real presence and a reason to exist. The world holds deliberate mysteries, and those mysteries are revealed only through play, never spoiled in the documentation.

### Multiplayer philosophy

Multiplayer is an invitation, never a requirement. Every campaign is fully playable and fully meaningful alone. Shared play adds company and collaboration to a world that already stands on its own.

### Knowledge philosophy

Knowledge is progression. What the player learns must always be able to change something in the world, in dialogue, or in what becomes possible next. Knowledge that changes nothing does not belong in the game.

### Combat philosophy

Violence is never the only answer. Conflict has many resolutions, and force is treated as a last resort with consequences. Weapons exist as emergency tools carrying responsibility, not as the central expression of play.

### Audio philosophy

Audio is gameplay, navigation, memory, communication, and identity, and it is never mere decoration. Everything has a deliberate acoustic identity, and silence is used as intentionally as sound. The Audio Bible defines the details.

### UI philosophy

The interface serves the player rather than the other way around. It is legible, navigable by screen reader, operable through multiple input methods, and consistent across the franchise. No essential function is reachable through only one sense or one control scheme.

### Save philosophy

Progress is respected. Saving is reliable, understandable, and never punishes the player for stepping away. Because the world continues to live independently, returning to it feels like coming home to a place that kept living, not like resuming a paused recording.

### Exploration philosophy

Exploration is invited and rewarded. Secrets, optional conversations, hidden histories, and quiet corners exist for the curious, and reward attention rather than punishing its absence. Discovery is a gentle engine of progression, not a gate.

## Things we will never do

These prohibitions are absolute. They bind every game, chapter, mechanic, character, and system in the franchise. The Canon Rules document elaborates several of them as operational rules; where it does, it must remain consistent with this list, which governs.

- Never require vision.
- Never require hearing.
- Never require one control method.
- Never force multiplayer.
- Never use accessibility as a cheat.
- Never make disability a joke.
- Never reduce a character to identity alone.
- Never make the player the chosen one.
- Never pause civilisation because the player logged out.
- Never treat an entire species as one culture.
- Never retcon established canon without explicit creator approval.
- Never make violence the only solution.
- Never make NPCs exist solely to give quests.
- Never make accessibility optional.
- Never design a mechanic without narrative purpose.
- Never design narrative without gameplay purpose.

## Documentation rules

- Every document uses proper Markdown headings, in a logical hierarchy.
- Every document must be navigable by screen readers.
- Never use bold text in place of a heading.
- Avoid unnecessary tables. Prefer headings and lists.
- Use descriptive link text rather than raw URLs where possible.
- Maintain one living document per file. Never create competing versions.
- GitHub provides the version history, so there is no need for versioned copies of a file.

## How decisions are made

1. A decision is proposed and discussed.
2. When approved, the relevant living document is edited to reflect it.
3. The change is summarised in the Decision Log with a date.
4. If the decision raises new uncertainties, they are added to Open Questions.

## Amending this constitution

This constitution may be amended by editing this document. Every amendment must also be recorded in the Decision Log so the reason and date are preserved.
