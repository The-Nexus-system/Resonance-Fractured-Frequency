# Accessibility Presentation

This document is the craft reference for how accessibility is presented across every recurring character, every profession, and every surface of Resonance: Fractured Frequency. It is part of the Player Identity and Character Presentation Bible. It exists so that artists, animators, audio designers, caption authors, braille and tactile designers, UI designers, writers, and gameplay programmers all present accessibility the same way: as an ordinary, foundational, dignified part of the world rather than a feature bolted on at the end.

This document rests on two authorities. The Canon Rules document is the canon authority, and the Character Identity Map is the locked identity source for each recurring character's profession, ethnicity, gender, pronouns, and disability. Where anything here appears to differ from either, those documents govern. The Accessibility Bible defines the accessibility systems themselves; this document defines how those systems and the characters who live with them are presented.

This document defines presentation standards and craft philosophy. It does not invent character backstory, ship history, place names, or new game systems. Where it describes a character, it applies only the identity facts already locked in the Character Identity Map, and it frames craft choices as guidance rather than as settled events.

## Guiding Principles for Accessibility Presentation

Accessibility in Resonance is standard equipment, not special equipment. The presentation of accessibility must always reflect that. A prosthetic, a sleeve projector, a navigation faceplate, or a neural-interface communication system is presented the way a uniform, a tool belt, or a rank insignia is presented: as an ordinary part of a competent person doing their work.

These principles apply to every section that follows.

- Accessibility is foundational, so it is never framed as inspirational, tragic, or exceptional. A character using an access method is simply a person living their life.
- Recognition emerges through observation and lived experience, not intrusive interface labels. Access methods are part of how the player learns to recognise a character, not a badge the interface announces.
- Every important piece of information reaches the player through more than one channel, so no player is excluded by their own access needs.
- Access methods are presented as competence, not limitation. The camera, the microphone, the caption, and the haptic pattern all treat the method as effective and complete.
- Characters accommodate one another as an ordinary part of the world, without praise, permission, or spectacle.
- Representation is character-first. An access method describes one meaningful aspect of a person; it never replaces personality, relationships, values, strengths, flaws, goals, and growth.

### Multichannel Redundancy as a Presentation Rule

No single sense may be the only path to any important information. This is both an accessibility system rule and a presentation rule, because presentation is where redundancy either succeeds or fails.

- Every event that matters to gameplay or story should be expressible through sound, through text, through braille, and through touch.
- Redundant channels should carry the same meaning, not competing meanings. The caption, the spoken line, and the haptic pulse should agree.
- Redundancy is authored deliberately. A haptic pattern is designed to mean something specific, never generated automatically from music or from a sound's amplitude.
- When a channel is unavailable to a player, the remaining channels must still carry the full meaning without loss.

### Dignity and Ordinariness in Accessibility Presentation

The default emotional register for any access method is ordinariness. The world has already accepted the method; the presentation must reflect a world that finished that conversation long ago.

- Access methods are never introduced with a swell of music, a lingering camera, or a line of dialogue that marks them as remarkable.
- Characters do not thank the ship for accessible design and do not ask permission to use standard equipment.
- Other characters do not narrate a person's access needs for the player's benefit. Understanding accrues through observation.
- Difficulty, fatigue, or frustration with a device is portrayed as an ordinary human relationship with a tool, not as suffering caused by disability itself.

## Blind-First Descriptions

Blind-first description is the discipline of describing people, places, and events so that a player who does not use the visual channel receives a complete, vivid, and usable understanding. Because audio is gameplay in Resonance, blind-first description is not a fallback; it is a primary authoring craft.

### Principles of Blind-First Description

- Lead with what can be heard, felt, and inferred, then add visual detail as enrichment rather than as the load-bearing information.
- Describe function and behaviour before surface appearance. What a person is doing and how they move tells the player more than what colour something is.
- Prioritise the information the player needs to act. Spatial relationships, distance, direction, and change over time come first.
- Keep descriptions concrete and specific. Replace vague visual adjectives with sound, motion, texture, temperature, and rhythm.
- Never require sight to resolve a puzzle, identify a character, or understand a story beat. If a beat currently depends on sight, it must be re-authored to carry through sound or touch.

### Blind-First Description of Characters

Characters are recognised through observation. Blind-first description gives the non-visual player the same recognition signals a sighted player receives, expressed through channels that do not depend on sight.

- Describe a character through voice, speech rhythm, movement, professional habits, environmental sound, and relationships, consistent with the recognition signals defined in canon.
- For Mara Chen, the chief engineering systems specialist, present recognition through a sure, listening gait, the habit of describing systems as sounds, and the audio and tactile cues of her engineering helmet's navigation faceplate. Her blindness is presented as expertise expressed through sound and touch, never as helplessness.
- For Samira Kade, the senior medical officer, present recognition through the soft mechanical whisper of her sleeve projector, her attentiveness to sight and vibration, and the visible rhythm of sign or text she projects. A blind-first description conveys her presence through the projector's sound and the responses of those around her.
- For the members of the Vale System, present recognition through voice, accent, vocabulary, breathing, posture, gait, gesture, and priorities, because the body is constant and the presentation changes with whoever is fronting. A blind-first description must let the player recognise which member is fronting through these audible and behavioural cues rather than through appearance.
- For Talia Rusk, the security lead, present recognition through steady movement, the sound and rhythm of a prosthetic limb in motion, and a habit of placing herself between others and a threat.
- For Soren Qadir, the senior research scientist, present recognition through the sound of a repulsor-assisted chair, a persistent investigative manner, and the way he refuses to leave until his team is away.
- For Jonah Briggs, the senior non-commissioned officer, present recognition through a calm voice reading out priorities and a steady logistical cadence.
- For Rowan Ives, the maintenance supervisor, present recognition through his knowledge of the hidden body of the ship and the ways he has redesigned his work around chronic pain and arthritis.
- For Mira Ives, a habitation-deck child, present recognition through the sounds of her neural-interface augmentative and alternative communication system and through her being often the first to hear when something aboard the ship is wrong.

### Blind-First Description of Spaces and Events

- Establish the acoustic identity of a location first, so the player recognises where they are by sound, consistent with the canon rule that every location has an acoustic identity.
- Describe scale and enclosure through reverberation, echo, and the behaviour of sound in the space rather than through visual dimensions alone.
- Describe motion and danger through direction, distance, approach, and retreat, so the player can act without sight.
- Use silence deliberately, as a storytelling tool that communicates emotion, danger, absence, distance, or anticipation, never as an absence of finished audio.

## Audio-First Descriptions

Audio-first description is the broader craft of building the world so that sound carries meaning first and visuals confirm it. It overlaps with blind-first description but extends to every player, because in Resonance the player should hear the world before seeing it and often instead of seeing it.

### Principles of Audio-First Description

- Sound is progression. The player should hear the community and the world growing before receiving visual confirmation.
- Every important object, person, and hazard has an audio signature that is distinct, consistent, and learnable.
- Audio descriptions are spatial. Direction, distance, elevation, and movement are encoded so the player can navigate and act by ear.
- Audio-first description respects the independent volume channels defined in the Accessibility Bible, so that Faience, navigation, dialogue, combat, music, ambience, accessibility prompts, and reality announcements never bury one another.

### Audio Identity for Recurring Characters

Every recurring character has an audio identity that lets the player recognise them without a label. The following are presentation anchors drawn from the locked identity facts; the detailed voice profiles live in each character's presentation profile.

- Mara Chen is anchored by a listening gait and by the audio output of her navigation faceplate, which turns visual data into audio and tactile cues.
- Samira Kade is anchored by the soft mechanical whisper of her sleeve projector, which shows sign or live text; her audio identity is defined partly by the sounds of her equipment rather than by her own speech.
- The Vale System is anchored by four distinct audio identities that share one body. Voice, accent, vocabulary, breathing pattern, and speech rhythm change with whoever is fronting, while the body's footsteps and equipment sounds remain constant.
- Soren Qadir is anchored by the sound of a repulsor-assisted chair with a manual backup.
- Talia Rusk is anchored by the rhythm of a prosthetic limb in motion and steady, deliberate movement.
- Mira Ives is anchored by the sounds of her neural-interface communication system, which give her voice in the world.
- Rowan Ives is anchored by the sounds of maintenance work and by movement adapted around chronic pain.
- Jonah Briggs is anchored by a calm, even vocal cadence used to read out priorities.

### Faience and Audio-First Presentation

Faience is a Shawabti artificial intelligence and stands outside human race, ethnicity, and gender. Faience is silent through Operation One and first stirs, wordlessly, in a damaged pod on Attack Day. Audio-first presentation must honour that silence.

- Through Operation One, Faience contributes no spoken audio identity. The world is carried by human voices, environmental sound, and silence.
- Faience's first stirring is wordless. It is presented through sound and sensation rather than language, and its meaning must reach the player through more than one channel.
- After Faience speaks, its audio presentation becomes a primary interface, but this document does not define events beyond the locked facts; it only requires that Faience's silence and first stirring be preserved exactly as canon states.

## Caption Standards

Captions are a primary output channel, not a courtesy. They must convey everything a hearing player receives through sound, including speech, speaker identity, tone, and meaningful non-speech audio. Captions are automatically enabled when a player selects no auditory input.

### Principles of Caption Authoring

- Captions convey meaning, not just words. When tone, volume, or delivery changes the meaning, the caption reflects it.
- Captions identify the speaker whenever the speaker is not obvious, using the name the character currently answers to.
- Captions describe meaningful non-speech sound, such as approaching danger, environmental cues, and the sounds of equipment, when that sound carries information.
- Captions are timed to the audio they describe and remain on screen long enough to be read comfortably.
- Captions never reveal information the player has not yet earned. A speaker whose identity the player has not learned is described by an honest, non-spoiling label rather than by a name the player does not yet know.

### Caption Standards for the Vale System

Captions for the Vale System require special care, because the body is one person and the fronting member changes. Captions must never present the members as four separate people and must never invent a single name such as "Captain Mara Vale".

- Speaker identification in captions uses the name the currently fronting member answers to, consistent with the personal identifiers that update automatically as ordinary ship operations.
- Before the player has learned to recognise a member, captions may identify the speaker by the constant office rather than by a member name, so that recognition still emerges through observation rather than through the caption doing the work.
- Captions reflect the audible differences between members, such as changes in vocabulary and speech rhythm, without adding narration that explains the switch. The switch is shown, not announced.
- Captions never contradict the canon that the body is constant and the presentation changes.

### Caption Standards for Non-Speech Communication

Some recurring characters communicate through channels other than spoken voice, and captions must represent those channels faithfully.

- When Samira Kade communicates through sign or through live text on her sleeve projector, captions present the content of her communication as her own words, not as a description of her signing.
- When Mira Ives communicates through her neural-interface augmentative and alternative communication system, captions present her output as her own speech, with her own voice and personality, never as a device speaking for her.
- Captions for augmentative and alternative communication never add commentary such as noting that a device produced the words. The person is speaking; the device is how.

### Visual Caption Presentation Standards

- Captions use high-contrast, resizable text with adjustable background opacity so they remain legible against any scene.
- Caption placement avoids obscuring information the player needs, and speaker labels remain consistent in position and style.
- Captions never depend on colour alone to convey speaker identity or meaning, because colour is not available to every player.

## Braille Descriptions

Braille output is a first-class communication channel. Braille display support is enabled when available, and it is automatically prioritised for players who use no auditory input, including DeafBlind players. Braille is not a transcript of captions; it is authored so that a braille reader receives complete, well-paced information.

### Principles of Braille Description

- Braille output conveys the same meaning as speech and captions, including speaker identity, tone where it changes meaning, and meaningful non-speech events.
- Braille is paced for reading by touch. Information is prioritised and sequenced so the most important content reaches the reader first.
- Braille descriptions avoid visual-only shorthand and translate visual cues into descriptions a braille reader can act on.
- Braille output respects the reader's control over verbosity, so a reader can request more or less detail without losing essential information.

### Braille Description of Characters and Recognition

- Braille recognition of a character follows the same observation-based signals as other channels, expressed as concise, readable descriptions of voice character, movement, habits, and relationships.
- For the Vale System, braille descriptions identify the fronting member by the name they currently answer to and convey the behavioural differences between members, without ever presenting them as separate people.
- Braille descriptions of access methods present them as ordinary equipment, in the same neutral register used for uniforms and tools.

### Braille Description of Spaces and Events

- Braille descriptions of a location establish its acoustic and physical identity concisely, so the reader can orient quickly.
- Time-critical information reaches the braille reader in a form and at a pace that lets them act, because braille reading is sequential and must never be the slowest path to safety.
- Where a haptic pattern and a braille description describe the same event, they agree in meaning.

## Tactile Information

Tactile information is an authored communication channel with its own deliberate language. Haptics are not a byproduct of music or sound amplitude; they are designed to communicate specific meaning such as direction, threat, distance, object proximity, urgency, environmental hazards, navigation, success, and failure.

### Principles of Tactile Presentation

- The tactile language is deliberate and learnable. A given pattern always means the same thing, so the player can build fluency the way they build fluency in any language.
- Tactile patterns are distinct enough to be told apart by touch alone, without relying on sound or sight to disambiguate them.
- Tactile information carries the same meaning as its companion audio, caption, and braille, so a player using touch as a primary channel loses nothing.
- Tactile urgency replaces spoken urgency for players who use no auditory input, so that timing and danger are still felt.

### Tactile Presentation of Characters and Environments

- Tactile cues can encode the direction and distance of a recognised character, supporting recognition through movement even for players who use touch as a primary channel.
- Tactile cues encode environmental identity and hazards, so a location's danger and layout can be felt as well as heard.
- Tactile cues for access equipment, such as the operation of a navigation faceplate, are presented as ordinary feedback, consistent with accessibility as standard equipment.

### Tactile Presentation of Accessibility Devices in the Fiction

Some characters in the fiction use tactile and haptic technology as part of their own access, and its presentation should match the same deliberate, dignified standard applied to the player's tactile language.

- Mara Chen's navigation faceplate turns visual data into audio and tactile cues. Its tactile output is presented as precise, informative, and trusted, an extension of her expertise rather than a crutch.
- Tactile signing and tactile communication used between characters are presented as fluent, natural conversation, never as slow or effortful by default.

## Sign-Language Animation

Sign language is a full language, and its animation must be authored with the same rigour as spoken dialogue. Sign language appears in the world, notably through Samira Kade, whose communication includes sign shown through her sleeve projector, and potentially between any characters who share it.

### Principles of Sign-Language Animation

- Sign is animated as language, with accurate handshapes, movement, and the non-manual markers, such as facial expression and posture, that carry grammar and meaning.
- Sign animation is authored with expert input from fluent signers and, where a specific sign language is depicted, from members of that language's community. This document does not assign a specific sign language; that choice is made with community expertise.
- Sign is never reduced to generic gesturing or decorative hand motion. Incorrect or invented signing is not acceptable placeholder content.
- Non-manual markers are animated with the same care as the hands, because facial expression, eye behaviour, and posture are grammatically essential, not optional flourish.

### Sign-Language Presentation for Samira Kade

- Samira Kade is Deaf and communicates through a sleeve projector that shows sign or live text. Her signing is presented as her natural, fluent language, and its content is conveyed to the player through captions and braille as her own words.
- Her sleeve projector is presented as ordinary standard equipment, with its soft mechanical whisper as part of her recognisable presence.
- When Samira reads a room by sight and vibration, her attentiveness is animated as competence and command of her environment, never as a deficit to be pitied.
- Other characters who sign with her are animated as fluent partners in conversation, reflecting a world where communication diversity is ordinary.

### Sign-Language Animation and Camera Presentation

- The camera and framing must keep both hands and the face legible during signed dialogue, because meaning is lost if either is cropped or obscured.
- Lighting must keep hands and face clearly readable, using the seizure-safe lighting standard already built into the ship's design.
- Signed dialogue is given the same pacing respect as spoken dialogue, without rushing or truncating for the convenience of other channels.

## Eye Gaze

Eye gaze appears in Resonance in two ways: as an input method available to players, and as an expressive behaviour that characters use to communicate. Both require careful, respectful presentation.

### Eye Gaze as Player Input

- Eye-gaze input is one of many supported activation methods, and no important action ever depends on a single mandatory input, consistent with the accessible input standard.
- Eye-gaze interactions are forgiving, with generous targets and timing, and always have alternative activation methods so no player is excluded.
- Feedback for eye-gaze input reaches the player through more than one channel, so confirmation is not dependent on sight alone.

### Eye Gaze as Character Behaviour

- Eye focus is part of how the Vale System's fronting members are told apart. Facial expression and eye focus change naturally with whoever is fronting, and animation should use eye behaviour as one of the recognition signals.
- Eye gaze in characters is animated to reflect attention, intention, and relationship, and it is authored so that its meaning is also available through non-visual channels for players who do not use sight.
- Eye behaviour is never the only carrier of an important story beat, because not every player perceives it.

## Lip-Reading Considerations

Some players and some characters rely on lip movement to understand speech. Lip-reading support is a presentation responsibility, primarily in animation and camera work.

### Principles of Lip-Reading Support

- Characters' mouth movements are animated accurately to the words they speak, so that lip-reading players receive correct information rather than generic mouth flapping.
- Camera framing keeps a speaking character's face visible when practical, so lip-reading remains possible during important dialogue.
- Lighting keeps faces legible, consistent with the ship's seizure-safe lighting design, without harsh shadows that obscure the mouth.
- Lip-reading is treated as a supplementary channel and is never the only path to spoken content, which is always also available through captions, braille, and audio.

### Lip-Reading and the Limits of the Channel

- Presentation acknowledges that lip-reading is partial and effortful even for skilled readers, so it is supported but never required.
- When a character's face is turned away, obscured, or off-screen, the full content of their speech still reaches every player through the redundant channels.
- Non-speech communication, such as sign and augmentative and alternative communication, is never replaced by lip movement, because those are complete languages in their own right.

## DeafBlind Communication

DeafBlind play is a first-class, fully supported way to experience Resonance, configured by experience rather than diagnosis. DeafBlind communication combines braille output, tactile language, and any residual channels the player chooses to use, and its presentation must be complete on its own.

### Principles of DeafBlind Presentation

- For a DeafBlind player, braille and tactile channels together must carry the full experience, including character recognition, spatial awareness, danger, story, and community growth.
- Timed actions are removed or greatly extended in single-player when a player uses no auditory input, so that reading and feeling are never punished by the clock.
- Spoken urgency is replaced with tactile patterns and braille, so that danger and timing are still communicated through touch.
- Every important piece of information has a tactile or braille form, because for a DeafBlind player these may be the only available channels.

### DeafBlind Character Recognition and Story

- Character recognition for a DeafBlind player is delivered through braille descriptions and tactile signatures that convey the same observation-based signals other players receive, including the fronting member of the Vale System.
- Community and world progression, which are normally heard first, are conveyed to a DeafBlind player through braille and tactile channels, so that sound as progression is not lost when sound is not used.
- Silence, used as a storytelling tool, is conveyed to a DeafBlind player through the deliberate absence or stillness of tactile and braille cues, authored so the meaning of that stillness is clear.

### DeafBlind Communication Within the Fiction

- Tactile signing and other DeafBlind communication methods used between characters are presented as fluent, natural, and complete, reflecting the canon that every communication method is equally valid.
- Characters who communicate with a DeafBlind person do so as an ordinary part of daily life, without praise or spectacle, consistent with the world adapting to people.

## Characters Accommodating One Another

The most important accessibility presentation in Resonance is not a device; it is the way people treat one another. In this world, accommodation is ordinary. People adapt to each other as a matter of course, and the game presents this without commentary, praise, or permission-seeking.

### Principles of Ordinary Accommodation

- Accommodation is mutual and reciprocal. Everyone teaches and everyone keeps learning, and access flows in every direction rather than from the able toward the disabled.
- Accommodation is presented as competence and courtesy, the ordinary skill of working with the people you work with, not as charity.
- No character asks permission to use standard equipment, and no character is thanked for meeting an ordinary access need.
- Accommodation is shown through behaviour rather than explained through dialogue. The player learns how the crew supports one another by observing them.

### Examples of Ordinary Accommodation in Presentation

These examples illustrate the register and are drawn only from locked identity facts. They are presentation guidance, not scripted events.

- A crew member speaking with Samira Kade naturally faces her, keeps their hands and face visible, and reads her projected sign or text as ordinary conversation, because communication diversity is ordinary aboard the ship.
- A crew member coordinating with Mara Chen naturally describes systems as sounds and trusts her audio and tactile reading of the ship, because her expertise is expressed through sound and touch.
- A crew member working with Mira Ives naturally engages with her neural-interface communication as her own voice, giving her the same conversational patience and respect given to anyone.
- A crew member working with Soren Qadir naturally keeps routes clear for his repulsor-assisted chair and treats his manual backup as ordinary equipment.
- Crew members interacting with the Vale System naturally use the name the fronting member currently answers to and treat the automatic updates to name tape, badge, login, and voice authentication as completely ordinary, remarking on them only when directly relevant.
- A crew member working alongside Rowan Ives naturally accepts the ways he has redesigned his work around chronic pain and arthritis, because that redesign is simply how the work is done well.
- A crew member working with Talia Rusk treats her prosthetic limb as ordinary equipment and never frames her security work as remarkable because of it.
- A crew member working with Jonah Briggs relies on the ship's seizure-safe lighting as standard design, without treating it as a special allowance made for him.

### Accommodation as Worldbuilding

- The environment itself accommodates, because accessibility technology is standard equipment aboard human vessels. The presentation of rooms, controls, lighting, and interfaces reflects a world designed for everyone from the start.
- Accommodation extends to the player through the accessibility interview and the independent, combinable options defined in the Accessibility Bible, so the player experiences the same ordinary access the crew does.
- The world adapts to people, and people are never required to adapt to exclusion. Every presentation choice in this document serves that single standard.

## Relationship to Other Documents

This document is the presentation reference for accessibility across the Resonance franchise. It elaborates how accessibility is presented; it does not create accessibility rules or character facts.

- The Project Constitution defines the core philosophy this document serves and has authority over all documents.
- The Canon Rules document is the canon authority, including the rules that accessibility is standard equipment, that characters are recognised through observation, and that the Vale System is one shared body.
- The Character Identity Map is the locked source for each recurring character's profession, ethnicity, gender, pronouns, and disability.
- The Accessibility Bible defines the accessibility systems, the initial accessibility interview, DeafBlind gameplay, tactile language, accessible input, and independent volume channels that this document presents.
- The Audio Bible elaborates acoustic identity, silence, and sound as progression, which underpin blind-first and audio-first description.
- The AI Bible elaborates Faience as a character and the orientation layer that drives navigation announcements.
- The other parts of the Player Identity and Character Presentation Bible carry each recurring character's full presentation profile, which this document references but does not replace.

Whenever a rule in the Canon Rules document or a fact in the Character Identity Map changes, this document must be checked for consistency.
