# Player Identity

This document defines how the player's own character is created, customised, and presented in Resonance. It applies the philosophy of the preceding document to the one character the player brings into the world: an original professional who works alongside the established crew and never replaces them. It is written for artists, animators, user-interface designers, audio designers, writers, and gameplay programmers so that every discipline builds the player's identity from the same understanding.

This document does not create canon. It applies the canon. The Canon Rules document is the authority on every rule cited here, the Character Identity Map is the locked source for the recurring cast, and the Project Constitution has authority over both. The customisation options described here are presentation standards and design philosophy for how player identity should work; they are not a settled user-interface specification, and where any wording here appears to differ from the canon, the canon governs.

The central promise of this document is simple and absolute. The player creates a complete, original person of their own, and nothing the player chooses ever alters, replaces, or contradicts a canonical character or a canon rule.

## The Player Character as an Original Professional

The player character in Resonance is not a recurring character and is not a blank stand-in for the player. The player character is an original professional aboard the ship, a colleague, a neighbour, and a member of a department, whose story unfolds alongside the established crew.

### The Player Never Replaces a Canonical Character

The canon states that the player never replaces a canonical character, that the player creates an original professional assigned to the chosen department whose story unfolds alongside the established crew, that the player may customise this original professional freely, and that customisation never alters established canon. The perspective map confirms that the player is a member of the chosen profession, not that profession's named department lead, and that the named leads are the player's colleagues, superiors, and neighbours rather than the player's own character. Every presentation choice in this document proceeds from this rule.

### The Player as a Complete Person in Their Own Right

The player character is designed to be a complete person, consistent with the principle that every character in Resonance is whole. The player character has a profession, a way of communicating, a way of moving, a name they answer to, and a place among the crew. The customisation system exists so the player can make this person genuinely theirs, and the presentation of the player character is authored with the same care given to the recurring cast, so that the player's own character feels as real as the people they meet.

### The Player Is Never the Smartest Person in the Room

The canon states that the player is never the smartest person in the room, that every crew member possesses expertise unavailable to the player, and that the player's greatest strength is bringing expertise together. The player character is therefore presented as a capable professional who still has much to learn from the recurring crew. Customisation lets the player shape who their character is, but it never makes the player character the authority the recurring cast defers to. The player character learns from people, because the canon holds that players learn from people and not from menus.

### The Player Character and the Silent Companion

The perspective map states that Faience is absent from every profession's ordinary week, that no profession hears Faience during the seven days or the last morning, and that the player ends Attack Day in a separate, damaged pod where Faience first stirs. Faience is a Shawabti artificial intelligence, silent through Operation One, first stirring wordlessly in a damaged pod on Attack Day, and stands outside human concepts of race, ethnicity, and gender. The player character's presentation across the ordinary week is therefore built without a companion at their side, and the arrival of Faience is presented as a turning point rather than a starting condition. Customisation never assigns Faience a race, ethnicity, or gender, because none exist to assign.

## The Character Creation Flow

Character creation is the player's first act in the world, and it is designed to be respectful, accessible, and grounded in the game's philosophy. The flow described here is a standard for how creation should feel and what it should cover, not a fixed sequence of screens.

### Accessibility First in Character Creation

Because accessibility is foundational, character creation is fully accessible before it is anything else. The Accessibility Bible owns the detail of the initial accessibility interview, in which Faience learns how the player experiences the world by asking how the player wants to interact rather than asking about any diagnosis. Character creation is configured by experience and never by diagnosis, every choice is legible through speech, captions, and braille, and no step depends on a single sense. The player may change any choice at any time, and no choice locks the player into a fixed profile.

### Creation Configured by Experience, Not Diagnosis

Consistent with the canon that accessibility is configured based on experience rather than diagnosis, character creation never asks the player to declare a condition. It asks how the player wants to communicate, how the player wants to hear the world, and how the player wants to move, and it builds the player's experience from those answers. This keeps creation respectful and future-proof, and it separates the player's real-world access needs, which are theirs, from the fictional attributes of the character, which are a creative choice.

### Separating Access Needs From Character Fiction

Character creation distinguishes two different things that both live in the same flow. One is the player's own accessibility configuration, which shapes how the game communicates with the player and is owned by the Accessibility Bible. The other is the fictional identity of the player character, including their name, pronouns, callsign, voice, background, and profession. Presentation keeps these clearly separate so that a player configures the game for themselves and, independently, creates a character who may resemble them or differ from them entirely.

### A Creation Flow That Teaches Through People

Consistent with the canon that people introduce mechanics and players learn from people rather than menus, character creation is framed as an entry into a community rather than a form to complete. The player is joining a department and a crew, and creation should feel like preparing to take a post among people. The recurring crew are the colleagues, superiors, and neighbours the player will meet, and creation should orient the player toward that community without ever letting the player edit it.

### Reviewing and Confirming the Player Character

Character creation ends with a review that presents every choice the player has made in an accessible summary, legible through speech, captions, and braille, so the player can confirm their character before entering the world. Because customisation never locks the player in, the review makes clear which choices can be revisited later. The confirmation is presented as taking up a post rather than as finalising a record, keeping the tone consistent with joining a living community.

## Every Customisation Option

The customisation system lets the player shape their original professional freely, within the one boundary that nothing may alter canon or the recurring cast. This section describes each customisation dimension as a presentation standard. The specific controls are for the user-interface and gameplay disciplines to realise; what follows is what those controls must let the player express and how each dimension should be presented.

### Overview of the Customisation Dimensions

The player customises their character across several dimensions, each of which is treated as a real part of who the character is rather than as a cosmetic slider.

- Pronouns.
- Preferred name.
- Callsign.
- Voice profile.
- Accessibility profile.
- Background.
- Profession.
- Appearance and presentation details consistent with the game's sound-first design.

Each dimension is described below. Every dimension is optional to adjust and carries a respectful default, and no dimension may be used to alter a recurring character or a canon rule.

### Appearance and Presentation Details for the Player Character

Because Resonance is sound-first, the player character's appearance is presented so that it is legible by ear, touch, and text as well as by sight. Visual appearance customisation is offered and is presented with respect and breadth, but it is authored alongside the audible and tactile presentation of the character so that a player who does not use sight has an equally complete character. Appearance choices are the player's own and never mimic a recurring character in a way that would create confusion with the established cast. Presentation ensures the player character is distinct from the recurring crew, so that the recognition skills the player builds for the crew are never undermined by a look-alike or sound-alike player character.

## Pronouns for the Player Character

Pronouns are a real part of the player character's identity, and the game treats them the way it treats the recurring cast's pronouns: as an ordinary part of daily life, respected by everyone.

### Choosing and Changing Pronouns

The player chooses their character's pronouns during creation and may change them at any time, consistent with the canon that pronouns are part of a person's present presentation and always reflect how a character currently identifies, and that a character whose gender identity changes over time naturally changes the pronouns they use. The option set is offered with breadth and without ranking any option as the default in a way that marks others as unusual, consistent with the canon that no gender identity is treated as the default.

### Pronouns Honoured as Ordinary

Consistent with the canon that crew, friends, and family respect and use a character's current pronouns as an ordinary part of daily life, the recurring crew use the player character's chosen pronouns naturally in dialogue. The game never treats the player's pronoun choice or a later change as remarkable, and it never subjects the choice to comment, justification, or spectacle. Pronouns are honoured the way everyone's pronouns are honoured aboard the ship.

### Pronouns Learned Without Intrusive Prompts

Consistent with the canon that the player learns a character's current pronouns through natural interaction rather than intrusive interface prompts, the game surfaces the player character's pronouns through natural dialogue and use rather than through persistent labels. The player has set their character's pronouns deliberately in creation, and the world simply uses them, keeping the interface quiet in the same spirit that governs the recurring cast.

## Preferred Name for the Player Character

The preferred name is the name the player character answers to, and it is presented as a real name used naturally by the crew.

### Setting the Preferred Name

The player sets a preferred name for their character during creation and may adjust it later, consistent with the principle that customisation is free and never locks the player in. The preferred name is the name the crew use in ordinary address, and it is authored to be spoken aloud, shown in captions, and rendered in braille so that it is legible through every channel.

### The Preferred Name Used Naturally by the Crew

The recurring crew address the player character by their preferred name as an ordinary part of daily life. The name is presented through natural dialogue rather than through a persistent label, in keeping with the game's quiet interface. The preferred name is the player character's own and never duplicates or impersonates a recurring character's name in a way that would confuse the player's recognition of the established cast.

### Names and the Ordinariness of Identity Updates

The recurring crew treat personal identifiers as ordinary ship operations, as the canon establishes for the Vale System's name tape, badge, and login updates. The player character's name and identifiers are presented in the same ordinary register: they appear on the player character's own name tape, badge, and terminal login as routine ship data, without ceremony, and are only remarked upon when directly relevant to a conversation.

## Callsign for the Player Character

The callsign is the player character's working handle, distinct from their preferred name, and it is presented as a natural part of shipboard and professional communication.

### Choosing a Callsign

The player chooses a callsign for their character, and the callsign is presented as the handle used on channels and in the flow of duty, where a short, clear identifier serves communication. The player may adjust the callsign consistent with the freedom of customisation. The callsign is authored to be legible by ear, in captions, and in braille, because it will be heard and read across department channels.

### The Callsign in Professional Communication

The callsign is presented as belonging to the player character's professional life and is used naturally by the crew in the vocabulary of the chosen profession, whether that is care, investigation, systems, the ship's hidden body, readiness, command, logistics, or civilian life. The callsign never replaces the preferred name in personal address; the two coexist the way a working handle and a personal name coexist for real crew. The callsign is the player character's own and never appropriates a recurring character's identity or authority.

## Voice Profile for the Player Character

Because audio is gameplay and Resonance is sound-first, the player character's voice profile is one of the most important customisation dimensions, treated as a primary attribute of the character rather than a cosmetic setting.

### The Voice Profile as a Core Attribute

The voice profile shapes how the player character sounds, and it is authored with the seriousness the game gives to the recurring cast's audio identities. The Audio Bible owns the detail of how voices are produced; this document establishes that the player is offered meaningful control over their character's voice as part of making the character their own, and that the voice profile is presented as central to who the character is.

### Voice Profile and Communication Method

Consistent with the canon that communication diversity is ordinary and every communication method is equally valid, the voice profile accommodates players whose characters communicate in different ways. A player character may communicate through spoken language, sign, augmentative and alternative communication, text, or another system, and the voice profile is presented so that whichever method the character uses is a full and valid voice rather than a lesser substitute. The player's own accessibility configuration, owned by the Accessibility Bible, is kept separate from the character's fictional communication method, so that either may be chosen independently.

### Voice Profile Kept Distinct From the Recurring Cast

Because the recurring crew are recognised through voice and speech rhythm, the player character's voice profile is presented so that it never impersonates a recurring character. The player character is distinct by design, so that the recognition skills the player builds for the crew remain reliable. The voice profile is the player character's own, and it is authored to sit naturally among the crew's voices without being mistaken for any of them.

## Accessibility Profile for the Player Character

The accessibility profile is where the player configures how the game communicates with them, and it is the clearest expression of the principle that accessibility is standard equipment rather than special equipment.

### Accessibility Configured as Standard Equipment

Consistent with the canon that no accessibility feature is special equipment, that accessibility technology is standard equipment aboard human vessels, and that crew members customise interfaces rather than requesting permission to use them, the accessibility profile is presented as ordinary configuration. The player adjusts how the game communicates, hears the world, and moves, exactly as any crew member customises their interface. Nothing in the accessibility profile is framed as a request, an exception, or a special mode.

### The Accessibility Profile Owned by the Accessibility Bible

The Accessibility Bible owns the full detail of the accessibility profile, including the initial accessibility interview, the communication options of speech, captions, and braille and their combinations, the listening options, the movement options, DeafBlind play, accessible input, the tactile language, reality-integrated navigation, and the independent volume channels. This document does not restate those details; it establishes that the accessibility profile is part of character creation, is configured by experience rather than diagnosis, and is fully changeable at any time so that no player is ever locked into a profile.

### Independence and Combination of Accessibility Options

Consistent with the canon that accessibility options are independent and may be combined freely, and that one option never disables another, the accessibility profile lets the player combine any options they wish. The profile is presented so that the player understands they may mix communication, listening, and movement methods without restriction, and the presentation never implies that a combination is unusual.

### The Accessibility Profile and the Character's Fictional Access

The player's accessibility profile configures the real player's experience and is separate from any fictional accessibility attribute the player gives their character. A player may configure the game one way for themselves and create a character whose fictional way of moving, communicating, or perceiving is the same or different. Presentation keeps this distinction clear so that access is never conflated with characterisation, and so that a character's fictional accessibility, like the recurring cast's, is presented as ordinary competence rather than as struggle.

## Background for the Player Character

The background gives the player character a sense of history and belonging within the world, and it is offered so the player can shape who their character has been without inventing canon.

### Background Presented Within Established Bounds

The player selects a background for their character from options that fit the established world, and the background is presented as the player character's own personal history rather than as a new addition to the world's canon. Backgrounds are authored to be consistent with the ship, its departments, and its community as the other documents describe them, and they never introduce a new place, faction, event, or person into canon. A background gives the player character texture and grounding while leaving the world's settled history untouched.

### Background Shaping Perspective, Not History

Consistent with the canon that the player's actions create history while the player's observations create understanding, and that Operation One happens once and no profession changes history, a chosen background shapes how the player character perceives and relates to the world without changing what happened aboard the ship. Background influences the player character's vantage, priorities, and relationships in the ways this document later describes for dialogue, and it never alters the fixed events, the recurring cast, or the timeline.

### Background and Belonging Over Time

Consistent with the canon that portraits represent belonging rather than recruitment, and that a crew member becomes home only after they settle and change the environment around them, the player character's background is a starting point rather than a finished belonging. The player character's place in the community grows through what they do and how they are observed, and the background gives that growth a beginning rather than a conclusion.

## Profession Selection for the Player Character

Profession selection is the most structurally important customisation choice, because the chosen profession determines the perspective through which the player lives the shared history of the game.

### The Eight Professions the Player May Choose

The player chooses one of the eight professions, each of which is a full perspective on the same world. The eight professions are the following.

- Medic.
- Scientist.
- Engineer.
- Maintenance.
- Security.
- Commissioned Officer.
- Non-Commissioned Officer.
- Civilian.

Each profession is presented as a complete way of living the game, and the player character is a member of the chosen profession rather than that profession's named lead.

### Profession as Perspective on Shared History

Consistent with the canon that Operation One happens once and is seen through many perspectives, that every profession experiences the same historical events, that no profession changes history, and that only perspective changes, profession selection sets the player's vantage rather than a separate story. The perspective map is canon over how each profession experiences the fixed events, and profession selection is presented as choosing which thread of the shared morning the player lives, not as choosing which events occur. Replaying different professions gives the player a fuller understanding of the same history.

### Profession and the Named Department Leads

Consistent with the perspective map, the player character is a member of the chosen profession and not the profession's named lead, and the named leads are the player's colleagues, superiors, and neighbours. The recurring leads associated with the professions, including Doctor Samira Kade in Medical, Doctor Soren Qadir in Research, Mara Chen in Engineering, Rowan Ives in Maintenance, Talia Rusk in Security, the Vale System in command, and Chief Petty Officer Jonah Briggs in logistics and operations, are people the player works beside and learns from, never characters the player replaces. Profession selection places the player among these people; it never turns the player into them.

### Profession Answering the Three Questions Differently

Consistent with the canon that every profession answers three questions differently, profession selection changes what the player character notices, what responsibilities the player character carries, and what knowledge is available only from the player character's perspective. Presentation gives each profession its own working vocabulary, its own starting world, and its own way of reading the ship, as the perspective map records, so that the player's chosen profession genuinely shapes the experience while the shared history remains fixed.

### Profession Selection Is Free and Never Alters the Cast

Profession selection, like every customisation choice, is free to the player and never alters canon. Choosing a profession does not change the recurring lead of that department, does not change the fixed events, and does not displace any recurring character from their role. The player joins a profession; the profession's canonical people remain exactly who they are.

## How Customisation Affects Dialogue

Customisation is meaningful, and one of the clearest places its meaning appears is in dialogue. This section defines how the player's choices shape conversation, always within the boundary that canon and the recurring cast are never altered.

### Dialogue Reflecting the Player's Pronouns and Name

The recurring crew address the player character by their preferred name and use their chosen pronouns naturally in dialogue, as an ordinary part of daily life. Dialogue is authored so that the player's name, pronouns, and callsign flow through conversation without comment or spectacle, keeping the world's treatment of the player character consistent with its respectful treatment of the recurring cast.

### Dialogue Reflecting Profession and Background

Consistent with the canon that every profession notices different things and carries different responsibilities, dialogue reflects the player character's profession and background. The vocabulary the crew use with the player, the tasks they bring to the player, and the knowledge they share are shaped by the player's chosen profession and coloured by the player's background, so that a medic and a maintenance worker are spoken to as the professionals they are. This shaping changes the player's vantage on conversations without changing what the conversations are ultimately about.

### Dialogue Reflecting Communication Method

Consistent with the canon that every communication method is equally valid and that the world adapts to people, dialogue accommodates the player character's communication method. Whether the player character communicates through speech, sign, an augmentative and alternative system, text, or another method, the crew engage with that method as a full and valid voice, and the conversation is complete through more than one channel so that no player is shut out of understanding it.

### Dialogue Recording Philosophy Rather Than Morality

Consistent with the canon that the game never scores morality and instead records how the player approached discovery, dialogue quietly reflects the philosophy the player expresses through their choices, such as curiosity, preparation, preservation, collaboration, leadership, scientific investigation, engineering, compassion, historical preservation, or accessibility. These are never displayed as scores; they subtly influence crew dialogue and related presentation over time. The player is never told they played correctly, and dialogue never praises or condemns the player character, because the world remembers how the player thinks rather than judging whether the player is good or bad.

### Dialogue That Keeps the Player Learning From People

Consistent with the canon that the player is never the smartest person in the room and that everyone teaches while everyone keeps learning, dialogue keeps the recurring crew as sources of expertise the player character does not have. Customisation shapes how the player character enters a conversation, but it never makes the crew defer to the player character as the final authority. The player's strength in dialogue is bringing expertise together, and dialogue is authored to reward that rather than to flatter the player character.

## What Customisation Must Never Affect

The freedom of customisation exists inside a firm boundary. This section states, plainly and completely, what customisation must never touch, so that every discipline can check any feature against a clear line.

### Customisation Never Alters Canon

The canon states that customisation never alters established canon, and that the existence, history, identity, relationships, and appearance of every recurring character remain unchanged by anything the player chooses. No customisation option may edit a canon rule, change the fixed events of Operation One, or add a place, faction, event, or person to canon. The player's choices shape the player's own character and the player's vantage, and nothing more.

### Customisation Never Alters the Recurring Cast

No customisation option may change any recurring character. The professions, ethnicities, genders, pronouns, disabilities, personalities, relationships, and appearances recorded in the Character Identity Map and elaborated in the profiles are fixed regardless of the player's choices. The recurring leads remain the leads of their departments, the recurring crew remain who they are, and the player never replaces, becomes, or reshapes any of them.

### Customisation Never Alters the Vale System

The Vale System requires explicit protection because its structure is easy to mishandle. No customisation option may present the Vale System as four separate people or as a single character named Captain Mara Vale, may assign or change the Vale System's shared body, ethnicity, or medical reality, or may change the genders, pronouns, or fronting of Elian, Naomi, Gideon, and Iris. The Vale System is one shared body inhabited by four members who front situationally and are recognised through behaviour, voice, posture, and priorities rather than appearance, and no player choice may alter that. Mara Chen is the blind chief engineer and is not a member of the Vale System, and no customisation may connect the player character or any choice to a false claim about that relationship.

### Customisation Never Alters Faience

No customisation option may assign Faience a race, ethnicity, or gender, because Faience is a Shawabti artificial intelligence that stands outside those human concepts and none are assigned. No customisation may make Faience present or vocal during Operation One, because Faience is silent through Operation One and first stirs, wordlessly, in a damaged pod on Attack Day. The player character's creation and the arrival of Faience are separate, and customisation never changes the nature or the silence of Faience.

### Customisation Never Overrides Accessibility Foundations

No customisation option may lock a player into a fixed accessibility profile, disable another accessibility option, or make any accessibility feature contingent on permission, because accessibility is standard equipment, options are independent and combinable, and the player may change any choice at any time. No customisation may cause important information to depend on a single sense, because important information must never depend on sound alone or on any one channel.

### Customisation Never Changes History or Perspective Rules

No customisation option may change the fixed events, order, or timing of Operation One, may let a profession change history, or may let the player character witness what their chosen perspective does not witness, because Operation One happens once, no profession changes history, only perspective changes, and no single profession witnesses the whole of Attack Day. Customisation sets the player's vantage within the shared history and never rewrites the history itself.

## How This Document Governs Player Identity Work

This document is the standard for building the player's character, and every discipline applies it. The user-interface and gameplay disciplines build a creation flow that is accessible first, configured by experience, and free to revisit. The art, animation, and audio disciplines build a player character who is complete and distinct from the recurring cast across every channel. The writing discipline builds dialogue that reflects the player's choices while keeping canon and the recurring cast untouched. Above all, every discipline holds the one boundary that gives the player their freedom safely: the player creates an original professional of their own, and nothing the player chooses ever alters, replaces, or contradicts a canonical character or a canon rule.
