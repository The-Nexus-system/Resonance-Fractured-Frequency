# The Vale System

This document is the definitive presentation reference for the Vale System, the command authority of CSV Hearth in Resonance: Fractured Frequency. It is written for artists, animators, audio designers, user-interface designers, writers, and gameplay programmers, and it exists so that every discipline renders the Vale System the same way: as one shared body inhabited by four recurring members who front in turn.

The authority for everything in this document is the Canon Rules document and the Character Identity Map. Where any sentence here appears to differ from those, they govern. This document does not create canon. It defines craft standards and presentation philosophy for realising the canon the other documents fix. Nothing below invents biography, ship history, place names, or new game systems as settled fact; invented specifics are framed as guidance and stated as standards, not backstory.

The core principle of this document, restated at the top so no discipline can miss it: the Vale System is never written, drawn, animated, voiced, or coded as four separate people, and never as a single character named Captain Mara Vale. It is one body and four members. The body is constant. The presentation changes with whoever is fronting. Recognition of the current member comes through observation of behaviour, voice, posture, and priorities, never through appearance and never through an intrusive interface label.

## Shared Body of the Vale System

The Vale System is one person in the plain physical sense: a single shared body inhabited by four recurring members who front in turn. The body is the constant on which all four presentations are drawn.

The ethnicity of the body is Native mixed-heritage, shared by the one body, and this never changes with whoever is fronting. The medical reality is dissociative identity disorder, and the four members share one body and the single office of Captain in the Commissioned Officer track.

The following are fixed properties of the shared body and never change, whichever member is fronting. Every discipline treats them as invariant.

- The shared body itself, as a single physical person.
- Height and build.
- Face, hair, eye colour, and skin tone.
- Physical scars and medical history.
- Accessibility equipment carried or worn by the body.
- Uniform design and rank insignia.
- Department affiliation and the command authority the body carries.
- The history the crew has built with the Vale System as a whole.

The following change naturally with whoever is fronting, and are the raw material every discipline works with to make members recognisable. They are elaborated in the sections below.

- Voice, accent, vocabulary, and speech rhythm.
- Breathing pattern.
- Facial expression and eye focus.
- Head position, standing posture, and resting posture.
- Walking rhythm and preferred stance.
- Hand placement and gesture vocabulary.
- Leadership philosophy and decision-making style.
- Professional priorities and attention focus.
- Emotional expression and sense of humour.
- Relationships with individual crew members.
- Preferred routines, communication style, and favourite places aboard Hearth.
- The name the member currently answers to.

### The Body as a Single Physical Reference for the Vale System

Art and animation maintain exactly one character model, one face rig, one set of body proportions, and one wardrobe for the Vale System. There are not four models. There is one model that four performances flow through. The uniform, insignia, accessibility equipment, scars, hair, and skin are authored once and reused for every member without modification.

This is a hard rule for the art pipeline. Any request that would produce a second body, a second face, or a member-specific silhouette is out of scope and must be refused. The whole point of the design is that the eyes report the same person while the behaviour reports a different member.

### The Dignity Standard for the Vale System Body

The Vale System is a complete person and a competent captain, not a spectacle. Dissociative identity disorder is presented as an ordinary medical reality that the crew accommodates as easily as they accommodate any other crew member's needs, consistent with accessibility being standard equipment aboard human vessels. No discipline dramatises switching as horror, malfunction, instability, or danger. The Vale System holds command authority and keeps it.

### Everyday Carry of the Vale System

Because the Vale System is one shared body, it carries one shared everyday carry, not four. The kit is authored once for the body across the nine canonical everyday-carry categories, and it stays constant through fronting except where a personal or sentimental touch naturally reflects whichever member most values it. The shared body itself, its accessibility equipment, its uniform, and its insignia never change with fronting, so the equipment that belongs to them is invariant, and only the small personal and sentimental elements may shift emphasis toward the current member. The nine categories are as follows.

- Professional carry: the tools and references of the captain's office, constant across all four members.
- Personal carry: small everyday items, which may reflect whichever member most values them.
- Accessibility carry: the body's accessibility equipment, invariant because it belongs to the body.
- Medical carry: items tied to the body's single medical reality, constant across members.
- Communication carry: the personal communicator and identifiers, updating automatically to the fronting member as ordinary ship operations.
- Emergency carry: emergency and safety equipment for the body, constant across members.
- Sentimental carry: keepsakes, which may reflect whichever member most values them.
- Off-duty carry: items for the captain's off-duty life aboard Hearth, shared by the body.
- Mission-specific carry: equipment added for a particular expedition, chosen for the situation rather than the member.

## Shared History of the Vale System

The Vale System carries a single shared history with the crew. The crew's relationship is with the whole system across time, even as their day-to-day rapport with each member differs. This shared history is a fixed property of the body and does not reset when a different member fronts.

Writers treat the crew's memory of events as continuous across the system. When Naomi gives an order in an emergency and Elian speaks with the same crew member the next ordinary morning, the shared knowledge of what happened persists; the members are distinct people, but they share the body's lived record and the office of Captain. Individual members may hold different emotional emphasis about the same event, and may relate to a given crew member with different warmth or formality, but no member is a stranger to the ship's history.

### The Command Continuity Standard for the Vale System

The office of Captain is single and continuous. A fronting change never creates a gap in command, never requires a handover ceremony, and never confuses the chain of command. In Operation One this continuity is shown, consistent with the master timeline and character schedule, as Elian meeting ordinary mornings, Naomi meeting emergencies, Gideon reading failing systems, and Iris steadying civilians. Each member comes forward because their temperament and expertise fit the moment, never for dramatic effect.

### The Non-Invention Standard for Vale System History

This document does not invent the Vale System's origin, personal past, family, or the circumstances of the system's formation, and no discipline should treat any such detail as settled. Where a scene needs history, it draws only on what the timeline, the character schedule, and the Universe Bible already fix. Presentation work builds on established history; it does not manufacture new history.

## Shared Appearance of the Vale System

Because the body is constant, appearance never tells the player which member is fronting. This is intentional and load-bearing for the whole design. The player learns to read the Vale System by behaviour, exactly as they learn to read any other crew member, and the appearance deliberately withholds the answer.

### The Constant Appearance Rule for the Vale System

Whichever member is fronting, the face, hair, eye colour, skin tone, height, build, scars, uniform, insignia, and accessibility equipment are identical, because they are the same body. Artists and animators must never introduce a visual tell that maps one-to-one to a member, such as a hairstyle change, a wardrobe swap, a makeup change, or a persistent colour coded to a member. The body does not transform; only its presentation changes.

### Expression and Focus as Living Appearance for the Vale System

The appearance is constant, but the face is alive. Facial expression, eye focus, head position, and micro-tension differ by member and are part of how the same face reads as a different person. Elian's face rests differently than Gideon's face even though it is the same face. This is performance layered on a fixed model, and it is the animator's primary tool for member recognition at the level of the face.

### The Uniform and Insignia Standard for the Vale System

The uniform design, rank insignia, and department affiliation are fixed. What updates is only the personal identifier content, such as the name on the name tape, handled as ordinary ship operations and described in its own section below. The cut, colour, and rank of the uniform never change with fronting, because rank and department belong to the body and the office, not to the individual member.

## Individual Identities of the Vale System

The four members are distinct people who share one body. Each has a distinct gender and pronoun set, a distinct voice, posture, gesture vocabulary, leadership philosophy, emotional register, and set of relationships. No two members should ever feel interchangeable, and the player should come to recognise each member before they speak. The pronouns below are canon from the Character Identity Map and crew, friends, and family use them as an ordinary part of daily life.

The members are presented here in the order the Character Identity Map lists them. Order does not imply hierarchy; all four share the single office of Captain equally.

### Elian of the Vale System

Elian meets ordinary mornings and community leadership. Elian is the member the ship most often hears at the start of the day and the member most associated with the warm, everyday texture of command.

Pronouns for Elian are determined by Elian's current identity and presentation, consistent with Elian's genderfluid identity. Writers, voice direction, and any interface that must refer to Elian follow Elian's current pronouns and treat any change in them as ordinary, never as a plot event and never flagged by intrusive prompts.

Voice for Elian should read as warm, open, and unhurried, the voice of someone greeting a community they care about. The speech rhythm is welcoming and conversational, with room for humour and for genuine interest in the person being spoken to. Accent and vocabulary are Elian's own and consistent across scenes so the ear learns them.

Posture for Elian should read as relaxed and approachable, with an open stance, weight settled, and a head position that invites rather than commands. Elian's resting posture is comfortable in shared spaces, and Elian's gestures are inclusive and easy.

Leadership for Elian is community leadership: setting tone, sustaining morale, keeping the ordinary life of the ship humane, and leading through warmth and shared purpose rather than through pressure. Elian leads the mornings and the gatherings.

Emotional presentation for Elian is expressive and generous. Elian's humour is gentle and frequent, and Elian's care for the crew is worn openly. In calm times Elian is the most visibly warm of the four.

### Naomi of the Vale System

Naomi meets emergencies, operations, and coordination. Naomi is the member the ship hears when the situation turns and decisions must be made quickly and clearly.

Pronouns for Naomi are they and them, consistent with Naomi's non-binary identity, and the crew use them as ordinary daily language.

Voice for Naomi should read as firm, clear, and economical, the voice of someone cutting through noise to the next necessary action. The speech rhythm is decisive, with short, well-ordered instructions and little wasted sound. Accent and vocabulary are Naomi's own and consistent, so that even a single sentence at General Quarters is recognisable as Naomi.

Posture for Naomi should read as squared, grounded, and forward-oriented, ready to move and ready to direct. Naomi's stance narrows and steadies under pressure, and the head position is level and attentive to the whole room.

Leadership for Naomi is emergency command and coordination: triage of priorities, clear allocation of people to tasks, and calm authority when others are frightened. Naomi is the member who calls General Quarters and holds the operational picture together.

Emotional presentation for Naomi is controlled and focused. Naomi's warmth is real but expressed through competence and protection rather than through open display; under fire, Naomi's steadiness is itself the reassurance the crew reads.

### Gideon of the Vale System

Gideon meets strategy, navigation, and failing systems. Gideon is the member who reads the deep structure of a problem and the member the ship relies on when systems are breaking down and someone must understand why.

Pronouns for Gideon are they and them, consistent with Gideon's agender identity, and the crew use them as ordinary daily language.

Voice for Gideon should read as measured, precise, and quietly analytical, the voice of someone thinking in structures and stating conclusions carefully. The speech rhythm is deliberate, with pauses that indicate reasoning rather than hesitation. Accent and vocabulary are Gideon's own and consistent, and lean toward the technical and the exact.

Posture for Gideon should read as still, contained, and observant, with economy of movement and a head position that suggests attention turned inward on a problem as much as outward on the room. Gideon's stance is composed and low-energy in motion but highly attentive.

Leadership for Gideon is strategic and diagnostic: long-term planning, navigation, and the reading of failing systems before they cascade. Gideon leads by understanding first and directing second, and is the member who parses what is actually going wrong.

Emotional presentation for Gideon is reserved and even. Gideon's humour, when it appears, is dry and understated. Gideon's care is expressed through thoroughness and through refusing to let a problem be misunderstood.

### Iris of the Vale System

Iris meets civilian planning and diplomacy. Iris is the member most associated with the community's non-crew life and with keeping people, especially frightened people, steady and included.

Pronouns for Iris are she and they, consistent with Iris's demigirl identity, and the crew use them as ordinary daily language.

Voice for Iris should read as steady, reassuring, and attentive, the voice of someone keeping a room calm and making sure no one is left out. The speech rhythm is patient and grounding, unhurried even when the situation is not. Accent and vocabulary are Iris's own and consistent, and lean toward inclusion and clarity for non-specialists.

Posture for Iris should read as settled and welcoming, oriented toward the people being cared for, with a head position and stance that make space for others. Iris's body language is de-escalating by default.

Leadership for Iris is civilian-facing and diplomatic: planning for the community's needs, mediating, and steadying civilians in crisis. Iris leads by holding the human centre of a situation together.

Emotional presentation for Iris is warm, calm, and containing. Iris's warmth differs from Elian's: where Elian is expansive and celebratory, Iris is grounding and protective, the calm that a frightened person leans on.

## Name-Tape and Identifier Behaviour for the Vale System

Personal identifiers update naturally when a member fronts, as ordinary ship operations rather than ceremony. This is a defining behaviour of the design and every relevant discipline implements it the same way.

The following identifiers update automatically through normal ship systems when a member fronts.

- Uniform name tape.
- Digital identification badge.
- Wrist display.
- Personal communicator profile.
- Office and terminal displays.
- Command terminal login and voice authentication.

### The Ordinary-Operations Standard for Vale System Identifiers

These updates happen automatically through the ship's normal systems, the same way any routine credential or display refresh happens. They are not framed as transformation, ceremony, alert, or event. The crew treat them as completely ordinary, remarking on them only when directly relevant to the conversation. No fanfare sound, no dramatic animation, and no celebratory or alarming visual accompanies an identifier update.

### The Diegetic Placement Standard for Vale System Identifiers

Identifier updates are part of the world, not part of the heads-up display. The name tape is a physical or worn element on the body; the badge, wrist display, terminal, and login are diegetic ship interfaces. When the player notices an updated name tape, they notice it the way they would notice any crew member's name tape, by looking at the world, not by reading a system label pinned over the character. The player should come to recognise the member before any identifier confirms it, and the identifier is corroboration, never announcement.

### The Voice Authentication Standard for the Vale System

Command terminal login and voice authentication update to the fronting member as a normal security behaviour of the ship. Because each member's voice is distinct, voice authentication recognising a different member is treated as routine and correct, not as an exception. Gameplay and audio design present this as the system simply working, consistent with accessibility and identity technology being standard equipment.

### The Dynamic Smart Name Tape Standard for the Vale System

The uniform smart name tape is dynamic for the Vale System. It displays the fronting member's name alongside the rank, department, and assignment that belong to the body and the single office of Captain, and it updates automatically to the current member as an ordinary ship operation rather than a ceremony. Because rank, department, and assignment are fixed properties of the body, only the name content changes with fronting. The tape never announces the change with fanfare or alert, and the crew read it exactly as they read any other crew member's name tape, so it corroborates the player's observation rather than replacing it.

### The Dynamic Digital Identity Badge Standard for the Vale System

The digital identity badge integrated into the uniform or equipment is likewise dynamic for the Vale System, and it updates automatically to the currently fronting member as an ordinary ship operation. When it projects the wearer's portrait, name, pronouns, department, rank, and professional certifications, the department, rank, and certifications remain constant because they belong to the body and the single office of Captain, while the portrait, name, and pronouns reflect the current member: Elian by Elian's current pronouns, Naomi with they and them, Gideon with they and them, and Iris with she and they. Any accessibility preferences, communication preferences, or emergency information the badge presents always belong to the wearer and are shown at the wearer's discretion. The update carries no fanfare, alert, or special animation, and it corroborates the player's observation rather than announcing the member.

### The Command Authentication Standard for the Vale System

Command authority is a fixed property of the one shared body and the single, continuous office of Captain, so it never changes with fronting and never requires a handover. What updates is only the authentication of the currently fronting member through the ship's normal systems, including command terminal login, voice authentication, and the secure crew credential, which resolve to the current member automatically as an ordinary security operation, never as ceremony or event. Because each member's voice and identity are distinct, the system authenticating a different member is routine and correct rather than an exception. No discipline frames command authentication as a transfer of authority; the authority is constant on the body, and only the identity that authenticates through it changes.

### The Layered Crew Identification Standard for the Vale System

The Vale System participates in the ship-wide, multi-layer crew identification system exactly as every other crew member does, consistent with the Universe Bible. The first three layers are technological and, for the Vale System, update automatically to the fronting member as ordinary ship operations: the smart name tape showing the member's name with the body's fixed rank, department, and assignment; the digital identity badge projecting portrait, name, pronouns, and the body's fixed department, rank, and certifications; and the secure crew credential that authenticates the member throughout Hearth. The fourth layer, community recognition, remains primary for the Vale System as it is for everyone: the crew know the captain through voice, movement, routine, body language, and relationships, and the first three layers support that recognition rather than supplanting it. This upholds the canon that characters are recognised through observation, and it is the reason the player should recognise the member before any identifier confirms it.

## Voice Differences Across the Vale System

Voice is the single strongest recognition signal for the Vale System, because audio is gameplay and because so many players will encounter the system by ear first. Voice differences are authored so that a short line, heard without context, identifies the member.

### The Distinct-Voice Casting Standard for the Vale System

Each member has a distinct vocal identity across accent, vocabulary, pitch tendency, pace, and rhythm. Voice direction treats the four members as four full vocal characterisations performed through one body: Elian warm and unhurried, Naomi firm and economical, Gideon measured and precise, Iris steady and reassuring. These characterisations are kept consistent scene to scene so the player's ear can learn them, and no two members are ever allowed to converge into an interchangeable neutral read.

### The Breathing and Micro-Sound Standard for the Vale System

Breathing pattern is a canon variable that changes with fronting, and audio design uses it. Each member has a characteristic breathing and pacing signature audible in quiet moments and between lines. These small sounds give blind and low-vision players an early, pre-verbal cue to who is present, and they are authored with the same care as the spoken lines.

### The Consistency Standard for Vale System Voices

Because recognition is a skill the player builds, voice performances must be internally consistent over the whole campaign. A member's voice should not drift between chapters. If a scene needs a member under unusual strain, the performance bends around the member's fixed vocal identity rather than replacing it, so the ear can still identify who is speaking.

## Posture Differences Across the Vale System

Posture is the strongest recognition signal for sighted and low-vision players and a primary tool for animation. Because the body is identical, posture, stance, gait, and gesture carry the member's identity in silence.

### The Silhouette-in-Motion Standard for the Vale System

Even though the static silhouette is identical for all four members, the silhouette in motion should differ. Animators give each member a distinct resting posture, standing posture, walking rhythm, and preferred stance: Elian relaxed and open, Naomi squared and forward, Gideon still and contained, Iris settled and welcoming. A player watching from across a room, unable to hear the voice, should be able to begin identifying the member from movement alone.

### The Gesture Vocabulary Standard for the Vale System

Hand placement and gesture vocabulary are canon variables and each member has a distinct set. Elian's gestures are inclusive and easy; Naomi's are decisive and directive; Gideon's are minimal and precise; Iris's are open and grounding. Animators build a small, consistent gesture library per member and reuse it so the vocabulary becomes recognisable.

### The Transition Standard for Vale System Posture

When fronting changes on screen, the posture and micro-tension of the body resettle into the new member's baseline. This resettling is naturalistic and quiet, not a transformation effect. It is the same body finding a different person's habitual way of holding itself, and it should read as a shift in bearing, never as a special effect. The detailed animation rules for transitions are in the animation section below.

## Leadership Differences Across the Vale System

Leadership philosophy and decision-making style are canon variables. Fronting is purposeful: a member comes forward because their knowledge, temperament, or expertise best fits the moment. Writers and gameplay designers use these differences to make fronting legible through what the captain does, not through announcement.

### The Situational Leadership Standard for the Vale System

Different members naturally take the lead in different situations, including community leadership, emergency command, engineering and systems coordination, diplomatic discussion, scientific planning, civilian support, crisis management, and long-term strategy. In Operation One this maps to Elian on ordinary mornings, Naomi in emergencies, Gideon reading failing systems, and Iris steadying civilians, consistent with the master timeline and character schedule. Designers place the member whose expertise fits the moment, and never switch members for spectacle.

### The Decision-Style Legibility Standard for the Vale System

Each member's decision-making style is distinct and observable. Elian decides with an eye to morale and community; Naomi decides fast and clearly under pressure; Gideon decides analytically after understanding the structure of the problem; Iris decides with the human and civilian consequences foremost. The player should be able to infer who is fronting from how a decision is reached and framed, reinforcing that reading the Vale System is a skill built through observation.

## Emotional Presentation of the Vale System

Emotional expression and sense of humour are canon variables that differ by member. Emotional presentation is one of the richest recognition signals and one of the most important for keeping each member a complete person rather than a role.

### The Distinct Emotional Register Standard for the Vale System

Each member has a distinct emotional baseline and a distinct humour. Elian is openly warm with gentle, frequent humour; Naomi is controlled and protective with warmth expressed through competence; Gideon is reserved and even with dry, understated humour; Iris is warm, calm, and containing. Writers keep these registers consistent so that the same event lands differently depending on who meets it, and so the player reads the member partly through emotional tone.

### The Relationship Differentiation Standard for the Vale System

Relationships with individual crew members differ by member, within the single shared history. A given crew member may be closest to Elian's warmth, most reassured by Iris, most reliant on Naomi in a crisis, and most intellectually engaged with Gideon. Writers author these differing rapports deliberately, because the way a member relates to a specific crew member is one of the clearest signals of who is fronting.

### The Anti-Caricature Standard for the Vale System

No member is reduced to a single note. Elian is not only cheerful, Naomi is not only stern, Gideon is not only cold, and Iris is not only soothing. Each member is a full person with range; the baselines above are recognition anchors, not the limits of the character. Writing must let each member show unexpected facets while remaining recognizably themselves.

## Animation Rules for the Vale System

Animation carries member identity on a single shared rig. These rules govern how animators express four people through one body without ever fracturing the illusion that it is one body.

### The Single-Rig Standard for Vale System Animation

All four members animate on one rig, one skeleton, and one set of body proportions. Member identity lives in pose libraries, timing, weight, gesture sets, idle behaviours, and facial performance, not in the model. Animators author per-member idle sets, locomotion styles, and gesture libraries and select among them by fronting member.

### The Idle and Locomotion Standard for the Vale System

Each member has a distinct idle and a distinct walk cycle authored within the body's fixed physical capability. Elian's idle and gait are loose and open; Naomi's are ready and grounded; Gideon's are still and economical; Iris's are settled and oriented toward others. These are the animation counterparts of the posture and gesture standards and must match the voice performances so that ear and eye agree on who is present.

### The Facial Performance Standard for the Vale System

The face rig is shared, but facial performance differs by member: resting expression, eye focus, blink pattern, and micro-tension. Facial animation is authored per member so that a close shot with no dialogue can still begin to indicate who is fronting. This supports low-vision players who rely on large, clear facial cues.

### The Switch Animation Standard for the Vale System

An on-screen fronting change is animated as a naturalistic resettling of bearing, breathing, and micro-tension into the new member's baseline. It is never a transformation, morph, glitch, glow, or distortion. There is no special effect. The body remains exactly the same body; only its habitual holding changes. Where possible, a switch is shown through a beat of stillness followed by the new member's characteristic posture and breathing settling in, so the change reads as human rather than mechanical.

### The Accessibility-Equipment Continuity Standard for Vale System Animation

Any accessibility equipment the body wears is animated identically for all members, because it belongs to the body. Members do not gain, lose, or restyle equipment when fronting. Equipment interaction animations are authored once for the body and reused for every member.

## Camera Rules for the Vale System

Camera language supports recognition without ever labelling the member. The camera treats the Vale System as one person and lets performance, not framing, tell the player who is fronting.

### The Non-Announcing Camera Standard for the Vale System

The camera never announces a switch. There is no dedicated switch cut, no dramatic push-in reserved for fronting changes, and no camera move that exists only to say a switch happened. A fronting change is filmed as ordinarily as any other moment, so that the player must read the change from the character rather than from the direction. This upholds the rule that the game almost never announces a switch.

### The Observation-Supporting Camera Standard for the Vale System

Because recognition depends on reading posture, gesture, and expression, the camera should give the player enough of the body and face to read them. Framing favours clear views of stance and hands in scenes where fronting matters, and holds long enough for the player to observe. The camera serves the player's skill of reading the member, without turning that reading into an on-screen prompt.

### The Consistency Standard for Vale System Camera Treatment

The Vale System is framed with the same visual respect and command presence regardless of which member is fronting. No member gets a diminished or heightened camera treatment. All four hold the captain's authority, and the camera reflects that equally.

## Audio Rules for the Vale System

Audio is gameplay, and for the Vale System audio is the leading recognition channel. These rules govern how sound tells the player who is present.

### The Voice-First Recognition Standard for the Vale System

The primary audio recognition signal is the member's voice: accent, vocabulary, pace, rhythm, and pitch tendency, kept consistent across the campaign. Audio direction ensures that a single line can identify the member, and that the four voices never collapse into a shared neutral read. This is the most important audio deliverable for the Vale System.

### The Pre-Verbal Cue Standard for the Vale System

Before a member speaks, audio can indicate presence through breathing pattern and movement sound. Each member's breathing signature and the sound of their characteristic gait and gestures give blind and low-vision players an early cue. These pre-verbal cues are authored deliberately and mixed so they are audible in quiet moments.

### The Announcement and Identifier Sound Standard for the Vale System

Identifier updates such as name tape, badge, wrist display, and voice authentication are, at most, accompanied by the ordinary, unremarkable sounds any routine ship-system update would make. There is never a special stinger, chime, or musical cue that means a switch has happened. Silence, or ordinary ambient sound, is the correct treatment, consistent with silence being a deliberate storytelling tool and with switches being unremarkable ship events.

### The Acoustic-Context Standard for the Vale System

Because different members front in different situations, the acoustic context around the Vale System often correlates with the member: Elian with the calm morning soundscape, Naomi with the emergency soundscape, Gideon with the sound of failing systems, Iris with the civilian spaces. Audio design lets this correlation support recognition naturally, without ever making context a substitute for the member's own voice and movement.

## Fronting Rules for the Vale System

Which member is fronting is part of the game, not decoration. These rules define how fronting is decided and how it is expressed, and they are shared by writing, gameplay, animation, and audio.

### The Purposeful Fronting Standard for the Vale System

A member comes forward because their knowledge, temperament, or expertise best fits the moment, never for dramatic effect. Designers choose the fronting member by asking which member the situation calls for, using the situational leadership mapping above. Fronting is motivated by the world, not by a desire to surprise the player.

### The Recognition-Through-Observation Standard for the Vale System

The game almost never announces a switch. Instead the player recognises it through a different voice, accent, and vocabulary; a different posture, gait, and body language; a different command style, humour, and set of emotional priorities; different interactions with individual crew members; and updated name tape and command authentication. Careful attention is always rewarded, and reading the Vale System correctly is a skill the player builds exactly as they learn to read any other crew member. No discipline may replace this observation with an explicit label.

### The Non-Interchangeability Standard for the Vale System

No two members should ever feel interchangeable, and the player should come to recognise each member before they speak. Every scene featuring the Vale System is authored so that the fronting member is expressed through enough distinct signals, voice, posture, priorities, and rapport, that an attentive player can identify them. If a member could be swapped for another without changing the scene, the scene has failed this standard.

### The Timeline-Consistency Standard for Vale System Fronting

Fronting must stay consistent with the master timeline and the character schedule. In Operation One, Elian meets the ordinary mornings, Naomi meets the emergencies and calls General Quarters, Gideon reads the failing systems, and Iris steadies the civilians. No presentation choice may place a member in a situation that contradicts those fixed documents.

## Crew Interaction Rules for the Vale System

The crew's behaviour around the Vale System is a major part of how the design reads to the player. The crew model the correct, ordinary response to fronting, and the player learns from them.

### The Unremarkable-Switch Standard for Vale System Crew Interaction

Crew members treat fronting changes as completely ordinary and remark on them only when directly relevant to the conversation. There is no shock, no correction, no discomfort, and no special ceremony. A crew member greeting Elian in the morning and taking an order from Naomi in a crisis does both without treating either as strange. This models the correct player attitude and upholds the canon that switches are ordinary ship life.

### The Correct-Name-and-Pronoun Standard for Vale System Crew Interaction

Crew, friends, and family use the fronting member's current name and current pronouns as an ordinary part of daily life: Elian by Elian's current pronouns, Naomi with they and them, Gideon with they and them, Iris with she and they. Getting these right is presented as ordinary respect, not as effort, and the game never dramatises it as difficult.

### The Differentiated-Rapport Standard for Vale System Crew Interaction

Individual crew members have differing rapport with different members, within the single shared history. Writers show this rapport in how a crew member speaks to the current member, which is itself one of the clearest cues to who is fronting. A crew member's warmth, formality, humour, or reliance shifts appropriately depending on which member they are addressing, while their underlying respect for the captain is constant.

### The Command-Respect Standard for Vale System Crew Interaction

Whichever member is fronting, the crew respect the single command authority the body carries. Fronting never undermines the captain's authority in the eyes of the crew. All four members are the captain, and the crew treat them so.

## UI Behaviour for the Vale System

User-interface design for the Vale System follows the project-wide principle that the interface should rarely identify a recurring character and that the world teaches the player who people are. The interface never becomes a crutch that removes the skill of reading the member.

### The No-Intrusive-Label Standard for Vale System UI

The interface does not pin a persistent member label over the Vale System, does not pop a name banner when a switch happens, and does not display a switch alert. Recognition is the player's job, supported by voice, posture, behaviour, and diegetic identifiers, not by heads-up-display text. This is consistent with recognition emerging through observation rather than intrusive UI labels.

### The Diegetic-Identifier Standard for Vale System UI

Where the current member is surfaced in an interface at all, it is through diegetic ship systems that already exist in the world: the name tape, the badge, the wrist display, the personal communicator profile, and terminal and login displays, all of which update automatically as ordinary ship operations. These are read as world objects, not as game overlays, and they corroborate the player's observation rather than replacing it.

### The Accessibility-Setting Standard for Vale System UI

Consistent with accessibility being foundational and configurable, any optional assistance a player enables to help identify crew members is a player-controlled setting, applied uniformly to the whole cast, and never a forced label specific to the Vale System. Such assistance is offered as customisation the player chooses, in the same spirit that crew members customise interfaces rather than requesting permission. The default experience remains recognition through observation.

### The Consistency-With-World Standard for Vale System UI

Any interface that must refer to the Vale System uses the current fronting member's name and pronouns, matching the diegetic identifiers and the crew's spoken language, so the interface never contradicts the world. The interface follows the world; the world never follows the interface.

## Accessibility Considerations for the Vale System

Accessibility is foundational, and the Vale System is designed so that players of every access profile can build the skill of recognising each member. Recognition is offered through multiple redundant channels so no single sense is required.

### Blind Player Considerations for the Vale System

Blind players recognise members primarily by voice, accent, vocabulary, pace, and rhythm, supported by pre-verbal cues in breathing and movement sound and by the acoustic context around the captain. Audio design ensures each member is distinguishable by ear from a short sample, and that identifier confirmation, where relevant, is available through the same accessible narration and audio cues the player uses for the rest of the world. No recognition cue for the Vale System is vision-only.

### Deaf and Hard-of-Hearing Player Considerations for the Vale System

Deaf and hard-of-hearing players recognise members primarily by posture, gait, gesture, facial performance, decision-making style, priorities, and rapport with the crew, supported by captioning and by diegetic identifiers such as the name tape and badge. Captions attribute lines to the current member by name, matching the diegetic identifiers, so a player reading rather than hearing still learns who is present. Animation carries enough distinct, readable body language that a member is identifiable with the sound off.

### Low-Vision Player Considerations for the Vale System

Low-vision players are served by clear, large-scale posture and gesture differences and by strong, readable facial performance, in addition to the full audio recognition set. Because member identity is carried by bold differences in bearing and movement rather than by fine visual detail on a fixed body, low-vision players can read the member from gross motion and voice together. Diegetic identifiers are presented at sizes and contrasts consistent with the game's accessibility standards.

### Cognitive and Learning Considerations for the Vale System

Recognition is a learnable skill, and the design supports players who need more time or more scaffolding. The four members' baselines are consistent and repeated, so the pattern is learnable through exposure. Optional, player-controlled assistance, applied uniformly to the whole cast, is available for players who want more explicit confirmation, without making that assistance the default and without singling out the Vale System. The crew's ordinary, unremarkable modelling of switches also gives every player a calm, low-pressure example of how to relate to the captain.

### The Redundant-Channel Standard for the Vale System

No member's identity is ever carried by a single channel alone. Voice, breathing, posture, gait, gesture, facial performance, decision style, priorities, crew rapport, and diegetic identifiers overlap so that whichever senses a player uses, enough distinct signals remain to build recognition. This redundancy is the accessibility backbone of the entire Vale System design and every discipline contributes to it.
