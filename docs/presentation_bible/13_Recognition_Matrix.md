# Recognition Matrix

This document is a defining feature of the Player Identity and Character Presentation Bible for Resonance: Fractured Frequency. For every recurring character it answers a single practical question from many angles: how is this person recognized? It records how different players, using different senses, and different people within the world, using different relationships, come to know each character, and how the player's own recognition deepens over five minutes, five hours, and fifty hours of play.

The authority for every identity fact here is the Canon Rules document and the Character Identity Map, which govern where any wording appears to differ. This document does not invent biography, ship history, place names, or new systems as settled canon. It defines recognition standards and presentation philosophy, rooted in the world: voice, gait, equipment sound, habits, relationships, and lived experience. Recognition is never carried by an intrusive interface label; the interface should rarely identify a recurring character, and the world teaches the player who people are.

Two characters carry deliberately open elements, which this document respects. Talia Rusk is the Two-Spirit character, and her nation, culture, and community are to be developed thoughtfully with Indigenous cultural consultation; this document invents no nation, language, or ceremony for her. Mira Ives is a genderfluid child using she and they, subject to development; her gender expression is treated with a light, respectful, non-fixed touch.

Each character section answers the same set of recognition questions as disambiguated subsections. The questions are the same across characters so any two can be compared; the answers are specific to each person.

## Mara Chen

Mara Chen is the chief engineering systems specialist, an Asian cisgender woman who uses she and her, and who is blind, navigating by sound, vibration, and touch with an engineering helmet whose navigation faceplate turns visual data into audio and tactile cues. She is known for a sure, listening gait and for describing systems as sounds. She is not a member of the Vale System.

### How a Blind Player Recognizes Mara Chen

- The listening quality of her gait, sure and deliberate, pausing to read a space by ear before moving through it.
- The soft working sounds of her engineering helmet and its navigation faceplate translating the environment into audio and tactile cues.
- Her voice and vocabulary, which describe systems as sounds and name the ship's notes rather than its readouts.
- The engineering ambience that gathers around her, the stable hum, relays, and generator cycling of the spaces she keeps.

### How a Deaf Player Recognizes Mara Chen

- Her distinctive engineering helmet with its navigation faceplate, a clear and consistent silhouette element.
- Her posture and gait, oriented toward listening and touch, hands and body reading surfaces and vibration.
- Captioned dialogue attributed to her, carrying her habit of describing systems as sounds.
- Her placement in the engineering spaces and at diagnostic and power stations she is known to work.

### How a Low-Vision Player Recognizes Mara Chen

- The bold shape of the engineering helmet and faceplate against her uniform silhouette.
- Her deliberate, grounded movement and the way she settles to listen, readable from gross motion.
- Consistent placement in the engineering bay and power room, spaces with their own recognizable look.
- Her voice and speech together with these visual cues, so no single channel carries the whole recognition.

### How a Child Recognizes Mara Chen

- The engineer with the helmet who can tell what is wrong just by listening to the ship.
- The way she kneels or leans in to hear a machine, which is memorable and easy to imitate.
- Her patience in explaining what a sound means, teaching through people rather than menus.

### How a Coworker Recognizes Mara Chen

- Her expertise expressed as acoustic diagnostics, hearing a fault before instruments report it.
- Her presence at engineering coordination, upgrade planning, and power management discussions.
- The reliability of her gait and helmet sounds announcing her arrival in a shared workspace.

### How a Close Friend Recognizes Mara Chen

- The subtleties of her voice and mood beneath the professional calm, the small shifts a friend hears.
- Her personal habits and the private ways she describes the ship, shared vocabulary built over time.
- The trust in how she asks for and offers help, treating accessibility as ordinary shared life.

### How a Stranger Recognizes Mara Chen

- The engineer moving with certainty through spaces without relying on sight, helmet faceplate active.
- Her way of describing a problem as a sound, distinctive on first meeting.
- Her clear competence in the engineering domain, evident before any introduction.

### How the Player Recognizes Mara Chen After 5 Minutes

- The player marks the helmet and faceplate, the listening gait, and the habit of naming systems as sounds as first, surface-level cues.

### How the Player Recognizes Mara Chen After 5 Hours

- The player identifies her by the sound of her approach and by her acoustic way of reasoning, and associates her with the engineering soundscape she stabilizes.

### How the Player Recognizes Mara Chen After 50 Hours

- The player knows her as a full person: her judgment, humor, and history with the crew, recognizing her instantly by voice, gait, and the notes she listens for, needing no label at all.

- The medical ambience around her, monitors, health alerts, and the steady rhythm of care.
- The way conversation with her runs through her projector's spoken text output, a distinctive cadence.

- Her attentive posture and the clear orientation of her gaze as she reads faces and vibration.
- Consistent placement in the recognizable medical spaces.

### How a Stranger Recognizes Doctor Samira Kade

### How the Player Recognizes Doctor Samira Kade After 5 Minutes

- The player marks the sleeve projector, its whisper, and her sign-and-text communication as first cues.

### How the Player Recognizes Doctor Samira Kade After 5 Hours

- The player recognizes her by the projector whisper and by her calm, room-reading manner, and associates her with the medical soundscape and the safety of her bay.

### How the Player Recognizes Doctor Samira Kade After 50 Hours

## Mira Ives

Mira Ives is a habitation-deck child and the daughter of Rowan Ives, a Black mixed-heritage child who is genderfluid and still coming to understand this for herself, using she and they, both subject to development as she grows. She is often the first to hear when something aboard the ship is wrong.

### How a Blind Player Recognizes Mira Ives

- The bright ambient sounds around her, laughter, questions, games, and the energy of a curious child.
- Her communication rhythm through AAC, a cadence the ear learns as uniquely hers.
- Her tendency to notice and call out a wrong sound in the ship before anyone else.

### How a Deaf Player Recognizes Mira Ives

- Her visible neural-interface AAC system and the messages it presents, her primary way of communicating.
- Her energetic, curious body language and her placement in the community room, education area, and creative spaces.

- Her presence near her father Rowan and among the habitation-deck children.

### How a Low-Vision Player Recognizes Mira Ives

- The visible AAC interface as a clear, consistent identifying element.
- Her small stature and lively movement, readable from gross motion.
- Her placement in the recognizable community and children's spaces.
- Her AAC output paired with these cues for redundancy.

### How a Close Friend Recognizes Mira Ives

### How a Stranger Recognizes Mira Ives

## Faience

Faience is a Shawabti artificial intelligence and is not human, standing outside human concepts of race, ethnicity, and gender, none of which are assigned. Faience is silent through Operation One and first stirs, wordless, in a damaged pod on Attack Day. Recognition of Faience is therefore unlike recognition of any human character: for most of Operation One, Faience is recognized by absence, and only at the very end by a first, wordless presence.

### How a Blind Player Recognizes Faience

- Through most of Operation One, by the silence where a companion would be, an absence the player comes to feel by ear.
- On Attack Day, in the damaged pod, by the first faint, wordless stirring that breaks that silence.
- By the distinctness of that first presence from every human voice and every machine sound heard before it.

### How a Deaf Player Recognizes Faience

- Through Operation One, by the consistent absence of any companion presence in the world.
- On Attack Day, by the first non-verbal indication of Faience stirring in the pod, presented through the game's accessible non-audio cues.
- By how clearly that first stirring differs from the human crew and the ship's systems.

### How a Low-Vision Player Recognizes Faience

- Through Operation One, by the absence of any companion, since Faience is not embodied as a visible crew member.
- On Attack Day, by the first, quiet, non-verbal sign of Faience in the damaged pod.
- By the way this first presence is set apart from everything encountered before it.

### How a Child Recognizes Faience

- For most of the story, not at all, because Faience has not yet stirred and says nothing.
- At the very end, as a new and gentle presence that wakes in the wreck, mysterious and not yet understood.

### How a Coworker Recognizes Faience

- No crew member hears Faience during the ordinary week or the last morning; Faience is absent from every profession's week.
- The crew's ordinary work has no companion AI at its side, so there is nothing yet to recognize.
- Only the player, alone in a damaged pod, meets Faience's first stirring, after the crew have launched.

### How a Close Friend Recognizes Faience

- No one is yet close to Faience, because Faience has only just begun to stir and has formed no relationships.
- The possibility of closeness begins only after the first wordless waking, beyond Operation One.
- Any future recognition by relationship is left to later development and is not present in Operation One.

### How a Stranger Recognizes Faience

- As nothing at all during Operation One, since Faience is silent and unencountered.
- At the very end, as an unknown first presence stirring in the dark of a damaged pod.
- As something plainly outside human race, ethnicity, and gender, unlike anyone met before.

### How the Player Recognizes Faience After 5 Minutes

- Not yet at all; in the opening the player has no companion, and Faience is entirely silent.

### How the Player Recognizes Faience After 5 Hours

- Still by absence; across the ordinary week the player feels the empty place where a companion would be, without knowing it will one day be filled.

### How the Player Recognizes Faience After 50 Hours

- By the memory of the first wordless stirring in the damaged pod and by whatever presence grows from it beyond Operation One, always recognized as something distinct from every human voice and outside human categories.
