# Cultural Presentation

This document is the craft reference for how culture is presented across the recurring cast, the professions, and the wider community of Resonance: Fractured Frequency. It is part of the Player Identity and Character Presentation Bible. It exists so that artists, animators, UI designers, audio designers, writers, and gameplay programmers portray culture with depth, specificity, and respect, and never as decoration, shorthand, or stereotype.

This document rests on two authorities. The Canon Rules document is the canon authority, and the Character Identity Map is the locked identity source for each recurring character's profession, ethnicity, gender, pronouns, and disability. Where anything here appears to differ from either, those documents govern. This document defines presentation standards and philosophy; it does not create canon.

This document does not invent character biography, ship history, specific place names, nations, ceremonies, languages, or new game systems as settled canon. Culture is one of the areas where fabrication does the most harm, so every specific here is framed as guidance rather than as established backstory. Where the identity map deliberately leaves an item open, this document keeps it open.

## Guiding Principles for Cultural Presentation

Culture in Resonance is portrayed as a living, ordinary part of a diverse human community, not as a set of exotic markers. The recurring cast intentionally reflects the breadth of humanity, and representation is a design requirement rather than a decorative afterthought. Cultural presentation is how that requirement is met with integrity.

- Representation is character-first. A character's culture is one meaningful aspect of a fully realized person, never the whole of who they are and never the reason they exist.
- No culture is treated as the default, and no culture is treated as exotic. Human diversity is presented as an ordinary part of civilization.
- Culture is specific, not generic. A real, particular cultural detail is always preferable to a blended, invented, or pan-cultural approximation.
- Culture is portrayed as lived and present-tense, not as museum artifact or costume. People practice culture; they do not merely display it.
- Cultural presentation avoids stereotype, caricature, and the reduction of a people to a single trait, food, garment, or symbol.
- Sacred, ceremonial, and closed cultural practices are handled with particular restraint and are never invented, appropriated, or shown without the guidance of the communities they belong to.

### Character-First Culture

- A character's culture emerges through their personality, relationships, habits, and choices, consistent with the canon that every recurring character is a complete person.
- Cultural detail is shown through lived behavior and observation rather than announced through labels, consistent with the canon that characters are recognized through observation.
- Culture is never used as a substitute for characterization. A character is not made interesting by their heritage; they are interesting people who also have heritage.
- Mixed-heritage characters are portrayed with the full complexity of belonging to more than one culture, without being reduced to a blend or forced to represent either heritage as a stereotype. Mara Chen, the Vale System, Rowan Ives, Mira Ives, Soren Qadir, Samira Kade, Jonah Briggs, and Talia Rusk each carry ethnicity as recorded in the Character Identity Map, and mixed-heritage characters among them are presented with that dual belonging intact.

### Specificity Over Generalization

- When a cultural detail is depicted, it should be grounded in a specific, real tradition developed with appropriate expertise, rather than assembled from generic impressions.
- Generic markers that stand in for a whole region or continent are avoided. A specific practice belonging to a specific community is always preferable to a vague gesture toward a broad group.
- Where a character's specific cultural background has not yet been developed, presentation waits for that development rather than filling the gap with invention.

### Culture as Ordinary Life

- Cultural practice appears woven into daily routines, work, meals, and relationships, consistent with the canon that the home is always evolving and that community is progression.
- Culture is presented without exoticizing camera work, music, or narration. A cultural detail receives the same ordinary register as any other part of a character's life.
- Cultural expression is shown as chosen and personal. Characters practice their culture in their own individual ways rather than as uniform representatives of a group.

## Hairstyles

Hair is a significant, personal, and often cultural aspect of presentation. It carries identity, heritage, profession, and individual choice, and it must be authored with care rather than defaulted to a narrow standard.

### Principles of Hairstyle Presentation

- Hair textures, styles, and care are portrayed accurately and respectfully across the full range of human hair, with none treated as the default.
- Culturally significant hairstyles are researched and depicted with accuracy, and those with specific cultural meaning are handled with the guidance of the relevant communities.
- Hair is personal. A character's hairstyle reflects their individual taste, profession, and practicality, not a generic assignment based on ethnicity.
- Protective styles, natural textures, coverings, and adornments are all presented as ordinary, valid, and well-crafted choices.

### Hairstyle Presentation and the Recurring Cast

- Each recurring character's hair is part of their consistent, recognizable presentation. For the Vale System, hair is one of the constants of the shared body and does not change with whoever is fronting, consistent with canon.
- Hair textures for Black and Black mixed-heritage characters, including Rowan Ives and Mira Ives, are rendered and animated with accuracy and respect, avoiding flattened or generic approximations.
- Any hairstyle carrying specific cultural or spiritual meaning, including for Indigenous characters, is developed only with community consultation and is never invented. This applies to Talia Rusk, whose specific nation and culture are to be developed, and no culturally specific hairstyle is assigned to her in advance of that work.
- Hair adornments that carry meaning are treated as meaningful, not as decoration, and are shown only when grounded in a developed, real tradition.

## Religious Expression

Religion and spirituality are portrayed as personal, varied, and ordinary parts of a diverse community. Cultural presentation must make room for belief, practice, and non-belief alike, without privileging or caricaturing any of them.

### Principles of Religious Presentation

- Religious and spiritual practice is portrayed with accuracy and respect, developed with appropriate expertise for any specific tradition depicted.
- No religion is treated as the default, as strange, or as a marker of virtue or menace. Belief is one ordinary aspect of a person's life.
- Religious practice is shown as personal and individual. Characters relate to their traditions in their own ways, including devout, occasional, cultural, questioning, and non-practicing relationships.
- The absence of religion is equally valid and is presented without judgment.

### Religious Presentation and Restraint

- Sacred practices, texts, spaces, and objects are handled with particular restraint and are never invented, misused, or shown without appropriate guidance.
- Religious garments, coverings, and symbols are presented as ordinary and dignified, never as costume or spectacle.
- Where a character's specific faith or spiritual life has not been developed in canon, presentation does not invent one. Religious identity is not assigned to a character merely to fill a slot.
- Indigenous spirituality in particular is never treated as generic or interchangeable, and is subject to the Indigenous representation framework in this document.

## Indigenous Representation

Indigenous representation in Resonance is held to the highest standard of care, because Indigenous peoples have been repeatedly misrepresented, flattened, and appropriated in media. This section defines a respect framework and a process. It intentionally does not define specific cultural content, because that content must be developed with the communities it belongs to.

### The Indigenous Representation Respect Framework

- Indigenous peoples are many distinct nations, cultures, languages, and communities. There is no single Indigenous culture, and pan-Indigenous representation that blends distinct peoples into one is never acceptable.
- Specificity is required. Authentic Indigenous representation belongs to a specific nation and community, developed with that community, rather than to a generic Indigenous identity.
- Nothing Indigenous is invented in this project. Nations, languages, ceremonies, regalia, symbols, songs, and spiritual practices are not created, approximated, or assembled by the development team.
- Sacred and closed knowledge is respected as closed. Some cultural knowledge is not meant to be shared publicly or depicted at all, and that boundary is honored absolutely.
- Indigenous characters are complete people whose lives are not reduced to their heritage, their trauma, or their relationship to colonization, consistent with the canon that every recurring character is a complete person.

### The Consultation Process for Indigenous Representation

- Indigenous representation is developed through direct, compensated, and ongoing consultation with members of the specific nation being represented, not through secondhand research alone.
- Consultation begins before cultural content is created and continues through review of art, animation, audio, and writing, so that community guidance shapes the work rather than merely approving it after the fact.
- Community members hold authority over how their culture is represented, including the authority to decline the depiction of any specific practice.
- Consultation determines what may be shown, how it may be shown, and what must not be shown, and the project abides by those determinations.

### Avoiding Pan-Indigenous Stereotype

- Presentation avoids all generic markers that flatten distinct peoples into one image, including generic regalia, generic symbols, generic music, and generic spiritual language.
- Presentation avoids historical stereotype that fixes Indigenous people in the past. Indigenous characters are contemporary, present-tense people within the world of the game.
- Presentation avoids the reduction of Indigenous identity to a single narrative of loss, and instead allows for the full range of a person's life, including expertise, humor, relationships, and joy.
- Visual, audio, and written shorthand that signals a generic Indigenous identity is prohibited.

### Talia Rusk and Deliberately Undeveloped Cultural Specifics

- Talia Rusk is the Native character and the Two-Spirit character. Two-Spirit is a culturally specific Indigenous identity and is never used as a generic gender label, consistent with canon.
- Talia's specific nation, culture, and community are deliberately undeveloped and are to be developed thoughtfully, ideally with Indigenous cultural consultation. This is a genuinely open item recorded in the Character Identity Map.
- Until that development happens, no nation, language, ceremony, regalia, song, symbol, or spiritual practice is invented or assigned to Talia. She is written and presented with respect for that specificity and without a placeholder culture.
- In the interim, Talia is portrayed through the parts of her that are established: a steady security lead, inclined to put others ahead of herself, who uses a prosthetic limb. Her Two-Spirit identity is honored as real and culturally specific, and its cultural context waits for community-guided development rather than being filled in.
- Because Two-Spirit identity is rooted in specific Indigenous cultures, its presentation is inseparable from the consultation process above and is never depicted as a generic or free-floating gender category.

## Tattoos

Tattoos and body markings carry a wide range of meaning across human cultures, from personal expression to deep cultural and spiritual significance. Their presentation requires distinguishing ordinary personal tattoos from culturally meaningful markings.

### Principles of Tattoo Presentation

- Personal, decorative, and individually meaningful tattoos are presented as ordinary self-expression and are a valid part of any character's look.
- Culturally or spiritually significant tattoos and markings are researched and depicted only with the guidance of the communities they belong to, and are never invented or approximated.
- Sacred or closed marking traditions are never appropriated, recreated, or worn by characters who do not belong to those traditions.
- Tattoo designs that belong to a specific culture are not applied generically or for aesthetic effect divorced from their meaning.

### Tattoo Presentation and Indigenous Marking Traditions

- Indigenous marking and tattoo traditions are culturally specific and, in some communities, sacred. They fall entirely under the Indigenous representation framework in this document.
- No Indigenous marking is invented or assigned to any character, including Talia Rusk, in advance of community-guided development.
- Where a marking tradition is closed to a community, it is not depicted at all.

## Jewelry

Jewelry ranges from personal adornment to items of profound cultural, familial, and spiritual meaning. Presentation must respect that range and avoid treating meaningful items as mere decoration.

### Principles of Jewelry Presentation

- Personal jewelry is presented as ordinary individual expression and can reflect taste, relationships, and profession.
- Culturally significant jewelry is researched and depicted accurately, and items with specific cultural or spiritual meaning are handled with community guidance.
- Jewelry that carries meaning, such as items marking kinship, milestone, faith, or heritage, is treated as meaningful within the fiction rather than as costume.
- Sacred or ceremonial adornment is never invented or worn casually, and closed items are not depicted outside their proper context.

### Jewelry Presentation and Recognition

- Personal jewelry can become part of a character's recognizable presentation, consistent with recognition through observation, when it is a consistent and characterful choice.
- For the Vale System, any jewelry worn on the shared body is part of the body's constants and does not change with whoever is fronting, consistent with canon.
- Culturally specific adornment for Indigenous characters, including Talia Rusk, is not invented and is subject to the Indigenous representation framework.

## Ceremonial Clothing

Ceremonial and traditional clothing carries some of the deepest cultural meaning of any presentation element, and it is among the most frequently appropriated. Its presentation is governed by restraint and by community authority.

### Principles of Ceremonial Clothing Presentation

- Everyday cultural clothing is presented as ordinary and dignified, worn by choice as a normal part of life.
- Ceremonial, sacred, and regalia clothing is depicted only when developed with the guidance of the community it belongs to, and only in ways that community approves.
- Ceremonial clothing is never treated as costume, spectacle, or generic decoration, and is never assembled from mixed or invented elements.
- Regalia and sacred garments belonging to a specific people are worn only by characters of that people and only in appropriate contexts, as determined through consultation.

### Ceremonial Clothing and Indigenous Regalia

- Indigenous regalia is culturally specific, often sacred, and frequently earned or conferred within a community. It falls entirely under the Indigenous representation framework in this document.
- No regalia is invented or assigned to any character, including Talia Rusk, and no ceremony that would involve regalia is invented.
- Where regalia or a ceremony is not appropriate to depict, it is not depicted, and its absence is respected rather than filled with invention.

## Family Traditions

Family traditions ground characters in relationships, memory, and continuity. They are portrayed as ordinary, warm, and specific parts of characters' lives, consistent with a world where community and relationships are progression.

### Principles of Family Tradition Presentation

- Family traditions are shown as lived and personal, emerging through behavior, routine, and relationship rather than through exposition.
- Family structures are portrayed in their real diversity, including many configurations of kin, chosen family, and community, without any single structure treated as the default.
- Traditions carry specificity when a character's cultural background is developed, and remain understated when it is not, rather than being invented to fill a gap.
- Family traditions are one meaningful aspect of a character and never reduce them to a representative of a culture.

### Family Tradition Presentation and the Recurring Cast

- Rowan Ives and Mira Ives are parent and child, and their family life is presented as an ordinary, evolving part of the habitation deck, with traditions shown through their relationship rather than announced.
- Mira Ives is a child still growing into her own understanding, and any family tradition around her identity is portrayed with a light, respectful, non-fixed touch, consistent with her genderfluid identity and the open development of how she expresses it.
- Family and community traditions across the cast are portrayed as part of the home that is always evolving, changing over time as relationships and routines change.
- For Indigenous characters, including Talia Rusk, family and community traditions tied to a specific nation are not invented and await community-guided development.

## Food

Food is one of the most immediate and joyful expressions of culture, and one of the easiest to reduce to stereotype. Its presentation is specific, respectful, and woven into ordinary life.

### Principles of Food Presentation

- Food is portrayed as specific and real, tied to particular dishes, ingredients, and practices developed with appropriate knowledge, rather than to generic impressions of a cuisine.
- Food is not used as a stand-in for a whole people, and a culture is never reduced to a single dish or flavor.
- Food is shown as shared, ordinary, and social, part of the daily life and community of the ship, consistent with community as progression.
- Food practices tied to faith, ceremony, or specific tradition are handled with the same respect and restraint as other cultural elements, and closed or sacred food practices are not invented.

### Food Presentation Through Sound and Accessibility

- Because audio is gameplay, food is presented through sound as much as sight, including the sounds of cooking, sharing, and eating together as part of a location's acoustic identity.
- Food-related information important to gameplay or story reaches the player through multiple channels, consistent with the accessibility foundation.
- Communal meals are an opportunity to present the crew's diversity as ordinary and warm, with different practices coexisting without comment.

## Holidays

Holidays and observances mark time, memory, and belief. They are portrayed as varied, personal, and ordinary parts of a diverse community's calendar, rather than as universal or defaulted.

### Principles of Holiday Presentation

- Holidays and observances are portrayed with specificity and respect for any real tradition depicted, developed with appropriate expertise.
- No single holiday tradition is treated as universal or as the default, and the community's calendar reflects its diversity.
- Characters relate to holidays individually, including celebrating, observing quietly, adapting traditions far from home, and not observing at all.
- Sacred and solemn observances are handled with restraint and are not invented, trivialized, or turned into spectacle.

### Holiday Presentation and the Evolving Home

- Observances are part of the home that is always evolving, and the ways the community marks time can change over the course of the story.
- Holidays adapted to life aboard a ship are presented as meaningful acts of continuity, portrayed through behavior and relationship rather than exposition.
- Indigenous observances tied to a specific nation, including anything relating to Talia Rusk's community, are not invented and await community-guided development.

## Languages

Language is central to Resonance, where communication diversity is ordinary and every communication method is equally valid. Cultural presentation of language must honor spoken languages, signed languages, and alternative communication alike.

### Principles of Language Presentation

- Multilingual communication is portrayed as an ordinary part of a global, diverse community, and no language is treated as the default against which others are marked.
- Spoken languages are depicted accurately, with correct pronunciation and usage developed with fluent speakers, and are never reduced to accent or generic foreign-sounding speech.
- Signed languages are full languages and are presented as such, with the accuracy defined in the accessibility presentation standards, developed with fluent signers and relevant communities.
- Augmentative and alternative communication is presented as a person's own voice, consistent with canon, never as a device speaking for them.

### Language Presentation and Indigenous Languages

- Indigenous languages are specific to specific nations and are never invented, approximated, or assembled. They fall entirely under the Indigenous representation framework in this document.
- No Indigenous language is assigned to Talia Rusk or created for her community, consistent with the deliberately undeveloped state of her specific culture.
- Where an Indigenous language would appear, it is used only with the guidance and consent of the specific community, and only in ways that community approves.

### Language Presentation Across Communication Methods

- The recurring cast naturally includes spoken language, signed language, augmentative and alternative communication, braille literacy, tactile signing, other alternative systems, and multilingual communication, consistent with canon.
- Samira Kade communicates through sign and live text, and her language is presented as complete and fluent, with its content conveyed to the player as her own words.
- Mira Ives communicates through her neural-interface augmentative and alternative communication system, and her language is presented as her own voice with her own personality.
- The presentation of every language and communication method carries equal validity and dignity, reflecting a world that adapts to people rather than requiring people to adapt to exclusion.

## Cultural Respect Guidelines

These guidelines consolidate the standards that govern all cultural presentation in Resonance. They apply to every section above and to any future cultural content.

### General Cultural Respect Guidelines

- Represent cultures with specificity, developed with the guidance of the communities being represented, and never through generic approximation.
- Do not invent cultures, nations, languages, ceremonies, sacred practices, or closed knowledge. Where content is not developed, wait rather than fabricate.
- Keep representation character-first, so culture enriches a complete person rather than defining or replacing them.
- Present culture as ordinary, present-tense, lived life, never as exotic spectacle or museum artifact.
- Avoid stereotype, caricature, and the reduction of a people to a single trait, food, garment, symbol, or narrative.
- Honor closed and sacred knowledge as closed, and abide by community determinations about what may and may not be shown.

### Process Guidelines for Cultural Content

- Involve members of the represented community early, throughout, and with fair compensation, giving them authority over how their culture is depicted.
- Treat consultation as authorship guidance rather than after-the-fact approval, so community input shapes art, animation, audio, and writing from the start.
- Record genuinely open cultural items honestly rather than closing them with invention, consistent with the open development items in the Character Identity Map.
- Recheck cultural presentation whenever the Canon Rules document or the Character Identity Map changes.

### Guidelines for Genuinely Open Cultural Items

- Talia Rusk's specific nation, culture, and community remain to be developed with Indigenous cultural consultation, and no nation, language, ceremony, regalia, or spiritual practice is invented for her in the meantime.
- Mira Ives is a genderfluid child using she and they, and the way she expresses her gender remains open to development as the character and story mature, so her presentation keeps a light, respectful, non-fixed touch.
- These open items are honored as deliberate, not as gaps to be filled, and they are developed only with the care and, where relevant, the community guidance they require.
