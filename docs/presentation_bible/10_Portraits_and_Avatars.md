# Portraits and Avatars

This document defines the standards for character portraits and player avatars in Resonance: Fractured Frequency. It is written for portrait artists, lighting artists, technical artists, UI designers, audio designers, writers, and accessibility engineers. In Resonance a portrait is never purely a picture. Because audio is gameplay and accessibility is foundational, every visual portrait is paired with an audio-first, blind-friendly description that carries the same identity, dignity, and information. A portrait that exists only as an image is incomplete.

This document rests on two higher authorities and never contradicts them. The Canon Rules document is the canon authority. The Character Identity Map is the locked source for each recurring character's profession, ethnicity, gender, pronouns, and disability. Where any guidance here appears to differ from those documents, those documents govern. This file defines craft standards and presentation philosophy; it does not invent character biography, ship history, place names, or new game systems.

## Foundational Principles of Portraits and Avatars

Portraits and avatars in Resonance serve belonging, recognition, accessibility, and character truth. They are how the community becomes visible and audible over time, and they carry the same weight for players who never see them as for players who do.

### Portraits Represent Belonging

Following the canon rule that portraits represent belonging and not recruitment, a recurring character's portrait is not fully present the moment they are rescued. A crew member becomes home only after they settle, begin contributing, and change the environment around them, and only then does their portrait illuminate.

- A portrait's state must reflect a character's journey from rescued to belonging, and full illumination is reserved for genuine belonging.
- The audio-first description of a portrait must express this same arc, so a non-sighted player perceives belonging as clearly as a sighted player perceives illumination.
- Sound is progression, so the acoustic presence of a character in the community grows before or alongside the visual illumination of their portrait, never lagging behind it as an afterthought.
- Portraits never present a character as a trophy of recruitment; they present a person who now belongs.

### Every Portrait Has an Audio-First Description

No portrait is complete without an authored audio-first, blind-friendly description that conveys who the person is with the same richness the image offers.

- Every portrait ships with a written description crafted for speech and braille output, not an automated alt-text stub.
- The description conveys identity, presence, profession, mood, and the sense of the person, in language that respects them as a complete person.
- Descriptions are authored in coordination with the writing team so they match each character's established voice and dignity.
- The description and the image are reviewed together and must agree; if the image changes, the description is updated in the same pass.

### Recognition Emerges Through Observation

Portraits support recognition but never replace the world's job of teaching who people are. The interface rarely identifies a recurring character, and portraits follow that restraint.

- A portrait reinforces a recognition the player has already been building through voice, movement, and habit; it does not substitute for that learning.
- Portrait descriptions avoid reducing a character to a label and instead express the observable qualities that make the person recognisable.
- The player should feel a portrait confirm someone they already know rather than being told who a stranger is.

### Portraits and the Vale System

The Vale System is one shared body inhabited by four members who front in turn, and the portrait system honours this fully. The body is constant and only its presentation changes, so the portrait represents one body whose expression, posture, and identifiers reflect whoever is fronting.

- There is one shared body portrait for the Vale System, never four separate character portraits and never a single person named Captain Mara Vale.
- The portrait's constant elements, such as face, hair, eye colour, skin tone, build, scars, accessibility equipment, uniform, and insignia, never change between members.
- The portrait's changing elements, such as expression, eye focus, head position, and posture, reflect the fronting member, and personal identifiers shown in or beside the portrait update automatically as ordinary ship operations.
- The audio-first description of the Vale System portrait describes one body and expresses the current member through observable presentation rather than by treating them as a separate person.

### Dignity, Authenticity, and Non-Tokenism in Portraits

Every recurring character is a complete person, and portraiture reflects that. Representation informs identity and never replaces it.

- Portraits present each character with dignity, specificity, and individuality, never as a symbol of a demographic.
- Assistive equipment appears naturally in portraits as standard equipment and is never framed as a burden or a spectacle.
- Cultural presentation is never invented for characters whose culture is undeveloped in canon, and portraits for such characters stay with personal, individual presentation.
- Portrait descriptions describe ethnicity, gender, and disability as ordinary facts within a full portrait of a person, never as the entire subject.

## Portrait Standards

This section defines the baseline requirements every recurring character portrait meets, regardless of context or chapter.

### Portrait Purpose and Placement Standards

- Each recurring character has a definitive portrait that serves as their canonical presented likeness across the franchise.
- Portraits appear in community and belonging contexts and are governed by the belonging rule for illumination state.
- Every portrait placement that shows the image also exposes the paired audio-first description so both are always available together.

### Portrait Consistency Standards

- A character's portrait remains recognizably consistent across chapters so returning players keep their recognition, allowing for authored change through ageing and growth described later in this document.
- The constant identity facts from the Character Identity Map, such as profession, ethnicity, gender, pronouns, and disability, are honoured consistently in every portrait and every description.
- The visual likeness and the audio-first description must always describe the same person in the same state.

### Portrait Emotional Range Standards

- Portraits may express a range of emotional states appropriate to context, from calm belonging to concern during hardship, while preserving the character's identity and dignity.
- Every emotional variant of a portrait has its own audio-first description so the emotional state is perceivable without sight.
- Emotional variation never contradicts a character's established personality or the canon that each character is a complete person.

### Portrait Illumination and Belonging States

- A portrait has defined states along the arc from rescued to belonging, and the illuminated state is reserved for genuine belonging as the canon requires.
- Each state is expressed both visually and in the audio-first description, so belonging is legible through either channel.
- The transition to full illumination is meaningful and is accompanied by the community's acoustic growth, honouring sound as progression.

## Lighting

Lighting shapes how a portrait reads, and in Resonance it is always designed with an accessible counterpart so that light itself carries meaning that a non-sighted player can also perceive.

### Portrait Lighting Design Standards

- Portrait lighting is designed to present each character with clarity, warmth, and dignity appropriate to belonging.
- Lighting supports recognition by keeping a character's defining features legible rather than obscuring them for style alone.
- Lighting respects the acoustic identity of the community spaces where portraits live, so the visual mood and the sonic mood agree.

### Portrait Illumination as Belonging Light

- The illumination that marks belonging is a deliberate lighting state, not merely brightness, and it signals that a character has become home.
- The belonging light has a defined audio-first counterpart so a non-sighted player perceives the same milestone through description and through the community's growing soundscape.
- Pre-belonging states use dimmer or unlit presentation, and their descriptions convey that the character is present but not yet fully home.

### Portrait Lighting Accessibility Standards

- Lighting must maintain sufficient contrast and clarity for low-vision players and must not rely on subtle lighting cues alone to convey state.
- Any meaning carried by lighting, such as belonging or emotional tone, is always also carried in the audio-first description.
- Lighting choices honour seizure-safe design consistent with the ship's standard seizure-safe lighting, avoiding harsh flashing in portrait presentation.

## Framing

Framing determines what a portrait includes and how the character is composed, and it directly affects both recognition and dignity.

### Portrait Framing Composition Standards

- Portrait framing centres the character with composition that presents them as a confident, complete person.
- Framing includes the elements that make a character recognisable, which may include their characteristic posture, professional presence, and assistive equipment as natural parts of who they are.
- Framing avoids compositions that crop out or hide assistive equipment as if it were something to conceal.

### Portrait Framing for Mobility and Equipment

- For characters who use assistive equipment, framing accommodates that equipment naturally; a portrait of Soren Qadir includes his chair as an ordinary part of his presence, and a portrait of Mara Chen includes her engineering helmet as ordinary.
- Framing never reduces a character to their equipment, and the audio-first description balances equipment with the whole person.
- Framing choices are consistent for a character across chapters so recognition is preserved.

### Portrait Framing and the Vale System

- The Vale System portrait uses consistent framing of the one shared body across all four members, so the constant body reads as constant.
- Framing accommodates the changing posture and head position of the fronting member without changing the body itself.
- The audio-first description frames the portrait as one body presenting the current member.

## Avatar Creation

The player creates an original professional assigned to a chosen department whose story unfolds alongside the established crew. Avatar creation is the system through which the player builds and presents that original character. It follows the canon that the player never replaces a canonical character and never alters canon.

### Player Avatar Scope Standards

- The player's avatar is always an original professional, never a recurring canonical character, and never the Vale System.
- Avatar customisation is free and expressive but never changes the existence, history, identity, relationships, or appearance of any recurring character.
- The player selects a department from the eight professions, which are Medic, Scientist, Engineer, Maintenance, Security, Commissioned Officer, Non-Commissioned Officer, and Civilian, and the avatar presents accordingly.

### Accessible Avatar Creation Standards

- Avatar creation is fully playable through multiple input and output methods and is configured by experience rather than diagnosis, consistent with the Accessibility Bible.
- Every visual option in avatar creation has an audio-first description so a non-sighted player can build and understand their avatar with equal richness.
- Avatar creation supports assistive equipment as ordinary, standard options rather than special additions, so a player may present with a wheelchair, cane, prosthetic, communication device, or other equipment as a natural choice.
- Nothing in avatar creation requires sight, and no confirmation or navigation depends on a visual cue alone.

### Inclusive Avatar Option Standards

- Avatar creation offers a broad, non-default range of ethnicity, gender presentation, and communication style so no identity is treated as the default.
- Gender presentation options and pronoun selection are offered respectfully and are learned by the game through natural interaction rather than intrusive repeated prompts, consistent with how pronouns are handled in the world.
- Options are presented without tokenism, as an ordinary reflection of the breadth of humanity.
- Avatar creation never asks the player to disclose a diagnosis and instead offers presentation and interaction choices directly.

### Player Avatar Portrait and Description Standards

- The player's avatar has its own portrait and its own audio-first description generated from the player's choices, so the player's character is represented with the same standards as the recurring cast.
- The avatar's audio-first description is available wherever the avatar portrait appears.
- The avatar participates in the belonging and community systems as an original member of the crew, without displacing any recurring character.

## Future Photogrammetry

Photogrammetry is identified here as a future capture technique for building portrait and character likenesses from real-world reference. It is described as forward-looking guidance, not as an existing pipeline, and any adoption is defined in coordination with the technical art and writing teams.

### Photogrammetry Adoption Standards

- Photogrammetry, if adopted, must preserve the established likeness and recognition signals of each recurring character rather than replacing them with a different-looking capture.
- Captured likenesses are reconciled against the canonical portrait and the Character Identity Map before use, and the identity facts always govern.
- Photogrammetry is treated as a tool serving the characters, consistent with the philosophy that technology exists to serve people.

### Photogrammetry Accessibility Standards

- Any photogrammetry-derived portrait still requires a full authored audio-first description; a captured image is never shipped without one.
- Visual fidelity gained through photogrammetry never becomes a reason to under-invest in the audio-first and tactile presentation of a character.

### Photogrammetry Ethics and Consent Standards

- Photogrammetry involving real people requires informed consent and respectful use, and cultural likeness is never captured or stylised casually.
- For characters whose culture is undeveloped in canon, such as Talia Rusk, photogrammetry must not be used to invent cultural specificity, and such development waits for appropriate cultural consultation.

## Motion Capture

Motion capture is identified here as a technique for capturing performance to drive character movement and expressive portraits. It connects portraiture to the animation standards in the animation document, and it is described as guidance rather than as an existing pipeline.

### Motion Capture Performance Standards

- Motion capture, if used, must preserve each character's established movement signature and gesture vocabulary so recognition is maintained.
- Captured performance is reconciled with the animation standards and the Character Identity Map, which govern over any raw capture.
- Performance for signing characters requires fluent signing performers and linguistic accuracy, consistent with the signing standards in the animation document.

### Motion Capture for the Vale System

- Motion capture for the Vale System must keep the body constant while capturing the distinct presentation of each fronting member, and never present four separate bodies or a single Captain Mara Vale.
- Performances for the four members are captured to express their distinct posture, gait, and gesture while sharing one rig and one body.

### Motion Capture Accessibility and Authenticity Standards

- Captured expressive performance that appears in portraits still requires audio-first descriptions of the resulting expression and state.
- Disability is portrayed authentically in captured performance, ideally with performers whose lived experience matches, and always with dignity and without tokenism.
- Cultural movement is never invented through capture for characters whose culture is undeveloped in canon.

## Portrait Ageing Through Future Operations

Resonance spans multiple Operations, and characters may age and change across them. Portrait ageing is the deliberate practice of evolving portraits over time so the passage of Operations is visible and audible while recognition is preserved. This is described as forward-looking presentation guidance and does not assert specific future events as settled canon.

### Portrait Ageing Continuity Standards

- Ageing a portrait preserves the core recognition signals of a character so returning players still know them, while reflecting the time that has passed.
- The constant identity facts from the Character Identity Map remain constant through ageing; ageing changes appearance over time, not who a person fundamentally is.
- Each aged portrait is treated as the current living portrait of the character rather than a competing alternate, consistent with maintaining one living representation.

### Portrait Ageing and Growth Standards

- Ageing reflects growth, experience, and the continuing life of the community, honouring the canon that the home is always evolving.
- For a growing child such as Mira Ives, ageing is handled with a light, respectful touch that leaves room for the character's development, including the open development of her gender expression, and never fixes what canon leaves open.
- Ageing never erases assistive equipment or a character's disability, which remain ordinary parts of who they are across Operations.

### Portrait Ageing Accessibility Standards

- Every aged portrait ships with an updated audio-first description that conveys the passage of time and the character's current presence.
- The audio-first description makes ageing perceivable to non-sighted players so the visible evolution of the community is not sight-dependent.
- The acoustic identity of a character may also evolve across Operations, and portrait ageing is coordinated with that so sound and image age together.

### Portrait Ageing for the Vale System

- Ageing the Vale System portrait ages the one shared body, and all four members share that ageing because they share the body.
- Each member's distinct presentation continues to read on the aged body, so recognition of the fronting member is preserved across Operations.
- The audio-first description of the aged Vale System portrait continues to describe one body presenting the current member.

## Portrait and Avatar Review and Acceptance

Every portrait and avatar is reviewed against consistent criteria before acceptance, so belonging, recognition, accessibility, and character truth are verified rather than assumed. This review complements the technical and representation review standards elsewhere in this bible.

### Portrait Acceptance Criteria

- The portrait presents the character with dignity and individuality as a complete person, consistent with the Canon Rules and the Character Identity Map.
- The portrait ships with a complete, authored audio-first, blind-friendly description that describes the same person in the same state.
- The portrait's belonging and illumination state is correct for the character's place in the community, and the community's soundscape supports it.
- Assistive equipment appears naturally as standard equipment, and no character is reduced to their equipment or their demographic.

### Vale System Portrait Acceptance Criteria

- The portrait depicts one shared body, never four separate people and never a single Captain Mara Vale.
- The constant body elements are unchanged across members while presentation reflects the fronting member.
- Personal identifiers shown with the portrait update as ordinary ship operations, and the audio-first description frames the portrait as one body presenting the current member.

### Portrait and Avatar Consistency Checks

- The portrait matches the character's established likeness and recognition signals from prior chapters, allowing for authored ageing and growth.
- The visual portrait and the audio-first description agree in identity, state, and mood.
- Any cultural or gender content that is undeveloped in canon has been kept open rather than invented, consistent with the open development items in the Character Identity Map.
- The player avatar honours the rule that customisation never alters canon and never replaces a recurring character.
