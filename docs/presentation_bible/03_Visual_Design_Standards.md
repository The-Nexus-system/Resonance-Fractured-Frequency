# Visual Design Standards

This document is the definitive reference for how the recurring cast of Resonance: Fractured Frequency is designed and presented to the eye. It exists for character artists, concept artists, modelers, texture and material artists, animators, riggers, technical artists, UI designers, lighting artists, and anyone else who shapes what a player sees. It is a companion to the audio identity standards, and it is subordinate to two authorities: the Canon Rules document and the Character Identity Map. Where anything here appears to differ from either of those, they govern and this document is corrected.

Resonance is a sound-first, accessibility-first game. Visuals enhance the experience but are never required, and the world must be fully understandable through sound alone. This creates a discipline that runs through every standard below: every visual identity signal must have a non-visual equivalent so that a player who never sees the screen loses no meaning. A visual design decision is not finished until its audio, haptic, or text counterpart is defined. Visual design in Resonance is therefore not the primary channel of characterization; it is a parallel channel that must always agree with, and never outrun, what the world already communicates through sound, movement, and behavior.

These standards deliberately give ranges, principles, and craft direction rather than fixed physical measurements. The canon documents establish who each character is, their profession, ethnicity, gender, pronouns, and disability, and this document builds presentation guidance on that foundation without inventing hard physical facts the canon does not establish. Where a specific look is described, it is written as guidance for the craft, not as settled backstory.

## Foundational Principles for Visual Design

Visual design in Resonance serves recognition, dignity, and truth, in that order of discipline and with no order of importance among them.

- Recognition through observation. Characters become recognizable naturally, the way real people do, through accumulated observation rather than labels. The interface should rarely name a recurring character. Visual design must give the eye enough consistent, honest signal that a returning player knows who they are looking at before any text confirms it.
- Character first, never tokenistic. Every recurring character is a complete person whose identity facts describe one meaningful aspect of who they are. Visual design never reduces a character to a single trait, never treats any ethnicity, gender, disability, or communication style as the default, and never stages a person as a symbol.
- Parallel channels, never a single channel. Every visual signal that carries meaning must have a non-visual equivalent. If a player learns something by seeing it, a player who is not seeing it must learn the same thing by hearing it, feeling it, or reading it.
- Honesty of the body. Bodies are drawn as bodies. Disability is neither hidden nor exaggerated for drama, neither sanded away for comfort nor spotlighted for pity. Equipment is shown as ordinary, well-used, and personal.
- Consistency is canon. The stable facts of a character's appearance do not change to suit a shot. Continuity across chapters, professions, and marketing is a promise to the player and a requirement of production.

### Non-Visual Equivalence as a Design Requirement

No visual identity standard in this document is considered complete on its own. Each must be paired with a non-visual counterpart before it ships.

- A color that carries meaning must also carry a sound, a texture, a haptic pattern, or a text label that means the same thing.
- A silhouette that identifies a character must be matched by a gait, a voice, an equipment sound, or an environmental signature that identifies the same person.
- A visual state change, such as an emergency posture or an off-duty change of clothes, must be legible in audio and text as clearly as it is legible on screen.
- When a visual and a non-visual signal could ever disagree, the non-visual signal is treated as the source of truth for meaning, because a player must be able to trust the world with the screen off.

## Face Standards

The face is where the eye first looks for a person, so faces in Resonance are designed to be individual, readable, and true to each character's ethnicity and age without becoming caricatures. Faces should read clearly at conversational distance and in the low, warm, practical light of a working ship.

- Individuality over idealization. Faces are specific, not smoothed toward a house standard of beauty. Asymmetry, character lines, and lived-in features are welcome and should be preserved across models and levels of detail.
- Ethnic truth without stereotype. Facial structure honors each character's ethnicity as established in the Character Identity Map, rendered with the care of reference and consultation rather than shorthand. Mixed-heritage characters are drawn as whole individuals, not as a visible sum of parts.
- Expressiveness serves reading. Faces are rigged to support the full range of expression a scene needs, including subtle expressions, because recognition of mood is part of how players read people. Expression is never the only carrier of a character's emotional state; voice, posture, and behavior carry it in parallel.
- Eyes and gaze. Gaze direction is a meaningful signal and is animated deliberately. For characters whose attention or focus differs from a hearing-sighted norm, gaze is authored honestly rather than forced into eye contact conventions.

### Face Standards for the Vale System

The Vale System is one shared body inhabited by four members who front in turn. The face is constant; its expression is not.

- The facial structure, skin tone, and features never change with fronting. The body is one body, Native mixed-heritage, and its face is the same face whichever member is present.
- What changes is expression, eye focus, and head position. Elian, Naomi, Gideon, and Iris each carry a distinct facial manner: the set of the brow, the resting mouth, the quality of the gaze, the tempo of expression. These are performance and animation layers on one unchanging face.
- Recognition of the current member must never depend on the face alone, and never on any visual relabeling. The face is one input among voice, posture, gait, gesture, and priorities. Visual design supports recognition; it does not carry it by itself.
- Never render the four members as four faces, never blend them into a single averaged persona, and never present the body as a character named Captain Mara Vale.

### Face Standards for Mara Chen

- Mara Chen is Asian, a cisgender woman, and blind, and her face is designed as an engineer's face: attentive, composed, oriented toward listening rather than looking.
- Her gaze is authored honestly and is not forced toward the camera or toward speakers for the comfort of sighted viewers. Her attention is shown through head tilt toward sound, stillness while listening, and the quiet focus of someone reading a room by ear.
- Her navigation helmet and its faceplate are part of her presentation and are addressed under equipment and accessibility devices, but her face beneath and around that equipment remains fully individual and expressive.

### Face Standards for the Recurring Cast

- Doctor Samira Kade is White mixed-heritage, a transgender woman, and Deaf. Her face is highly communicative because she reads rooms by sight and vibration and expresses through signed language and text; facial grammar that accompanies sign is animated with accuracy and respect, never as generic gesturing.
- Rowan Ives is Black, a cisgender man, living with chronic pain and arthritis. His face carries the honest, changeable weather of someone managing pain across a day, without being staged as suffering.
- Mira Ives is Black mixed-heritage, a child, genderfluid, autistic and with attention-deficit hyperactivity disorder. Her face is a child's face, bright and mobile, authored with respect for autistic expression rather than corrected toward neurotypical convention.
- Jonah Briggs is White, a transgender man, living with epilepsy. His face reads as the calm, steady presence who reads out priorities.
- Talia Rusk is Native, Two-Spirit, and a lower-limb amputee. Her face reads as steady under threat and attentive to others. Her nation and culture are deliberately undeveloped and must not be signaled through invented facial or ornamental markers.
- Doctor Soren Qadir is Asian mixed-heritage, a demiboy, and a wheelchair user. His face reads as the persistent investigator, focused and unhurried.
- Faience is a Shawabti artificial intelligence and has no human face and no assigned human gender, ethnicity, or race. Any visual form given to Faience is addressed as presentation of an artificial intelligence and never as a human face.

## Hair Standards

Hair is a strong recognition signal and a place where ethnicity, personality, and daily life are honestly expressed. Hair is designed to be specific, correct, and practical for shipboard work.

- Texture truth. Hair texture, density, and growth patterns follow each character's ethnicity with accuracy and are rendered and groomed with appropriate reference. Textured and coily hair is authored with the same craft budget as straight hair, never simplified as a shortcut.
- Practicality of a working crew. Most crew keep hair in styles that suit hazardous, hands-on work, helmets, and long shifts. Practicality is characterization, not blandness.
- Change over time is allowed and meaningful. Hair may grow, be cut, be restyled, or gray across a long campaign. Such changes are continuity events, planned and recorded, not incidental.

### Hair Standards for the Vale System

- Hair is part of the constant body and does not change with fronting. Its length, texture, and color are the same whichever member is present.
- Fronting is never signaled by a change of hair. Any recognition the hair contributes is the recognition of the one shared body, not of a particular member.

### Hair Standards for the Recurring Cast

- Hair honors each character's ethnicity as established in the Character Identity Map and is groomed for a working crew.
- For Talia Rusk, hair is styled as an individual's practical choice and must not be used to imply a specific nation, culture, or ceremony, since those are deliberately undeveloped.
- For Mira Ives, hair is a child's hair, and its styling is kept light and non-fixed, consistent with a young character still growing into her own presentation.
- Any character may carry a personal hair choice that becomes a recognition cue, provided that cue is also expressed non-visually through voice, movement, or environmental sound.

## Body Shape Standards

Bodies in Resonance are varied, honest, and specific. The cast should look like a real community rather than a set of variations on one physique.

- Real range. The cast spans a genuine range of builds, weights, and proportions. No single body type is the default, and no character is reshaped toward an idealized standard.
- Profession informs the body without dictating it. Physically demanding roles may show corresponding strength or wear, but bodies are individual and never reduced to their jobs.
- Age and life show honestly. Bodies carry the marks of age, work, and history. Scars established in canon are preserved; new marks are treated as continuity decisions.
- Disability is part of the body, drawn truthfully. Bodies with limb differences, mobility aids, and other disabilities are designed as whole and capable, never as incomplete versions of an able body.

### Body Shape Standards for the Vale System

- Height and build are part of the constant body and never change with fronting. The physical frame is one frame.
- What changes with fronting is carriage, not shape: posture, stance, the way weight is held, the readiness or ease in the shoulders. These are animation layers on one unchanging build, and are a primary channel of member recognition alongside voice and gait.

### Body Shape Standards for Characters With Mobility and Limb Differences

- Talia Rusk is a lower-limb amputee who uses a prosthetic limb. Her body is designed as strong and capable, and the prosthetic is an ordinary, well-fitted part of her, addressed further under accessibility devices.
- Doctor Soren Qadir is a wheelchair user with a repulsor-assisted chair and a manual backup. His seated posture, reach, and working stance are authored as fully expressive; the chair is part of his silhouette and is designed as personal equipment rather than a medical prop.
- Rowan Ives lives with chronic pain and arthritis, which may show in how he holds and moves his body across a shift. His build is that of an experienced maintenance worker, and his adaptations are competence, not limitation.

## Height Range Standards

Height contributes to silhouette recognition and to how characters relate in a shot. It is treated as a stable, individual fact per character rather than a variable to be adjusted for staging.

- Stable per character. Each character has a consistent height that does not change between chapters, professions, or shots. Continuity of relative height between characters is maintained across all scenes.
- A believable spread. The cast covers a natural range of heights so that groups read as a real community and silhouettes remain distinguishable. Children and adults are proportioned truthfully for their ages.
- Seated and standing eye lines. For wheelchair users and for scenes where characters sit, work, or crouch, camera and staging respect natural eye lines rather than always composing to a standing norm.

### Height Range Standards for the Vale System

- The body's height is constant and never changes with fronting. Whichever member is present, the eye line and frame are the same.
- Apparent height may read subtly differently through posture, since one member may stand more squarely and another more at ease, but the underlying height never changes. This is carriage, not stature.

### Height Range Standards for Seated and Standing Presentation

- Doctor Soren Qadir's primary eye line is a seated eye line, and scenes with him are staged so that conversation and framing meet him naturally rather than looking down at him.
- Mira Ives is a child, and her height is proportioned for a child and staged with the same respect, without infantilizing framing.

## Age Presentation Standards

Age is presented honestly and specifically, spanning at least a child and a range of adult life stages across the recurring cast.

- Truthful aging. Skin, hair, posture, and movement reflect a character's life stage. Older characters are not smoothed toward youth, and younger characters are not aged up for gravitas.
- Children are children. Mira Ives is designed, animated, and framed as a real child, with a child's proportions, energy, and expression, and with the dignity afforded any character.
- Aging across a campaign. Over a long franchise, characters may visibly age. Such changes are planned continuity events and are matched by corresponding changes in voice and movement.

### Age Presentation Standards for Mira Ives

- Mira is a habitation-deck child, and her visual design keeps her gender expression light and non-fixed, consistent with a young genderfluid character still coming to understand this for herself.
- Her clothing, hair, and accessories are chosen to leave room for change and are never locked into a single fixed presentation. This openness is a deliberate, respectful design stance, not an omission.

## Clothing Philosophy Standards

Clothing in Resonance is functional, personal, and legible. It tells the player who a person is, what they do, and how they are doing, and it does so in a way that always has a non-visual counterpart in sound and text.

- Function first. Clothing suits a working spacefaring community. It protects, carries tools, and endures long shifts before it decorates.
- Personal within the practical. Within functional norms, characters express themselves through fit, wear, small personalizations, and the way they wear the same garments differently. These personal choices are recognition cues and are kept consistent.
- Legible states. Clothing communicates duty status, department, and situation, and every such state has a matching audio and text signal so that the same information reaches a player who is not looking.
- Cultural respect. Any culturally specific clothing is grounded in real, respectful reference and consultation. For Talia Rusk, no clothing may imply a specific nation or ceremony, because her nation and culture are deliberately undeveloped.

### Clothing Philosophy and Its Non-Visual Equivalent

- Fabric and gear have sound. Every garment and worn item is paired with its own cloth and equipment audio, so that the rustle of a coat, the creak of a harness, or the whisper of a sleeve projector identifies a person and a state without a single pixel.
- Duty status is announced in parallel. When clothing signals on-duty, off-duty, or emergency readiness, that same state is available through audio cues and text so the player is never dependent on seeing the change.

## Uniform Standards

Uniforms organize the crew, communicate profession and role, and carry accessibility as standard equipment rather than as special issue. There are eight professions, and each has a clear, consistent uniform language.

- The eight professions. Uniform language distinguishes Medic, Scientist, Engineer, Maintenance, Security, Commissioned Officer, Non-Commissioned Officer, and Civilian. Each profession is legible at a glance and, equally, through its parallel audio and text signals.
- Rank and role. Insignia communicate rank and role clearly and consistently, and are supported by non-visual equivalents such as how a character is addressed, their command authority in a scene, and their identifiers in ship systems.
- Accessibility is standard equipment. No accessibility feature built into a uniform is treated as special or as requiring permission. Seizure-safe design, tactile markings, and interface customizations are ordinary parts of standard issue that crew adjust to suit themselves.
- Fit for every body. Uniforms are designed to fit the full range of bodies in the cast, including seated wear for wheelchair users, compatibility with prosthetics, and compatibility with worn devices such as sleeve projectors and navigation helmets.

### Uniform Standards for the Vale System

- The uniform design and rank insignia are part of the constant body and never change with fronting. The Vale System holds one office of Captain in the Commissioned Officer track, and the command authority the body carries is constant.
- Personal identifiers update automatically when a member fronts, as ordinary ship operations rather than ceremony. These include the uniform name tape, the digital identification badge, the wrist display, the personal communicator profile, office and terminal displays, and command terminal login and voice authentication.
- These updates are treated by the crew as completely ordinary. They are shown, when shown at all, as normal ship behavior, and the crew remark on them only when directly relevant. The updates are never staged as a transformation, and the body is never relabeled as a different rank or office.

### Uniform Standards for Each Profession

- Medic uniforms read as clinical and calm and support Doctor Samira Kade's sleeve projector as standard equipment.
- Scientist uniforms read as field-and-lab practical and support seated wear for Doctor Soren Qadir.
- Engineer uniforms read as heavy-duty and tool-ready and support Mara Chen's navigation helmet as standard equipment.
- Maintenance uniforms read as rugged and utility-forward, suited to Rowan Ives's work in the hidden body of the ship, with adaptations for chronic pain and arthritis treated as ordinary.
- Security uniforms read as protective and ready and accommodate Talia Rusk's prosthetic without special issue.
- Commissioned Officer uniforms read as command attire and carry the Vale System's constant insignia and authority.
- Non-Commissioned Officer uniforms read as the working backbone of operations and suit Jonah Briggs's logistics role, within the ship's standard seizure-safe lighting design.
- Civilian clothing is addressed as its own standard below and covers community members such as Mira Ives.

## Civilian Clothing Standards

Civilians are full members of the community, and civilian clothing is designed with the same care as any uniform. Civilian is one of the eight professions and reads clearly as belonging to the ship's community.

- Everyday individuality. Civilian clothing is more personal and varied than uniformed wear and is a primary way civilians express who they are.
- Belonging, not lesser status. Civilian clothing communicates full membership in the community rather than a visitor or outsider status.
- Practical for shipboard life. Even at its most personal, civilian clothing suits life aboard a working vessel.

### Civilian Clothing Standards for Mira Ives

- Mira's clothing is a child's clothing, comfortable, practical, and expressive, and it is kept light and non-fixed with respect to gender, leaving room for her to grow into her own presentation.
- Her clothing accommodates her visible neural-interface augmentative and alternative communication system as an ordinary, worn part of her daily life, never as a clinical attachment.
- Sensory comfort is a design consideration for Mira as an autistic child, and clothing choices favor comfort and predictability where that supports her.

## Equipment Standards

Equipment is personal, well-used, and legible. Tools and worn gear are among the strongest recognition cues in the game, and each has a matching sound so that equipment identifies a person by ear as reliably as by eye.

- Personal and worn-in. Equipment shows use and ownership. A character's tools look like their tools, arranged and worn the way that person keeps them.
- Legible function. What a piece of equipment does is readable from its form, and its use is always paired with a distinctive sound.
- Recognition through gear. The specific equipment a character carries and how they carry it is a consistent recognition cue maintained across chapters.

### Equipment Standards for Mara Chen

- Mara Chen uses an engineering helmet whose navigation faceplate turns visual data into audio and tactile cues. The helmet is designed as ordinary, expert engineering equipment, personal to her and well-used, never as a marker of limitation.
- The helmet and faceplate are a strong visual recognition cue and are matched by their own operating sound and by Mara's characteristic listening gait, so that she is recognizable by ear before she is seen.

### Equipment Standards for Doctor Samira Kade

- Samira Kade communicates through a sleeve projector that shows sign or live text. The projector is designed as elegant, reliable, standard medical-officer equipment worn as part of her uniform.
- Its soft mechanical whisper is a signature sound of her presence in a bay and is preserved as a recognition cue in parallel with its visual form.

### Equipment Standards for the Working Cast

- Rowan Ives carries maintenance tools arranged for someone who knows the hidden body of the ship intimately and who has redesigned his work around chronic pain and arthritis; his tools and adaptations are shown as expertise.
- Talia Rusk carries security equipment suited to a steady protector, fitted to work with her prosthetic without special accommodation being remarked upon.
- Jonah Briggs carries logistics and operations equipment suited to the calm voice that reads out priorities.
- Doctor Soren Qadir carries research equipment configured for use from his chair, integrated into his working setup rather than added onto it.

## Accessibility Device Standards

Accessibility technology is standard equipment aboard human vessels, and no accessibility device is ever treated as special equipment or as requiring permission to use. Devices are personal, dignified, and designed with the same craft as any other part of a character.

- Ordinary, not exceptional. Devices are part of daily life and daily competence. Crew customize their interfaces rather than requesting permission to use them.
- Personal and specific. Each device belongs to its user and reflects that person's taste and needs, and it is maintained as a consistent recognition cue.
- Designed as capability. Devices are drawn as enabling and expert, never as objects of pity, and never as the whole of a character.
- Always paired with sound and touch. Each device has a characteristic sound and, where relevant, a tactile behavior, so a player who is not looking still perceives the device and its user.

### Accessibility Device Standards for Mara Chen

- Mara's navigation helmet and faceplate convert visual data into audio and tactile cues and are her ordinary means of navigating a world she reads by sound, vibration, and touch. The device is expert engineering equipment and a recognition cue in both image and sound.

### Accessibility Device Standards for Doctor Samira Kade

- Samira's sleeve projector displays sign or live text and is her ordinary means of communication as a Deaf medical officer. It is standard, elegant equipment whose soft whisper is a signature of her presence.

### Accessibility Device Standards for Talia Rusk

- Talia's prosthetic limb is a well-fitted, ordinary part of her body, designed as capable and personal. Its distinctive gait sound is a recognition cue and is authored with dignity. No aspect of the prosthetic implies a specific nation or culture, since those are deliberately undeveloped.

### Accessibility Device Standards for Doctor Soren Qadir

- Soren's repulsor-assisted chair, with its manual backup, is his personal equipment and part of his silhouette. It is designed as capable and expert, and its characteristic sound is a recognition cue. Its manual backup is part of the same honest design, present because real equipment has fallbacks.

### Accessibility Device Standards for Mira Ives

- Mira's visible neural-interface augmentative and alternative communication system is her ordinary voice. It is designed as an everyday, worn part of a child's life, and its synthesized voice and interface are recognition cues treated with full respect as her own way of speaking.

### Accessibility Device Standards for Jonah Briggs

- Jonah lives with epilepsy, and the ship's seizure-safe lighting is standard design rather than a special accommodation. His accessibility need shapes the environment as ordinary infrastructure, and this is reflected in lighting standards that apply to everyone.

## Department Color Language Standards

Color helps organize the crew by profession and situation, but in a sound-first, accessibility-first game color is never the sole carrier of any meaning. Every use of color has a redundant non-color equivalent.

- Redundant by rule. Any information conveyed by color is also conveyed by shape, pattern, text, position, sound, or haptics. No player may be required to distinguish colors to understand a department, status, or alert.
- Color-vision safe. Department and status palettes are chosen and tested to remain distinguishable across common color-vision differences, and they never rely on red-green contrast alone.
- Consistent meaning. A given color means the same thing wherever it appears, so that color, once learned, is reliable.
- Contrast and legibility. Colors are chosen for strong contrast and legibility in the ship's practical lighting, supporting low-vision players.

### Department Color Language for the Eight Professions

- Each of the eight professions, Medic, Scientist, Engineer, Maintenance, Security, Commissioned Officer, Non-Commissioned Officer, and Civilian, has a distinct color association that is always reinforced by a distinct shape or insignia and by non-visual role signals.
- Because Civilian is a full profession in the cast, civilians have their own clear color-and-shape identity of belonging rather than an absence of one.

### Department Color Language and Its Non-Visual Equivalent

- Department identity reaches players who do not use color through insignia shape, uniform silhouette, how a character is addressed, and the audio and text signals of their role.
- Status and alert states expressed in color are always mirrored by distinct sounds, distinct haptic patterns, and captions, consistent with the game's rule that important information never depends on sound alone and, equally, never depends on color alone.

## Silhouette Recognition Standards

Silhouette is a primary visual recognition channel and is designed so that each character is identifiable from outline alone, in low light, at distance, and in motion. Silhouette recognition is always backed by an audio recognition equivalent.

- Distinct outlines. Each character has a distinct silhouette built from build, posture, hair, worn equipment, and mobility aids. No two recurring characters should be confusable in outline.
- Motion signatures. Characters are recognizable by how they move as well as how they stand. Gait, wheel motion, and gesture rhythm are part of the silhouette in motion.
- Equipment as outline. Worn devices such as a navigation helmet, a sleeve projector, or a chair are deliberate, consistent parts of a silhouette.
- Always matched by sound. Every silhouette recognition cue has a parallel audio cue, so the same person is identifiable by ear, consistent with recognition through observation.

### Silhouette Recognition for the Vale System

- The shared body has one silhouette in its stable elements: build, height, hair, uniform, and worn equipment do not change with fronting.
- Within that one body, fronting changes carriage, stance, gait, and gesture, and these motion and posture signatures are how the silhouette distinguishes the current member. The distinction is legible in motion and posture, and never through a change to the body's fixed outline and never through visual relabeling.
- Silhouette alone is never sufficient to identify the current member; it works together with voice, gait, gesture, and priorities, and the audio equivalents carry equal weight.

### Silhouette Recognition for Characters With Devices and Aids

- Mara Chen's navigation helmet gives her a distinct standing and moving outline, matched by her listening gait and the helmet's sound.
- Doctor Samira Kade's sleeve projector and signing posture give her a distinct outline, matched by the projector's whisper.
- Doctor Soren Qadir's chair gives him a distinct seated silhouette and motion signature, matched by the repulsor chair's sound.
- Talia Rusk's stance and prosthetic give her a distinct outline and gait, matched by her prosthetic's gait sound.
- Mira Ives's child proportions, her worn communication system, and her uneven footfalls give her a distinct silhouette in motion, matched by her footstep pattern and her communication system's voice.

## Emergency Presentation Standards

In emergencies, characters look and move differently, and every recurring character has an emergency presentation that is legible visually and, in parallel, through sound and text. Emergency states must never be communicated by a single channel.

- Readable urgency. Posture, pace, gear, and expression shift to communicate urgency clearly, and the same urgency is carried by voice, breathing, movement sound, and alert audio.
- In character under pressure. Each character's emergency presentation is specific to who they are and how they respond, not a generic panic state.
- Devices under stress. Accessibility devices continue to function and to be shown as reliable equipment in an emergency; they are never dropped from the design to heighten drama.
- Safety-first infrastructure. Emergency lighting and alerts follow accessibility standards, including seizure-safe design, and always pair color and light with sound, haptics, and captions.

### Emergency Presentation for the Vale System

- Which member is fronting in an emergency is meaningful and is part of the game. Emergencies commonly bring Naomi forward for command and coordination, and Gideon forward when systems are failing, consistent with how fronting works as gameplay.
- The change of fronting member in a crisis is recognized through voice, posture, gait, command style, and priorities, and through the ordinary automatic update of personal identifiers, never through a visual transformation of the constant body.
- The body's constant elements, including uniform, insignia, and command authority, remain constant throughout any emergency.

### Emergency Presentation for the Cast Under Pressure

- Doctor Samira Kade continues to communicate through sight, vibration, and her sleeve projector under pressure, and emergency staging ensures she can see what she needs to read a chaotic room.
- Mara Chen continues to navigate by sound, vibration, and touch through her helmet, and emergencies are designed so that acoustic information remains available to her.
- Doctor Soren Qadir responds as the persistent investigator who refuses to leave until his team is away, and his chair is shown as fully reliable in a crisis.
- Talia Rusk responds as the steady protector inclined to put others ahead of herself, and her prosthetic is shown as reliable under stress.
- Jonah Briggs remains the calm voice reading out priorities, within the ship's seizure-safe emergency lighting.
- Mira Ives, as a child, is protected within the community, and her communication system remains her reliable voice in an emergency.

## Off-Duty Presentation Standards

Off duty, characters relax their presentation, and this rest state is a meaningful part of who they are. Off-duty presentation is legible visually and, in parallel, through changes in movement sound, environment, and behavior.

- The person beyond the job. Off-duty clothing, posture, and pace reveal personality beyond profession and deepen the player's recognition of each character.
- Continuity of self. Stable facts do not change off duty. The body, hair, height, scars, and needed accessibility devices remain, while uniform and duty gear give way to personal choices.
- Devices remain. Accessibility devices are present off duty because they are part of daily life, though a character may use them more casually when at rest.
- Legible rest. The shift from on-duty to off-duty reads through relaxed silhouette and pace and is matched by softer movement sounds, quieter equipment, and calmer environmental signatures.

### Off-Duty Presentation for the Vale System

- Off duty, fronting still follows the ordinary rhythm of who best meets the moment, and Elian commonly meets ordinary community time, consistent with how the members front.
- The body's constant elements do not change off duty. What changes is presentation and priority, recognized through the same behavioral, vocal, and postural signals as always, with personal identifiers updating automatically as ordinary ship operations.

### Off-Duty Presentation for Mira Ives

- Off duty, Mira is simply a child at home aboard the ship, and her presentation stays light, comfortable, and non-fixed with respect to gender, consistent with a young character still growing into her own understanding.
- Her communication system remains her ordinary voice at rest, and her off-duty life is shown with the same dignity as any character's.

## Cross-Reference and Authority for Visual Design

This document is one part of the Player Identity and Character Presentation Bible and does not stand alone.

- The Canon Rules document and the Character Identity Map are the authorities for who each character is, and they govern wherever anything here appears to differ.
- The Audio Identity standards are the companion to this document, and every visual standard here assumes the matching non-visual standard defined there.
- The Accessibility Bible governs accessibility as standard equipment, seizure-safe design, color-independent and sound-independent information, and the tactile language, and no visual standard may violate it.
- The Audio Bible governs sound as the primary renderer, and visual design must always agree with the world the audio establishes.

## Open Development Items for Visual Design

Two items are deliberately left open in the Character Identity Map and are carried here so that visual design respects that openness rather than closing it prematurely.

- Talia Rusk is the Two-Spirit character, and Two-Spirit is a culturally specific Indigenous identity. Her nation, culture, and community are to be developed thoughtfully, ideally with Indigenous cultural consultation. Until then, no clothing, ornament, hairstyle, insignia, or facial marking may imply a specific nation, culture, or ceremony for her, and she is designed with respect for that specificity and without a placeholder.
- Mira Ives is a child who is genderfluid and uses she and they, and she is still growing into her own understanding. Her visual presentation, including clothing, hair, and accessories, is kept light and non-fixed and is open to development as the character and the story mature.
