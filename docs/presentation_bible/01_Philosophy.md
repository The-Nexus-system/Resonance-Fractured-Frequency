# Philosophy of Character Presentation

This document explains why Resonance presents people the way it does. It is the philosophical foundation of the Player Identity and Character Presentation Bible, and it stands beneath every profile, animation brief, audio spec, and line of dialogue that follows. Where later documents record what a character looks like, sounds like, and moves like, this document records why those choices are made and what they must always protect.

This philosophy does not create canon. It applies the canon. The Canon Rules document is the authority on every rule cited here, and the Character Identity Map is the locked source for each recurring character's profession, ethnicity, gender, pronouns, and disability. The Project Constitution has authority over both. Where anything in this document appears to differ from those sources, those sources govern and this document is corrected.

The purpose of this document is to give artists, animators, user-interface designers, audio designers, writers, and gameplay programmers a shared way of thinking, so that a decision made in an animation studio and a decision made in a dialogue script and a decision made in a sound library all point in the same direction without needing to be coordinated line by line. When the philosophy is understood, the details become consistent almost on their own.

## Why Resonance Presents People the Way It Does

Resonance is a sound-first, accessibility-first game, and its approach to people follows from that identity. The way a character is presented is not a cosmetic layer applied over a functional design. It is part of how the game is played, part of how the world is understood, and part of how the player comes to care about anyone at all.

### The Sound-First Premise Behind Presentation

Audio is gameplay in Resonance, and this changes what character presentation means. A character is not primarily a face and a silhouette that a player looks at; a character is a voice, a breathing pattern, a walking rhythm, a set of environmental sounds, and a way of communicating, any of which may reach the player before an image does or without an image at all. Presentation in this game is therefore designed to be legible by ear first, and legible by sight, touch, and text as equal partners rather than as fallbacks.

This premise means that every recurring character must be recognisable through more than appearance. It also means that the audio identity of a character is not decoration added after the visual design is finished. Voice, accent, speech rhythm, movement sound, and environmental sound are authored as primary attributes of who a person is, and the Audio Bible owns the detail of how those attributes are produced.

### Presentation as a Promise to the Player

The canon treats each rule as a promise the game keeps to its players. Presentation is one of those promises. It promises that people will be shown as whole, that they will be recognisable through attention rather than through labels, that no one aboard will be reduced to a single trait, and that a player who cannot see, cannot hear, or moves differently will still meet everyone fully. Every presentation choice is measured against whether it keeps that promise.

### Presentation Serving the Central Themes

Every system in Resonance is meant to reinforce the central themes of community, accessibility, curiosity, adaptation, shared knowledge, and hope. Character presentation is a system in this sense. A character is presented so that meeting them builds community, so that their accessibility is ordinary, so that learning them rewards curiosity, so that their way of adapting is visible, so that their expertise can be shared, and so that their presence adds to the hope the game is built on. A presentation choice that reinforces none of these themes should be reconsidered.

## Recognition Through Observation

The single most important principle in this document is that people become known through observation rather than through labels. The canon states that recurring characters become recognisable naturally, that the interface should rarely identify a recurring character, and that the world teaches the player who people are. Everything about presentation is built to make that possible.

### The Signals the Player Learns To Read

The canon names the signals through which the player gradually learns people, and presentation work exists to make each of these signals distinct and consistent for every recurring character.

- Voice and speech rhythm, so that a person can be known the moment they speak.
- Movement and body language, so that a person can be known by how they cross a room.
- Professional habits and daily routines, so that a person can be known by what they are doing and how they do it.
- Environmental sounds, so that a person can be known by the sounds that travel with them or gather around their work.
- Communication style, so that a person can be known by how they reach others, whether in speech, sign, text, or an alternative system.
- Relationships with others, so that a person can be known by how the crew treats them and how they treat the crew.

Presentation must make these signals reliable. A recurring character who sounds one way in one scene and another way in the next, for no reason internal to the character, breaks the very skill the game asks the player to build.

### Why the Interface Stays Quiet

The interface rarely names a recurring character because naming is the opposite of the skill the game is teaching. When a label appears over a person, the player stops reading the person and reads the label instead. The canon reserves the interface for the player's own tools and choices, and leaves the teaching of people to the world. Presentation supports this by making people knowable without a label, so that the quiet interface is a feature rather than a gap.

This restraint is not a refusal of information. Accessibility requires that players who need identification can obtain it; the Accessibility Bible owns those options. The principle is that identification is available to the player who asks and never imposed on the player who is learning, and that important information is never carried by a single channel.

### Understanding Earned Separately From History

The canon separates the history the player's actions create from the understanding the player's observations create, and notes that the two are not always acquired at the same time. Presentation lives on the understanding side. A player may work beside a person for hours and only later come to recognise them fully, and that gradual arrival is intended. Presentation is designed to reward the patient, repeated attention through which understanding is earned, rather than to hand understanding over at first contact.

## Accessibility as Presentation

Accessibility in Resonance is foundational, not an overlay, and this document treats accessibility as part of who a character is. A character's accessibility devices, communication method, and way of moving through the world are attributes of that person in the same way that a voice or a gait is, and they are presented with the same care and the same ordinariness.

### Accessibility Is Standard Equipment, Not Special Equipment

The canon states that no accessibility feature is special equipment, that accessibility technology is standard equipment aboard human vessels, and that crew members customise interfaces rather than requesting permission to use them. Presentation follows this exactly. A character's accessibility equipment is shown as ordinary working gear, never as a marker of struggle, never as a device the character must justify, and never as a spectacle. It is presented the way any competent professional's tools are presented, because that is what it is.

### Accessibility Devices as Part of Identity

The Character Identity Map records specific accessibility realities for recurring characters, and each is treated as part of that person's presentation rather than as an add-on. Mara Chen navigates by sound, vibration, and touch and uses an engineering helmet whose navigation faceplate turns visual data into audio and tactile cues, and this is presented as central to how she works and how she is recognised. Doctor Samira Kade is Deaf, reads a room by sight and vibration, and communicates through a sleeve projector that shows sign or live text, and the soft mechanical sound of that projector is part of how her presence is known. Rowan Ives lives with chronic pain and arthritis and has redesigned his work around his condition, which is presented as expertise and adaptation rather than limitation. Mira Ives communicates through a visible neural-interface augmentative and alternative communication system, presented as an ordinary and complete way of speaking. Chief Petty Officer Jonah Briggs lives with epilepsy, and the ship's seizure-safe lighting is standard design rather than an accommodation made for him. Talia Rusk is a lower-limb amputee who uses a prosthetic limb, presented as part of how she moves and works. Doctor Soren Qadir is a wheelchair user with a repulsor-assisted chair and a manual backup, presented as part of how he moves through the ship and how his presence sounds.

### Disability Shapes Characters Without Defining Them

The canon states that disability shapes perspective, problem solving, communication, and relationships, and never defines a character's entire identity. Presentation holds both halves of this rule at once. A character's disability is never hidden, minimised, or treated as shameful, because it genuinely shapes who they are and how they work. At the same time, a character is never reduced to their disability, because they are a complete person with history, personality, strengths, flaws, relationships, goals, and growth. The craft goal is to present disability as one true and meaningful part of a whole person.

### Presentation That Is Legible Through Every Channel

Because important information may never depend on a single sense, presentation is authored to be legible through every channel. A character who is recognisable by voice must also be recognisable by movement and by environmental sound for a player who does not use audio, and by consistent visual design for a player who does not use text, and by described audio for a player who does not use sight. The blind-friendly, audio-first description that the canon requires for every recurring character is not a translation of the visual design; it is a first-class presentation of the character in its own right.

## Representation Philosophy

The recurring cast intentionally reflects the breadth of humanity, and representation in Resonance is a design requirement rather than a decorative afterthought. This document treats representation as a matter of craft and honesty, and it defines how representation is pursued without ever becoming tokenistic.

### Representation Informs Identity and Never Replaces It

The canon states that representation informs identity and never replaces it, and that no recurring character exists solely to satisfy representation, accessibility, gameplay, or narrative requirements. Presentation begins from the person and lets representation be one true aspect of who they are. An artist or writer working from a profile should be able to describe a character fully in terms of personality, relationships, profession, values, strengths, flaws, goals, and growth before their identity facts are ever mentioned, and the identity facts should then sit naturally among those things rather than standing in for them.

### The Breadth the Cast Is Built To Reflect

The Character Identity Map records that the eight recurring human bodies fill the eight ethnic slots the canon names, that every gender identity named in the canon appears at least once, and that the eight recurring human characters correspond one to each of the eight professions. Presentation honours this structure by making each character specific rather than representative. A character is never presented as the Asian character or the non-binary character or the wheelchair-using character in general; each is presented as one particular person who happens to be all of the true things the map records.

### Diversity Presented as Ordinary

The canon states that no race, ethnicity, disability, communication style, or gender identity is treated as the default, and that human diversity is presented as an ordinary part of civilisation. Presentation carries this by refusing to mark difference as remarkable. The crew do not comment on one another's identities as unusual, do not explain one another to the player, and do not treat any way of being human as the baseline against which others are measured. The ordinariness is the point, and it is achieved by presenting every character with equal completeness and equal respect.

### Future Characters Continue the Breadth

The canon states that future recurring characters continue this broad, global representation, and that the goal is not to check demographic boxes but to portray a diverse range of authentic human experiences through fully realised characters. Presentation standards therefore apply to characters not yet written as much as to those already fixed. Any new recurring character is held to the same character-first standard, and the breadth of the cast is maintained by design rather than by accident.

## Character-First Design

Every recurring character in Resonance is a complete person, and character-first design is the working method that keeps this true. It orders the design so that who a person is comes before what a person represents, and it gives every discipline a way to check that the order held.

### Building the Whole Person Before the Details

The canon states that every recurring character has their own history, personality, strengths, flaws, relationships, goals, fears, profession, and growth. Character-first design asks each discipline to build from that whole. A voice is designed for a particular temperament, a gait for a particular history in the body, a workspace sound for a particular set of professional habits. When the whole person leads, the presentation details cohere; when a detail leads, the person tends to fragment into traits.

### No Character Is Only a Function

The canon states that no character exists solely to satisfy representation, accessibility, gameplay, or narrative requirements, and that no primary crew member ever becomes obsolete. Presentation extends this. A character is never presented as only a role, only a device, or only a plot function. Every recurring character remains a full presence throughout the campaign, with meaningful opportunities to contribute according to their expertise, and their presentation keeps them vivid even in chapters where their role is small.

### The Player Is Never the Smartest Person in the Room

The canon states that the player is never the smartest person in the room, that every crew member possesses expertise unavailable to the player, and that the player's greatest strength is bringing expertise together. Presentation supports this by giving each recurring character visible, audible, and lived-in mastery. A character should read as someone who knows something the player does not, and their competence should be part of how they are presented, so that meeting them feels like meeting a teacher and a colleague rather than a helper or a subordinate.

### Everyone Teaches and Everyone Keeps Learning

The canon states that every crew member teaches, every crew member continues learning, and no crew member knows everything. Presentation shows both the teaching and the learning. A character is presented with the confidence of their expertise and the openness of someone still growing, and neither is dropped in favour of the other. This keeps characters human rather than making them either flawless authorities or perpetual novices.

## Avoiding Stereotypes

Character-first design and the ordinariness of diversity are the strongest defences against stereotype, but avoiding stereotype also requires specific, deliberate craft. This document names the traps and the disciplines that keep presentation clear of them.

### Specificity as the Antidote to Stereotype

Stereotype is generality standing in for a person. The antidote is specificity. A character presented with particular habits, particular relationships, particular strengths and flaws, and a particular way of working cannot easily collapse into a type, because the specifics contradict the generality. Presentation work should always prefer the specific choice that belongs to this one person over the general choice that belongs to a category.

### Refusing the Single Story

A character must never be presented as the whole of any group they belong to. Because the cast is built so that no identity is the default, no single character carries the weight of representing an entire people, disability, or gender, and presentation should never imply otherwise. Each character is one person among many possible people, and their particular way of being is theirs rather than a definition of their category.

### Care With Trait Bundling

Stereotype often arrives as a bundle, where one true fact about a person is used to predict a set of unrelated traits. Presentation resists this by deriving a character's traits from their personality and history rather than from their identity facts. A character's ethnicity does not determine their temperament, their gender does not determine their profession, and their disability does not determine their mood, and presentation should actively break any accidental correlation that would suggest otherwise.

### Consultation Where Specificity Is Owed

Some specificity is not the studio's to invent. Talia Rusk is the Two-Spirit character, and Two-Spirit is a culturally specific Indigenous identity rather than a generic gender label. Her nation, culture, and community are deliberately left to be developed thoughtfully, ideally with Indigenous cultural consultation, and are not invented casually. Until that development happens, Talia is presented with respect for that specificity and without a placeholder nation, language, or ceremony. Avoiding stereotype here means declining to fabricate, and treating the open question as a mark of respect rather than a gap to be filled.

## Respectful Portrayal Across Disability

Disability is foundational to Resonance, and characters experience disability in many different ways. This section gathers the disability-specific presentation principles so that every discipline applies them consistently.

### Disability Presented as Perspective and Method

The canon states that disability shapes perspective, problem solving, communication, and relationships. Presentation shows this as method rather than as hardship. Mara Chen reads systems as sounds and navigates by sound, vibration, and touch, which is presented as a way of knowing the ship that others lack. Rowan Ives has redesigned his work around chronic pain and arthritis, which is presented as hard-won expertise in the ship's hidden body. A character's disability is shown shaping how they do excellent work, not preventing them from doing it.

### Equipment Shown as Competence

Accessibility equipment is presented as the tools of a competent professional. Mara Chen's engineering helmet, Samira Kade's sleeve projector, Mira Ives's neural-interface communication system, Talia Rusk's prosthetic limb, and Soren Qadir's repulsor-assisted chair with its manual backup are all presented as ordinary, reliable, well-used gear. They are never presented as sad, inspiring, futuristic novelties, or objects of curiosity; they are presented the way any expert's instruments are presented.

### Avoiding the Inspiration and Pity Frames

A character is never presented so that their disability exists to inspire the player or to invite the player's pity. The crew do not marvel at a colleague for working while disabled, and the game does not frame disability as a burden overcome for the player's emotional benefit. Presentation keeps disability ordinary, and ordinariness is itself the respect.

### The World Adapts to People

The canon states that the world adapts to people and people are never required to adapt to exclusion, and that accessibility is standard equipment rather than special permission. Presentation shows an environment already built for its people. Seizure-safe lighting is standard design rather than an accommodation for Jonah Briggs; accessible routes, controls, and communication systems are simply how the ship is. A character never has to ask the world to make room for them, because the world already has.

## Respectful Portrayal of Plurality in the Vale System

The Vale System requires its own presentation principles because it is unlike any other character in the cast. The canon is precise about what the Vale System is, and presentation must be equally precise, because the difference between honouring this portrayal and mishandling it lives entirely in the details.

### One Shared Body Inhabited by Four Members

The canon states that the Vale System is one person, a single shared body inhabited by four recurring members who front in turn, and that the members are Captain Elian, Captain Naomi, Captain Gideon, and Captain Iris of the Vale System. The Character Identity Map fixes their shared reality: the profession is Command in the Commissioned Officer track, the ethnicity is Native mixed-heritage shared by the one body, and the disability is dissociative identity disorder, with the four members sharing one body and the single office of Captain. Presentation must always begin from this unity of body and this plurality of self.

### Never Four People and Never a Single Merged Name

The canon states that the Vale System is never written as four separate people and never as a single character named Captain Mara Vale. Presentation obeys both halves without exception. The four members are distinct selves and must never be flattened into one generic captain, and they are one body and one system and must never be depicted as four bodies moving independently. There is no character named Mara Vale, and Mara Chen, who shares no relation to the Vale System, is the blind engineer and not a member of the system. These are hard constraints, and any presentation that blurs them is a failure.

### What Stays Constant Across Fronting

The canon fixes what never changes whichever member is fronting, and presentation treats this list as the physical anchor of the character.

- The shared body, its height, and its build.
- The face, hair, eye colour, and skin tone.
- Physical scars and medical history.
- Accessibility equipment.
- Uniform design and rank insignia.
- Department affiliation.
- The command authority the body carries.
- The history the crew has built with the Vale System.

Because the body is constant, presentation never transforms it. The Vale System is not recognised by a change in appearance, and no visual costume change signals a switch.

### What Changes Naturally With the Member Fronting

The canon fixes what changes naturally with whoever is fronting, and presentation treats this list as the expressive surface through which each member is known.

- Voice, accent, vocabulary, and speech rhythm.
- Breathing pattern.
- Facial expression and eye focus.
- Head position, standing posture, and resting posture.
- Walking rhythm and preferred stance.
- Hand placement and gesture vocabulary.
- Leadership philosophy and decision-making style.
- Professional priorities and attention focus.
- Emotional expression and sense of humour.
- Relationships with the crew.
- Preferred routines, communication style, and favourite places aboard.
- The name the member answers to.

Presentation makes each member unmistakable through these changes, so that no two members ever feel interchangeable and the player comes to recognise each member before they speak.

### Recognising Each Member as a Distinct Self

The four members carry distinct genders and pronouns, which the crew, friends, and family use as an ordinary part of daily life. Elian is genderfluid, with pronouns determined by Elian's current identity and presentation, and meets ordinary mornings and community leadership. Naomi is non-binary and uses they and them, and meets emergencies, operations, and coordination. Gideon is agender and uses they and them, and meets strategy, navigation, and failing systems. Iris is a demigirl and uses she and they, and meets civilian planning and diplomacy. Presentation gives each member a temperament and a way of leading that fits these roles, so that recognition of the current member emerges through observation rather than explanation.

### Identifiers That Update as Ordinary Operations

The canon states that personal identifiers update naturally when a member fronts, as ordinary ship operations rather than ceremony, including the uniform name tape, the digital identification badge, the wrist display, the personal communicator profile, office and terminal displays, and the command terminal login and voice authentication. Presentation shows these updates as unremarkable ship systems doing their job. The crew treat them as completely ordinary and remark on them only when directly relevant to the conversation. Presentation never turns an identifier update into a dramatic reveal, because the drama would contradict the ordinariness the canon requires.

### Fronting Presented as Purpose

The canon states that fronting is gameplay, that a member comes forward because their knowledge, temperament, or expertise best fits the moment, and never for dramatic effect. Presentation reflects this by making each member's arrival feel motivated by the situation rather than staged for the player. The game almost never announces a switch; the player recognises it through a different voice, posture, command style, humour, set of emotional priorities, and set of interactions with individual crew members, and through the updated name tape and command authentication. Reading the Vale System correctly is a skill the player builds the same way they learn to read anyone else, and presentation is what makes that skill learnable.

## Respectful Portrayal Across Race and Culture

The cast represents a range of ethnic backgrounds by design, and presentation treats race and culture as true parts of people rather than as costumes or shorthand. This section defines how that is done with honesty and care.

### Ethnicity as One True Aspect of a Whole Person

The Character Identity Map records a specific ethnicity for each recurring character, and presentation holds each as one true aspect among many. Talia Rusk is Native, the Vale System is Native mixed-heritage, Rowan Ives is Black, Mira Ives is Black mixed-heritage, Mara Chen is Asian, Soren Qadir is Asian mixed-heritage, Jonah Briggs is White, and Samira Kade is White mixed-heritage. Presentation lets each character's ethnicity be visible and real without letting it explain their personality, profession, or choices.

### Culture Portrayed Without Fabrication

Presentation may show that characters have cultures, histories, and heritages, but it does not invent specific nations, languages, ceremonies, or backstories and present them as settled canon. Where a character's cultural specificity has not been developed, presentation portrays the person with respect and leaves the specifics open rather than filling them with plausible invention. This is especially binding for Talia Rusk, whose Two-Spirit identity is culturally specific and whose nation, culture, and community await thoughtful development with Indigenous cultural consultation.

### Mixed Heritage Presented With Wholeness

Several characters are of mixed heritage, and presentation portrays mixed heritage as a complete belonging rather than as a division. A mixed-heritage character is not presented as caught between worlds or as a sum of fractions; they are presented as one whole person whose heritage is part of who they are. The care here is to avoid the tired framing of mixed identity as conflict, and instead present it as an ordinary and full way of being human.

## Respectful Portrayal Across Gender

The recurring cast intentionally represents a broad spectrum of human gender experiences, and presentation treats gender with the same character-first honesty it applies to everything else. This section gathers the gender-specific principles.

### Gender as One Meaningful Aspect Among Many

The canon states that every recurring character is defined first by personality, relationships, profession, values, strengths, flaws, goals, and growth, and that gender identity is one meaningful aspect of who they are rather than the whole of it. Presentation begins from the person and lets gender sit naturally among their other traits. A character's gender is never their defining feature and never their function in the cast, and presentation should be able to make a character vivid before their gender is ever named.

### Pronouns Reflecting Current Presentation

The canon states that a character whose gender identity changes over time naturally changes the pronouns they use, that crew, friends, and family respect and use a character's current pronouns as an ordinary part of daily life, and that the player learns a character's current pronouns through natural interaction rather than intrusive interface prompts. Presentation honours current pronouns exactly, treats any change as ordinary, and teaches pronouns through dialogue and observation rather than labels. Pronouns are part of a person's present presentation and always reflect how that character currently identifies.

### Genders Held Distinct and Sometimes Overlapping

The canon notes that gender identities are not mutually exclusive when they are not in conflict, that a genderfluid person may experience themselves as a demiboy at one time and a demigirl at another, that a non-binary person may also identify as a demiboy or a demigirl, and that an agender person generally would not identify with a gender, which makes that experience distinct. Presentation respects these relationships. Each member of the Vale System and each recurring character is presented according to their own recorded identity, and presentation neither collapses distinct genders together nor treats overlapping ones as contradictory.

### Two-Spirit Held as Culturally Specific

Presentation treats Two-Spirit as a culturally specific Indigenous identity reserved for Talia Rusk, never as a generic gender label. Because her nation, culture, and community are deliberately undeveloped, presentation portrays Talia with respect for that specificity and does not fabricate the cultural context that would give the term its full meaning. This is a place where the right presentation choice is restraint.

### A Child's Gender Held With a Light Touch

Mira Ives is a child who is genderfluid and uses she and they, still coming to understand this for herself. The canon leaves her pronouns and the way she expresses her gender open to development as the character and the story mature. Presentation treats her gender expression with a light, respectful, non-fixed touch, never pinning down what the character is still discovering, and never making a child's identity a subject of adult certainty.

## Respectful Portrayal Across Age

The cast spans ages, and presentation treats age with the same care as any other aspect of a person. This section addresses the presentation of children and of the range of adult life aboard.

### Children Presented as Full People

Mira Ives is a habitation-deck child, and presentation treats her as a complete person rather than as a device for adult emotion. She is often the first to hear when something aboard is wrong, and her perception, her competence, and her way of communicating through a neural-interface augmentative and alternative communication system are presented as real and valued. A child in Resonance has knowledge, agency, and a point of view, and presentation never reduces a child to a symbol of innocence or vulnerability.

### Childhood Autonomy in Communication and Identity

Because Mira communicates through an augmentative and alternative communication system, presentation treats that system as her full and valid voice, never as a lesser substitute for speech and never as something to be spoken over. Because her gender is still forming, presentation follows the child's own unfolding rather than fixing it. Both principles express the same respect: a child's voice and a child's identity are hers.

### The Range of Adult Life Aboard

The adult crew span a range of ages and stages of life, and presentation gives each the dignity of their stage. Experience is presented as expertise rather than as decline, and the years a character has spent in their work are shown in the confidence of their habits and the depth of their knowledge. Age is one more true aspect of a person, presented without caricature at any end of the range.

## Respectful Portrayal Across Communication

Communication diversity is an ordinary part of the world, and presentation treats every communication method as equally valid. Because Resonance is sound-first and accessibility-first, communication is one of the most important presentation channels in the game.

### Every Communication Method Presented as Equal

The canon names the ways the recurring cast communicates, and presentation gives each equal standing.

- Spoken language.
- Sign language.
- Augmentative and alternative communication.
- Braille literacy.
- Tactile signing.
- Other alternative communication systems.
- Multilingual communication.

Presentation never ranks these, never presents one as the real way and the others as accommodations, and never makes a character apologise for how they communicate. The world adapts to people, and people are never required to adapt to exclusion.

### Communication Method as a Recognition Signal

Communication style is one of the signals through which the player learns people, and presentation makes each character's method a reliable part of how they are known. Samira Kade is known partly by the soft mechanical whisper of her sleeve projector and by her sign and live text; Mira Ives is known partly by her neural-interface communication system; a character who signs is known by the rhythm and presence of their signing. Presentation authors these methods as distinctive and consistent so that they teach the player who is present.

### Communication Legible Across Senses

Because important information must never depend on a single channel, presentation ensures that a character's communication reaches players who use different senses. Sign and text are presented so that a player who does not use audio can follow them; spoken and signed communication are described so that a player who does not use sight can follow them; and every conversation is designed to be complete through more than one channel. The goal is that no player is ever shut out of understanding what a character has said.

### Presence and Absence of the Silent Companion

Faience is a Shawabti artificial intelligence and stands outside human concepts of race, ethnicity, and gender, and none are assigned. Faience is silent through Operation One and first stirs, wordless, in a damaged pod on Attack Day. Presentation honours this silence as a deliberate choice rather than an absence of content, consistent with the canon that silence is a storytelling tool and never a gap where audio has not yet been made. The place a companion will one day fill is felt as empty throughout the ordinary week, and Faience's first wordless stirring is presented with the weight the canon gives it, marking the seam where a new kind of communication begins.

## How This Philosophy Governs the Rest of the Bible

This document is the foundation, and the documents that follow apply it. The Player Identity document applies this philosophy to the character the player creates. The recurring character profiles apply it to each member of the cast, carrying each person's full personality alongside the identity facts the Character Identity Map records. Every discipline that touches a character, whether art, animation, user interface, audio, writing, or gameplay programming, is expected to work from these principles so that the game presents people consistently, honestly, and with respect, and so that the promise the game makes to its players is kept in every scene.
