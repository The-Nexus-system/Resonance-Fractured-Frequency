# Operation One: Exodus — Chapter Four: Salvage

This is a gameplay design document for the Maintenance perspective of Chapter Four. It maintains all previously established canon. It is not a screenplay and it is not code. It describes how the chapter plays, what the player decides, what each decision teaches, and what permanently changes as a result.

## Primary Question

Can we build something worth protecting?

## Chapter Answer

Yes. By remembering what came before.

## Ending Question

What really happened aboard Hearth before its destruction?

## Purpose

Chapter Four introduces the expedition gameplay loop that will define the remainder of Operation One.

For the first time, the player deliberately leaves home. Not because of an emergency. Because the community has chosen to explore.

The player is no longer escaping Hearth. The player is returning to Hearth.

This chapter introduces exploration, mapping, environmental storytelling, and recovery. The player learns that salvaging history is just as important as salvaging technology.

## Emotional Themes

- Memory.
- Loss.
- Healing.
- Discovery.
- Responsibility.
- Respect.
- Hope.
- Curiosity.

The player should experience grief without despair. The player is returning to places they once called home.

## Gameplay Themes

- Mission preparation.
- Crew consultation.
- Expedition planning.
- Environmental exploration.
- Navigation.
- Mapping.
- Artifact recovery.
- Historical investigation.
- Meaningful salvage.
- Community growth.

## Chapter Structure

Every expedition throughout Chapter Four follows the same rhythm.

- Preparation.
- Departure.
- Exploration.
- Recovery.
- Return.
- Reflection.
- Rebuilding.

The player should become familiar with this rhythm because it becomes the foundation for future exploration chapters.

## Part One: Preparing For The First Expedition

### Part One — Purpose

Teach expedition planning. Teach that preparation determines success. Teach that leaving home is a community effort rather than a solo activity.

The player should never simply press a button labeled "Start Mission." Every expedition begins through conversation, planning, and preparation.

### Part One — Narrative

Several days have passed since the completion of the station's first major expansion.

Life has become more predictable. Morning routines exist. Construction continues. The community is stable enough to begin exploring.

During the morning briefing, the crew agrees it is finally time to return to Hearth. Not to reclaim it. To learn from it.

### Community Discussion

Every primary crew member contributes.

- Rowan wishes to recover tools and fabrication equipment.
- Mara wishes to recover engineering systems and power infrastructure.
- Samira wishes to recover medical supplies and patient records.
- Jonah wishes to recover logistics data and station maps.
- Talia wishes to secure safe expedition routes.
- Soren wishes to investigate unusual readings from the wreckage.
- Mira asks whether anyone left messages behind.
- The Vale System moderates discussion while encouraging every voice to be heard.

### Part One — Player Decisions

The player must make three decisions before departure. These choices change the expedition. They do not change canon.

### Decision One: Select Expedition Priority

#### Choice A: Engineering Recovery

- Primary goal: recover fabrication equipment and engineering components.
- Immediate benefits:
  - Additional engineering resources.
  - New fabrication recipes.
  - Engineering research progresses faster.
- Future effects:
  - Construction projects require fewer salvaged materials.
  - Advanced station upgrades unlock earlier.

#### Choice B: Medical Recovery

- Primary goal: recover medical equipment and patient archives.
- Immediate benefits:
  - Expanded medical inventory.
  - Additional rehabilitation equipment.
  - Medical Archive entries.
- Future effects:
  - Future injuries recover more quickly.
  - Preventative healthcare projects expand sooner.

#### Choice C: Historical Recovery

- Primary goal: recover personal records, educational archives, and cultural artifacts.
- Immediate benefits:
  - The Knowledge Archive expands significantly.
  - Additional optional conversations.
  - Historical discoveries.
- Future effects:
  - Diplomatic understanding.
  - Additional exploration clues.
  - Greater emotional understanding of Hearth.

#### Choice D: Infrastructure Recovery

- Primary goal: recover station systems and habitat modules.
- Immediate benefits:
  - Additional construction materials.
  - Habitat expansion.
  - Life support redundancy.
- Future effects:
  - Larger station modules become available sooner.
  - Community growth accelerates.

### Decision Two: Preparation Focus

#### Choice A: Pack additional tools

- Benefits:
  - Additional repair options during exploration.
  - Reduced equipment failures.
  - Less inventory space for recovered items.

#### Choice B: Pack additional medical supplies

- Benefits:
  - Improved emergency response.
  - Safer exploration.
  - Reduced cargo capacity.

#### Choice C: Pack additional research equipment

- Benefits:
  - Additional environmental discoveries.
  - Expanded Knowledge Archive.
  - Reduced available space for engineering equipment.

#### Choice D: Travel light

- Benefits:
  - Maximum salvage capacity.
  - Less flexibility when unexpected problems arise.

### Decision Three: Consult A Crew Member Before Departure

The player may consult everyone. However, one crew member may be selected as the expedition's primary advisor. This determines who provides the majority of guidance throughout the mission.

- Choose Rowan:
  - Maintenance shortcuts.
  - Hidden repair opportunities.
  - Structural observations.
- Choose Mara:
  - Advanced audio navigation.
  - Engineering insight.
  - Power diagnostics.
- Choose Samira:
  - Environmental health observations.
  - Medical preparedness.
  - Crew safety advice.
- Choose Jonah:
  - Improved navigation planning.
  - Mission efficiency.
  - Resource management.
- Choose Talia:
  - Hazard awareness.
  - Risk identification.
  - Safer exploration routes.
- Choose Soren:
  - Research observations.
  - Artifact analysis.
  - Historical interpretation.
- Choose Mira:
  - Additional optional discoveries.
  - Hidden environmental storytelling.
  - Unexpected interactions adults overlook.
- Choose The Vale System:
  - Balanced strategic guidance.
  - Leadership advice.
  - Crew coordination.
  - Decision support.

### Part One — Gameplay Systems Introduced

- Mission preparation.
- Expedition planning.
- Equipment selection.
- Mission priorities.
- Advisor system.
- Expedition briefing.
- Long-term mission consequences.

### Part One — Accessibility

All preparation choices must remain fully reviewable before departure. Players may change selections until confirming launch.

Mission summaries must clearly explain:

- Benefits.
- Trade-offs.
- Recommended crew expertise.
- Accessibility considerations.

No player should be required to memorize preparation choices.

### Part One — Emotional Goal

The player should understand that every expedition belongs to the entire community. They are leaving home. Everyone is trusting them to return.

## Part Two: Departure

### Part Two — Purpose

Teach the player how expeditions actually begin. Leaving the station should always feel meaningful. The player is leaving the safety of home. The community watches them go. Every departure should reinforce that someone is waiting for the player to return.

### Part Two — Narrative

Construction briefly pauses. Crew members naturally gather near the docking bay. No ceremony is held. This has already begun to feel normal.

The player performs a final systems check. The advisor selected during mission preparation joins the player over radio. The remaining crew continue supporting the expedition from home.

The player launches. The station slowly disappears behind them.

### Part Two — Gameplay Purpose

Teach:

- Launch procedures.
- Long-range navigation.
- Mission logging.
- Return point management.
- Safe travel.
- Orientation outside the station.

Players should learn that traveling away from home requires preparation.

### Sound Design

Before launch:

- Airlocks cycling.
- Dock clamps releasing.
- Crew conversations fading.
- Morning activity continues behind the player.

During departure:

- Thrusters.
- Navigation tones.
- Beacon confirmation.
- Station communications.

As distance increases:

- The station grows quieter.
- The surrounding debris field grows louder.
- The player realizes how isolated they truly are.

### Part Two — Accessibility

At any point the player may request:

- Current heading.
- Distance from home.
- Estimated travel time.
- Remaining oxygen.
- Power status.
- Mission objective.
- Current destination.

Orientation information is always available without interrupting gameplay. Blind players should never become disoriented while traveling.

### Player Decision: Route Toward Hearth

The player must choose the route toward Hearth. The destination remains the same. The journey differs.

#### Choice One: Shortest Route

- Benefits:
  - Arrive more quickly.
  - Consume fewer supplies.
- Future effects:
  - Miss optional discoveries.
  - Fewer opportunities to recover minor salvage.
  - Lower Knowledge Archive growth.

#### Choice Two: Survey Route

- Benefits:
  - Map surrounding debris.
  - Identify future exploration locations.
  - Discover additional radio signals.
- Future effects:
  - Additional optional expeditions unlock later.
  - The Knowledge Archive expands.
  - Travel takes longer.

#### Choice Three: Hazard Route

- Benefits:
  - Travel through a dangerous debris field containing unusually valuable salvage.
- Future effects:
  - Rare engineering materials.
  - Earlier advanced upgrades.
  - Greater navigation difficulty.
  - Higher equipment wear.

#### Choice Four: Historical Route

- Benefits:
  - Travel through known civilian sections of the former station.
  - Recover cultural information.
  - Hear preserved environmental recordings.
- Future effects:
  - Additional historical discoveries.
  - Personal stories.
  - Family recordings.
  - Educational archives.
  - Unique conversations become available later.
  - Engineering rewards are reduced.

### Advisor Interaction

The selected advisor actively changes the journey.

- Rowan notices structural debris.
- Mara identifies damaged systems through sound.
- Samira monitors environmental health.
- Jonah tracks mission efficiency.
- Talia identifies hazards.
- Soren recognizes unusual scientific patterns.
- Mira notices details adults overlook.
- The Vale System provides balanced strategic guidance.

The advisor is not passive narration. They actively teach the player throughout travel.

### Dynamic Events

During travel several optional discoveries may appear. Examples include:

- A drifting toolbox.
- A damaged maintenance drone.
- A personal backpack.
- An emergency beacon.
- A sealed cargo container.
- A classroom assignment floating through space.
- A family photograph.
- A musical instrument.

The player chooses whether to investigate. Ignoring these discoveries never prevents progression. Investigating them expands understanding of Hearth and its people.

### Part Two — Gameplay Systems Introduced

- Long-range navigation.
- Exploration routing.
- Dynamic discoveries.
- Mission logging.
- Advisor dialogue.
- Environmental storytelling.

### Part Two — Emotional Goal

The player should gradually realize they are not simply traveling through wreckage. They are traveling through the remains of people's lives. Every object once belonged somewhere. Every location once mattered to someone.

### Part Two — End Condition

The player reaches the outer edge of Hearth's debris field. The navigation computer identifies multiple possible expedition sites. The player stops. Looks outward.

For the first time since Chapter One, the player voluntarily returns home. Not to reclaim it. To remember it.

The first exploration zone becomes available. Part Three begins.

## Part Three: First Expedition

### Part Three — Purpose

Teach the player how exploration functions throughout Resonance. Teach that exploration is not about collecting resources. Teach that every location tells a story. Teach that preparation affects the player's experience without preventing success.

This establishes the core exploration gameplay loop used throughout the remainder of the campaign.

### Part Three — Narrative Purpose

The player arrives at the outer edge of Hearth's debris field. The navigation systems automatically identify several possible expedition sites.

These sites are not randomly generated. Each location existed before the destruction of Hearth. Each location has history. Each location has purpose.

The player decides where to begin. Every location eventually becomes explorable. The player's decision determines which story is experienced first.

### Part Three — Gameplay Purpose

Introduce:

- Zone selection.
- Environmental storytelling.
- Mapping.
- Exploration logs.
- Mission recording.
- Recoverable history.
- Recoverable technology.
- Recoverable culture.
- Recoverable accessibility technology.

### Exploration Philosophy

Every location should answer one question. Every location should create additional questions.

Every location should provide:

- Something useful.
- Something emotional.
- Something historical.
- Something mysterious.

Resources alone are never sufficient rewards.

### Expedition Choice One: Engineering Deck

- Primary question: how did Hearth keep millions of people alive?
- Primary crew: Rowan, Mara, and the player.
- Player learns: large engineering systems function because many small systems work together.
- Gameplay introduced:
  - Power restoration.
  - Equipment recovery.
  - Engineering mapping.
  - Structural diagnostics.
- Immediate rewards:
  - Fabrication equipment.
  - Engineering components.
  - Maintenance records.
- Future benefits:
  - Earlier engineering upgrades.
  - Improved station construction.
  - Additional engineering conversations.
- Future chapters: engineering discoveries influence future construction options.

### Expedition Choice Two: Medical Complex

- Primary question: how did Hearth care for its people?
- Primary crew: Samira and the player.
- Player learns: medicine extends beyond emergencies.
- Gameplay introduced:
  - Medical archives.
  - Hospital exploration.
  - Patient record recovery.
  - Medical logistics.
- Immediate rewards:
  - Medical supplies.
  - Medical equipment.
  - Healthcare archives.
- Future benefits:
  - Improved rehabilitation.
  - Expanded preventative medicine.
  - Additional medical accessibility technology.
- Future chapters: medical discoveries improve long-term crew wellbeing.

### Expedition Choice Three: Educational District

- Primary question: what kind of civilization were people trying to build?
- Primary crew: Mira, Soren, and the player.
- Player learns: knowledge survives disaster.
- Gameplay introduced:
  - Educational archives.
  - Historical reconstruction.
  - Civilian exploration.
  - Memory preservation.
- Immediate rewards:
  - Books.
  - School projects.
  - Recorded lessons.
  - Children's artwork.
  - Community history.
- Future benefits:
  - The Knowledge Archive expands dramatically.
  - Additional cultural conversations.
  - Greater understanding of Hearth.
- Future chapters: recovered educational materials unlock optional learning opportunities throughout the campaign.

### Expedition Choice Four: Habitat Ring

- Primary question: who lived here?
- Primary crew: the Vale System and the player.
- Player learns: civilizations are remembered through ordinary life.
- Gameplay introduced:
  - Residential exploration.
  - Personal belongings.
  - Family archives.
  - Environmental storytelling.
- Immediate rewards:
  - Personal journals.
  - Family photographs.
  - Voice recordings.
  - Community records.
- Future benefits:
  - Crew relationships deepen.
  - Additional optional conversations.
  - Greater emotional connection to Hearth.
- Future chapters: recovered memories influence later diplomatic and ethical discussions.

### Canon Rule: Zones Are Chosen, Not Completed First

Every expedition zone eventually becomes required. The player chooses exploration order. Not exploration completion. Different playthroughs emphasize different discoveries first. Canon remains unchanged.

### Exploration Gameplay

Every location contains multiple categories of discoveries.

- Recoverable technology.
- Recoverable history.
- Recoverable accessibility innovations.
- Recoverable cultural artifacts.
- Recoverable environmental recordings.
- Recoverable scientific observations.
- Recoverable personal stories.
- Recoverable maintenance records.

Players are encouraged to investigate every category. None are mandatory. Curiosity is rewarded.

### Environmental Storytelling

No location should explain itself through exposition. Instead players learn through observation. Examples include:

- Coffee mugs left on desks.
- Wheel tracks through corridors.
- Braille signage.
- AAC terminals.
- Children's drawings.
- Maintenance notes.
- Half-finished repairs.
- Open books.
- Musical instruments.
- Gardens.
- Personal decorations.

These details should communicate that Hearth was lived in.

### Dynamic Discoveries

Every expedition includes optional discoveries. Examples include:

- An emergency beacon still transmitting.
- A maintenance drone continuing its programmed route.
- A service animal quietly waiting beside its handler.
- An unfinished engineering project.
- A music rehearsal preserved in environmental recordings.
- A classroom lesson interrupted by evacuation.
- A community garden still growing under emergency lighting.

The player chooses whether to investigate. Ignoring optional discoveries never prevents chapter completion. Investigating them permanently expands the Knowledge Archive and deepens community understanding.

### Advisor System

The chosen advisor continues guiding the player. However, every crew member may occasionally contribute remotely if their expertise becomes relevant. The station remains part of the expedition. The player never feels alone.

### Part Three — Accessibility

Every environmental clue must exist through multiple sensory channels.

- Visual.
- Audio.
- Captions.
- Braille.
- Tactile feedback.
- Environmental descriptions.

Players should never depend upon one sensory modality to understand a location.

### Part Three — Emotional Goal

The player should realize that Hearth was never simply a spacecraft. It was a city. A home. A school. A hospital. A workplace. A neighborhood.

Exploration should feel like walking through memory rather than ruins.

### Part Three — End Condition

The player successfully completes their first exploration zone. Before returning home, they receive a notification. Multiple additional locations have now been identified.

The player realizes that they have explored only a tiny portion of Hearth. The journey home begins, leading into Part Four.

## Part Four: Homecoming

### Part Four — Purpose

Complete the expedition gameplay loop. Teach that returning home is as important as leaving it. Demonstrate that discoveries only become meaningful when they are shared with the community.

Every expedition should permanently change the station. Every expedition should permanently change the player. Every expedition should permanently change the crew.

This becomes the standard ending structure for every major expedition throughout Resonance.

### Part Four — Narrative Purpose

The player docks with the station. The station immediately feels different from the silence of Hearth. People are living. Working. Laughing. Building.

The contrast between Hearth and home should be emotionally obvious. The station is no longer simply where the player lives. It is where the player belongs.

### Part Four — Gameplay Purpose

Introduce:

- Mission debriefing.
- Salvage processing.
- Artifact cataloging.
- Knowledge Archive integration.
- Community reactions.
- Project unlocking.
- Expedition reflection.
- Station growth.
- Future mission planning.

### Arrival

The player approaches the station. Automatic docking systems respond. Crew members continue their daily routines. Nobody stops working simply because the player returned.

Instead, people naturally gather as recovered materials begin arriving. The player immediately understands that the expedition belongs to the whole community.

### Mission Debrief

The player attends a community debrief. Every primary crew member responds according to their expertise.

- Rowan evaluates recovered equipment.
- Mara evaluates engineering systems.
- Samira evaluates medical discoveries.
- Jonah updates station inventories and future priorities.
- Talia evaluates risks introduced by recovered materials.
- Soren begins cataloging discoveries.
- Mira asks questions nobody else considered.
- The Vale System facilitates discussion.

The player participates. The player does not simply watch.

### Part Four — Player Decisions

Every expedition ends with four major decisions. Every decision permanently affects station development. Nothing permanently locks the player out of content. Choices determine what develops first. Not what develops forever.

### Decision One: Recovered Equipment

#### Choice A: Install immediately

- Immediate result: new gameplay systems become available immediately.
- Future effect:
  - Resources are consumed.
  - Later upgrades require additional salvage.

#### Choice B: Study first

- Immediate result:
  - The Knowledge Archive expands.
  - Research opportunities increase.
- Future effect:
  - Installation takes longer.
  - Future upgrades become more efficient.

#### Choice C: Store for future use

- Immediate result: emergency reserve grows.
- Future effect:
  - Current progress slows.
  - Future flexibility increases.

### Decision Two: Recovered Historical Material

#### Choice A: Archive immediately

- Immediate result:
  - The Knowledge Archive expands.
  - Additional conversations unlock.
- Future effect:
  - Crew understanding grows.
  - New diplomatic dialogue becomes available in later chapters.

#### Choice B: Display throughout the station

- Immediate result: recovered artwork, photographs, educational materials, and personal items appear throughout the station.
- Future effect:
  - Crew morale improves.
  - The station increasingly reflects Hearth's culture.
  - Additional environmental storytelling develops.

#### Choice C: Return personal items to surviving families if possible

- Immediate result: items remain unavailable.
- Future effect:
  - Later survivors recognize recovered belongings.
  - Relationships deepen.
  - Future missions gain additional emotional significance.

### Decision Three: Recovered Resources

#### Choice A: Prioritize station expansion

- Immediate result: construction progresses rapidly.
- Future effect: research and specialized equipment develop more slowly.

#### Choice B: Prioritize research

- Immediate result: the Knowledge Archive expands.
- Future effect:
  - Construction progresses more gradually.
  - Additional discoveries become available sooner.

#### Choice C: Prioritize community wellbeing

- Immediate result:
  - Living spaces improve.
  - Accessibility improvements expand.
  - Crew morale increases.
- Future effect: large construction projects require additional expeditions.

### Decision Four: Next Expedition Priority

The player recommends what should receive attention next.

- Engineering.
- Medical.
- History.
- Infrastructure.
- Unknown Signals.

The recommendation influences planning discussions at the beginning of the next expedition. The player never becomes locked into one path. The recommendation simply determines what the community prepares first.

### Community Changes

The station should visibly and audibly change after every expedition. Examples include:

- Recovered artwork hanging on walls.
- Plants transplanted from Hearth.
- Recovered books appearing in common areas.
- Engineering equipment installed.
- Medical devices activated.
- Recovered music occasionally playing.
- Children's educational materials displayed.
- Crew members discussing discoveries while working.

Every expedition leaves permanent evidence throughout the station.

### Knowledge Archive

Every expedition updates the Knowledge Archive. Entries include:

- Recovered history.
- Recovered engineering knowledge.
- Recovered medical practices.
- Recovered accessibility innovations.
- Recovered cultural traditions.
- Recovered personal stories.
- Recovered scientific observations.

The player should immediately understand that preserving knowledge is just as valuable as recovering technology.

### Crew Conversations

Following every expedition, every primary crew member gains new optional conversations. These conversations should reflect:

- What was discovered.
- How it changes their work.
- How it changes their understanding of Hearth.
- How it changes future plans.

The player is encouraged to spend time talking with the community before beginning another expedition.

### Part Four — Accessibility

Every recovered accessibility innovation should become available to the player once it has been researched and installed. Examples include:

- Improved tactile navigation.
- Expanded braille support.
- Additional environmental beacon options.
- Improved AAC interfaces.
- Enhanced wearable haptic support.
- Adaptive workstation improvements.

Accessibility evolves alongside civilization. It is never treated as optional content.

### Part Four — Emotional Goal

The player should feel relief upon returning home. The station should become emotionally safer after every expedition. Players should look forward to coming home just as much as leaving.

### Chapter Climax

As the community finishes cataloging the expedition, Soren notices an anomaly. The recovered data does not match known records. Several engineering logs reference locations that should not exist. Historical timestamps contain impossible inconsistencies.

The strange signal detected at the end of Chapter Three appears again. This time, it responds to something recovered during the expedition. Not to the station. Not to the player. To something brought home.

The room falls silent. Nobody understands why.

### Chapter End State

The expedition loop has been fully taught. The player understands:

- How to prepare.
- How to explore.
- How to recover discoveries.
- How to return home.
- How discoveries permanently change the station.
- How community transforms exploration into progress.

The station is larger. The crew is wiser. The Knowledge Archive has expanded. The mystery has deepened.

Chapter Four continues with one final question in mind: what was Hearth carrying that someone still wants?

## Part Five: Living With Discovery

### Part Five — Purpose

Expand the expedition system beyond exploration. Teach that discoveries continue affecting the community long after an expedition ends. Introduce long-term consequences, return expeditions, and personal reflection.

This section completes the gameplay philosophy of Chapter Four.

### Expedition Philosophy

No expedition is ever considered completely finished. Every expedition changes future expeditions. Every discovery creates new opportunities. Every unanswered question becomes a future destination.

The player should regularly revisit previously explored locations with new knowledge, new equipment, and new perspectives.

### Canon Rule: No Zone Is Ever Fully Completed On The First Visit

No exploration zone should ever be permanently completed on the first visit. Every major location should contain discoveries that become accessible only after later chapters, later professions, new equipment, or new Knowledge Archive discoveries. The player should always have reasons to return. Curiosity should outlive completion.

### Part Five — Gameplay Purpose

Introduce:

- Return expeditions.
- Incomplete discoveries.
- Long-term exploration.
- Expedition journals.
- Artifact categorization.
- Crew requests.
- Mission consequences.
- Adaptive exploration.

### Expedition Consequences

Every expedition should evaluate the player's preparation and decisions. Poor planning does not end the game. Poor planning creates additional work. Examples include:

- Equipment wears out sooner.
- Power reserves become limited.
- Navigation becomes more difficult.
- Additional maintenance is required after returning home.
- Certain discoveries must wait until another expedition.
- Crew discussions reflect lessons learned.

Failure becomes history. Not punishment.

### Part Five — Player Decisions

Every expedition should include meaningful optional decisions. Every decision changes future gameplay. Nothing permanently removes content. The player chooses priorities. Not permanent outcomes.

### Decision One: Continue Exploring

The player discovers another promising area.

#### Choice A: Continue searching

- Immediate result:
  - Additional discoveries.
  - Greater rewards.
- Future effect:
  - Higher fatigue.
  - Increased equipment wear.
  - Reduced remaining supplies.

#### Choice B: Return home now

- Immediate result:
  - Safer return.
  - Earlier community development.
- Future effect:
  - Additional discoveries remain for later expeditions.
  - Nothing is lost.

### Decision Two: Unexpected Discovery

The player finds an object that cannot immediately be understood.

#### Choice A: Recover it immediately

- Immediate result: unknown technology returns home.
- Future effect:
  - Research begins immediately.
  - Potential risks remain unknown.

#### Choice B: Document its location

- Immediate result: detailed survey completed.
- Future effect:
  - Later expeditions may return with better equipment.
  - Research becomes more efficient.

#### Choice C: Leave it untouched

- Immediate result: no immediate change.
- Future effect:
  - The location becomes an ongoing mystery discussed by the crew.
  - Future storylines expand naturally.

### Decision Three: Crew Request

Before every expedition at least one crew member asks the player to look for something personally meaningful. Examples include:

- Rowan requests specialized maintenance tools.
- Mara requests engineering components.
- Samira requests medical records.
- Jonah requests logistics databases.
- Talia requests security logs.
- Soren requests scientific samples.
- Mira asks whether her old classroom still exists.
- The Vale System requests command archives.

The player chooses whether to prioritize the request.

#### Choice A: Complete the request

- Immediate result:
  - Relationship deepens.
  - Unique conversations unlock.
- Future effect:
  - Additional cooperative opportunities become available.
  - Crew trust increases.

#### Choice B: Focus on primary mission

- Immediate result: mission progresses more efficiently.
- Future effect:
  - The crew member understands.
  - The request remains available for future expeditions.
  - Relationships are unchanged.

### Artifact Classification

Every recovered item belongs to one primary category.

- Engineering.
- Medical.
- Scientific.
- Historical.
- Educational.
- Accessibility.
- Personal.
- Cultural.
- Environmental.
- Unknown.

Artifact categories determine:

- Who studies the item.
- Which gameplay systems expand.
- Which Knowledge Archive entries unlock.
- Which conversations become available.
- Which future expeditions are generated.

Resources should never exist without context. Every artifact should tell part of Hearth's story.

### Expedition Journal

After every expedition the player is invited to record observations. Participation is optional. The journal never grades the player. Possible entries include:

- What surprised you?
- What questions remain unanswered?
- What would you investigate next?
- What discovery mattered most?
- Which crew member would appreciate this discovery?

The journal becomes part of the player's permanent history. Future professions may reveal that earlier assumptions were incomplete. Players gradually reconstruct the truth across multiple perspectives.

### Community Reflection

Following every expedition, crew members naturally discuss discoveries with one another. Conversations continue even when the player does not participate. The station remembers.

Recovered discoveries begin appearing throughout the station.

- Books appear on shelves.
- Recovered artwork hangs on walls.
- Engineering equipment enters workshops.
- Medical devices begin operating.
- Plants are replanted.
- Historical displays emerge.

The station slowly becomes a living museum of Hearth.

### Part Five — Accessibility

Every exploration zone records:

- Visited areas.
- Unfinished discoveries.
- Outstanding crew requests.
- Artifact categories.
- Remaining mysteries.

Players may request summaries at any time through speech, captions, braille displays, or tactile interfaces. No player should need to remember unfinished exploration from memory alone.

### Part Five — Emotional Goal

The player should leave Chapter Four understanding one central truth: nothing important has been lost forever if someone remembers it. Every expedition preserves part of a civilization. Every return home keeps that civilization alive.

### Chapter Four Design Outcome

By the end of Chapter Four the player understands that exploration is not driven by loot. It is driven by memory. Knowledge. Community. Hope.

The expedition loop has become second nature. The player no longer asks, "What can I collect?" The player asks, "What can I preserve?"

## Part Six: Expedition Consequences

### Part Six — Purpose

Complete the player's decision framework for exploration. Every major expedition should conclude with meaningful decisions. Every decision changes future gameplay. No decision permanently removes content. Different choices create different histories.

### Decision Four: Salvage Capacity

The player's inventory is nearly full. Not everything can be recovered. The player must decide what leaves Hearth first.

#### Choice A: Prioritize People

- Examples:
  - Medical records.
  - Personal journals.
  - AAC devices.
  - Family photographs.
  - Recorded messages.
  - Adaptive equipment.
- Immediate result: less technology recovered.
- Future effect:
  - Crew relationships deepen.
  - The Knowledge Archive expands.
  - Historical understanding improves.
  - Future dialogue becomes richer.

#### Choice B: Prioritize Technology

- Examples:
  - Fabricators.
  - Medical devices.
  - Power systems.
  - Engineering equipment.
  - Scientific instruments.
- Immediate result: station capabilities expand rapidly.
- Future effect:
  - Community conversations about Hearth develop more slowly.
  - Some personal stories remain undiscovered until later expeditions.

#### Choice C: Prioritize Knowledge

- Examples:
  - Research databases.
  - Educational materials.
  - Historical archives.
  - Navigation records.
  - Scientific observations.
- Immediate result: the Knowledge Archive grows substantially.
- Future effect:
  - Research and diplomacy progress sooner.
  - Construction progresses more gradually.

#### Choice D: Balanced Recovery

- Recover smaller amounts from every category.
- Immediate result: steady progress across every gameplay system.
- Future effect:
  - No single system advances rapidly.
  - Future expeditions remain highly flexible.

### Decision Five: Unexpected Survivor Signal

A weak emergency beacon briefly activates. Its source cannot be confirmed. Continuing toward it risks delaying the expedition.

#### Choice A: Investigate immediately

- Immediate result:
  - Additional exploration.
  - Possible rescue.
- Future effect:
  - The expedition becomes longer.
  - Additional resources are consumed.
  - Optional future storyline begins.

#### Choice B: Mark the location

- Immediate result: precise coordinates recorded.
- Future effect:
  - Dedicated rescue mission becomes available later.
  - The current expedition remains on schedule.

#### Choice C: Ignore for now

- Immediate result: the mission continues uninterrupted.
- Future effect:
  - The signal becomes an ongoing discussion aboard the station.
  - Its fate remains unknown until another opportunity arises.

### Decision Six: Damaged Area

A damaged section blocks further exploration.

#### Choice A: Repair immediately

- Immediate result:
  - The current expedition continues.
  - Resources consumed.
- Future effect:
  - The area is permanently accessible.
  - Later expeditions become easier.

#### Choice B: Find another route

- Immediate result:
  - Exploration continues.
  - Additional time required.
- Future effect:
  - Alternative passages become permanently mapped.
  - Hidden discoveries become available.

#### Choice C: Return later with better equipment

- Immediate result: the current expedition ends sooner.
- Future effect: future engineering upgrades unlock additional exploration opportunities.

### Decision Seven: Mission Completion

The player has completed the primary objective. Several optional discoveries remain.

#### Choice A: Leave immediately

- Immediate result:
  - Safe return.
  - Earlier station development.
- Future effect:
  - Remaining discoveries await later expeditions.
  - The crew praises disciplined decision making.

#### Choice B: Remain until supplies reach minimum safety limits

- Immediate result:
  - Additional discoveries.
  - Additional risk.
- Future effect:
  - Greater rewards.
  - More maintenance required after returning.

#### Choice C: Continue until every accessible area has been searched

- Immediate result:
  - Maximum exploration.
  - Highest resource expenditure.
- Future effect:
  - Future expeditions to this area become shorter.
  - Crew fatigue increases.
  - Additional recovery time required before the next expedition.

### Canon Rule: Completion Should Inspire Curiosity

Every expedition should conclude with the player believing they could have discovered even more. Completion should inspire curiosity. Not exhaustion. Players should feel excited to return. Not relieved to leave.

- Recovery.

- Community growth.

These systems become the foundation for every exploration chapter that follows throughout Resonance.

Chapter Four ends with the station preparing for a future that extends far beyond simple survival, and with one question still open: what was Hearth carrying that someone still wants?
