# Operation One: Exodus — Master Timeline

This document is the single canonical chronology of Operation One: Exodus. It records what happened aboard CSV Hearth during the last week of its ordinary life and the morning it ended. The Campaign Bible defines the shape of the operation, the chapter design notes define how the maintenance perspective lives through it, and the Crew Progression System defines how the survivors change afterward. This document sits beneath all of them as the shared spine of time that every one of those documents assumes is true.

Every campaign references this timeline. Every cinematic references this timeline. Every conversation, flashback, and future operation assumes this timeline is correct. There is only one timeline. There are many perspectives.

## Purpose

The purpose of this document is to fix the events of Operation One in a single, unchanging order so that every profession, every chapter, and every future retelling depicts the same history.

A companion document, the Perspective Map, will describe how each profession experiences these events differently. A further companion, the Character Schedule, now exists at docs/design_notes/operation_one_exodus_character_schedule.md and describes the ordinary routines of individual characters in fuller detail; it remains synchronized with this timeline. The Perspective Map is anticipated by this one and, once written, will remain synchronized with it as well. Where any campaign, scene, or later document conflicts with the order of events recorded here, this document is canon.

The timeline answers one question and only one question: what happened, and when. It does not decide how any single player experiences a moment. It decides only that the moment occurred, that it occurred in this order, and that it occurred the same way for everyone aboard, whether they were on the bridge, in a garden, in a maintenance crawlspace, or asleep.

## Canon Rules

The following rules govern every use of this timeline. Several of them restate principles already recorded elsewhere in the canon; here they are applied specifically to chronology and continuity. Each rule is given its own heading so that no two rules share a label.

### Canon Rule: One Timeline, Many Perspectives

Every campaign depicts identical historical events. Perspective changes. Knowledge changes. Vocabulary changes. What a medic notices differs from what an engineer notices, and what a child understands differs from what a captain understands. History itself never changes. A player who returns to Operation One as a different profession does not visit an alternate world. They visit the same week from a different chair.

### Canon Rule: Responsibility Before Convenience

Professional responsibilities always come before gameplay convenience. No character abandons their post, their patients, their students, or their duties because a scene would be tidier if they did. When a character appears beside the player, it is because their work genuinely brought them there, not because the plot needed a familiar face.

### Canon Rule: Life Continues Without the Player

Characters continue living their lives when the player is elsewhere. Every recurring character follows a complete daily routine of waking, working, eating, resting, and belonging. Departments continue functioning whether or not the player is present. When the player leaves a room, the conversation in it does not pause. When the player sleeps, the night shift still works.

### Canon Rule: The Player Participates, Never Dominates

The player participates in civilization. The player never becomes the only hero. CSV Hearth survives each ordinary day because thousands of people quietly perform their jobs together, and it is overwhelmed on its final day for reasons no single person could have prevented. The player is one competent member of a competent community, never its sole author.

### Canon Rule: Accessibility Is Ordinary Infrastructure

Accessibility appears naturally throughout every day and is never treated as exceptional. Braille, speech, captions, sign language, augmentative and alternative communication, haptic systems, adaptive workstations, mobility equipment, accessible transportation, adaptive education, and accessible emergency systems are simply how this civilization functions. No character comments on accessibility as though it were unusual, because aboard Hearth it is not.

### Canon Rule: Each Day Matters More Than the Last

Every day must become progressively more emotionally meaningful than the day before it. Across the week the player must gradually stop thinking of Hearth as a ship and begin thinking of Hearth as home. The timeline is built so that the loss on the final morning is unbearable precisely because the days leading to it were so ordinary, so warm, and so full of plans.

## The Shape of the Week

The timeline counts down toward the attack rather than forward from an arbitrary beginning. Day Seven is the earliest day recorded here, seven days before the ship is lost. Day One is the last full day of ordinary life. Attack Day follows Day One, and Operation One's chronology ends there.

The week is not a neutral stretch of time. It is deliberately shaped as a rising curve of belonging. The earliest days establish competence, routine, and the sound of a working ship. The middle days deepen relationships and introduce the first faint traces of the anomaly that will end everything. The final days fill with festival preparation, plans for the future, and a community entirely convinced it has decades ahead of it.

Attack Day is also Founding Day. The colony's founding anniversary, the largest celebration of Hearth's year, was scheduled to begin on the very morning the attack arrived. Every festival preparation across the week points toward a celebration that never happens.

## How to Read Each Day

Each day is written as a complete chronological narrative and is divided into the same recurring set of sections so that the reader can find the same information in the same place on any day. To keep the document navigable, every section heading is scoped to its day, so that no two headings across the document are identical.

Each day contains the following in order: the earliest hours before dawn and the night shift; the morning announcement; the morning routines across the ship; the working departments; the living departments; midday; evening and night; the state of the anomaly on that day; the presence of each primary character; the sound of the day; environmental storytelling; accessibility in practice; cross-perspective anchor scenes; and the narrative purpose, gameplay purpose, emotional goal, and end state of the day.

## Relationship to Chapter One's Scene Days

The Chapter One maintenance design note labels its playable scenes by counting forward from the player's first morning, so that its "Day One" is the first day the player experiences aboard Hearth and its later parts move toward the attack. This master timeline counts in the opposite direction, downward toward the attack, so that its "Day One" is the last ordinary day.

The two schemes describe the same week and are reconciled as follows: a Chapter One scene labeled forward day number N corresponds to this timeline's countdown day numbered eight minus N. Chapter One's first morning is this timeline's Day Seven. Chapter One's Pressure Junction 14 scene is this timeline's Day Six. Chapter One's hidden-route scene is this timeline's Day Four. Chapter One's little-emergency scene is this timeline's Day Three. The attack in both documents is Attack Day. Because Chapter One's first morning is this timeline's Day Seven, the morning announcement quoted in Chapter One is recorded here, unchanged, as the Day Seven announcement.

## The Anomaly Across the Week

A single phenomenon runs quietly beneath the entire week. It is never a monster in the walls and never a countdown the crew can read. It is a genuinely unprecedented disturbance in the region of space Hearth is crossing, and its earliest signs are indistinguishable from ordinary sensor noise.

On Day Seven it is almost impossible to notice. On Day Six, Research quietly documents a reading that does not fit. On Day Five, Navigation notices small inconsistencies it cannot explain, and Engineering detects a tiny resonance drift in the ship's own harmonics. On Day Four, several departments begin, independently and casually, comparing notes. On Day Three, the first cross-department conversations happen, and nobody believes there is any danger. On Day Two, the evidence grows but remains far short of a conclusion. On Day One, an interdisciplinary meeting is finally scheduled for the following morning. That morning is Attack Day, and the meeting never happens.

When the attack comes, it becomes clear that no department behaved incompetently. The phenomenon was outside every framework the crew possessed. The attack succeeds not because anyone failed, but because what arrived had never been seen before.

## The Morning Announcements

Every morning at exactly 06:30 Ship Standard Time a gentle two-tone claxon sounds, and Captain Elian of the Vale System delivers the morning announcement in the warm morning-announcement voice. The office of Captain belongs to the Vale System as a whole, and different members lead in different situations, but the daily morning announcement is always Elian's.

Every announcement is unique. Across the week they gradually reinforce the central themes of community, belonging, responsibility, hope, memory, and civilization. The Day Seven announcement is the one already recorded in Chapter One and is preserved here as canon. The Day One announcement, spoken on the eve of Founding Day, is written so that it becomes emotionally devastating once the player has finished Operation One and understands that no one aboard heard Elian speak again.

## Day Seven

Day Seven is the first day this timeline records, seven days before CSV Hearth is lost. It is an utterly ordinary day, and its purpose is to feel that way. Nothing important appears to happen. That is the point.

### Day Seven — Before Dawn and the Night Shift

At 05:30 Ship Standard Time the ship is still in its night cycle. Ventilation runs at its low overnight setting. The corridors are dim and quiet. The night shift is in its final hour, and the handover to day crews has begun in the departments that never fully sleep.

In Engineering the overnight watch confirms that power distribution held steady through the night. In Medical a single night nurse completes the last observation round before the day team arrives. In Maintenance two technicians finish a slow, unglamorous filter replacement they chose to do overnight precisely so that no one in the habitation decks would hear it. On the bridge a skeleton command watch logs an uneventful night and prepares the morning report. Across the ship, the people who kept everything moving while the rest of Hearth slept begin, quietly, to hand their work to the people waking up.

### Day Seven — The Morning Announcement

At 06:30 the two-tone claxon sounds, soft and familiar, and Captain Elian of the Vale System speaks:

"Good morning, everyone. This is Captain Elian of the Vale System. It is now 0630 Ship Standard Time. Morning shift begins in thirty minutes. Whether you're heading to Engineering, Medical, Security, Research, Flight Operations, Maintenance, or just starting another day with your family, thank you for everything you do to keep this ship alive. If today is your day off, enjoy it. If today is your first day aboard, welcome home. For those coming off night shift, thank you for keeping us moving while the rest of us slept. Have a good morning, everyone."

This is the announcement already recorded in Chapter One, and it is canon exactly as written. It establishes the pattern every later announcement varies against: the greeting, the time, the naming of the departments, the gratitude, the welcome, and the thanks to the night shift.

### Day Seven — Morning Routines Across Hearth

By 06:45 the habitation decks have come fully awake. Families prepare breakfast. Children are dressed and gathered. The smell of coffee and cooked grain drifts through the residential corridors. People greet each other by name in doorways and at lift landings.

The morning commute aboard Hearth is short and communal. Residents move toward their departments on foot and by the internal transit loops. The Commons, the ship's central public crew space, fills with the first wave of the day: officers, medical staff, security personnel, research staff, maintenance crews, and civilians, all sharing the same tables before the same shift.

### Day Seven — The Working Departments

Maintenance opens the day with Rowan Ives's briefing. The board lists routine items: intermittent pressure warnings on Deck Twelve, a life-support quirk in Section C, an inspection drone behaving oddly, a scheduled check of Pressure Junction 14 later in the week, a restricted-access request from Research that has not been explained, and Engineering's standing complaint about small unexplained power draws. None of it is alarming. All of it is normal.

Engineering runs its morning diagnostics. Mara Chen listens to the power harmonics the way others read a display, confirming that generators, relays, and distribution are all singing the notes they should. Medical opens its clinic hours under Doctor Samira Kade, whose sleeve projector renders her speech as sign or text for anyone who needs it. Security begins its patrol rotation under Talia Rusk, including the checkpoint outside the restricted Research section. Research works quietly behind its shielded doors, where Dr. Soren Qadir reviews the deep-space sensor logs. Command, through Captain Gideon of the Vale System, reviews the overnight report and finds nothing worth a second look.

### Day Seven — The Living Departments

The departments that are not about repair or defense are just as busy. In Education the school day begins, with adaptive tools laid out as ordinary equipment for every learner. In Hydroponics the agricultural decks run their morning irrigation and the growers walk the rows. In Cargo the day's manifest is checked and the lift platforms begin moving crates. Transportation keeps the transit loops and shuttle schedules running on time. Manufacturing spins up its fabrication lines. Civilian Services handles the ordinary business of a town: housing, supplies, disputes, and the hundred small requests that keep a community livable.

### Day Seven — Midday

At midday the meal rotations bring the ship together again. The Commons is at its loudest and warmest. Captain Elian walks the room, as Elian often does, greeting residents and remembering their names and their families. Rowan eats with the maintenance crew and, as always, checks that his daughter Mira has eaten. Jonah Briggs keeps the flow of people moving without anyone quite noticing he is doing it.

The afternoon returns each department to its work. Maintenance handles a short list of small jobs. Engineering runs a scheduled load test. Medical sees its afternoon appointments. Research continues its quiet review. The day is uneventful in the way that only a healthy ship can be.

### Day Seven — Evening and Night

In the evening the ship softens. Families gather. The gardens draw people who want to stand somewhere green. There is music in the Commons, informal and unhurried. Places of worship hold their evening observances. Children finish their projects and are, eventually, put to bed.

By 22:00 the habitation decks quiet. Ventilation drops to its night setting. The night shift takes over again, and Hearth carries its sleeping town onward through the dark.

### Day Seven — The Anomaly

On Day Seven the anomaly is present but effectively invisible. A single deep-space sensor drops one sample from a routine sweep. The event is logged automatically and reviewed by no one. There is nothing yet to notice, and no one notices it. This is the only day on which the phenomenon leaves no impression on any living person aboard.

### Day Seven — Character Presence

The Vale System holds the captain's office as a single person with dissociative identity disorder, and its members lead as each situation calls for them. On Day Seven, Elian gives the morning announcement and walks the Commons at midday; Gideon reviews the overnight report; Naomi keeps the emergency-readiness schedule current; Iris sits with the Founding Day planning committee.

Rowan Ives runs the maintenance briefing and moves through the decks by ear. Mara Chen tends the engineering harmonics. Doctor Samira Kade holds clinic hours. Chief Petty Officer Jonah Briggs keeps the day organized and the corridors flowing. Security Lead Talia Rusk patrols and mans the Research checkpoint. Dr. Soren Qadir reviews sensor logs behind the restricted doors. Mira Ives attends school and spends her free time near the maintenance shop, close to her father.

Faience is not active. The presence the player will one day know as Faience has no voice aboard Hearth yet. On Day Seven there is only the ordinary silence where that voice will someday be, and no one aboard has any reason to listen for it.

### Day Seven — The Sound of the Day

The morning is coffee, doors, greetings, and the low day-cycle hum of a healthy ship. Midday is the crowded, layered sound of the Commons. The evening is music, gardens, and the settling of families. The night is quiet ventilation and the distant, patient work of the night shift. A player who learns Day Seven learns the baseline against which every later change will be heard.

### Day Seven — Environmental Storytelling

The community board in the Commons carries the first Founding Day notices. A garden bed shows the earliest green of a crop that will be ready by the celebration. A children's mural in the education corridor is only sketched in outline. Nothing is finished, because the week has only begun.

### Day Seven — Accessibility in Practice

Samira's sleeve projector renders speech as sign and text throughout the day. Mara navigates Engineering entirely by sound and touch. Soren's chair hovers on its repulsors through the research decks. Talia's prosthetic carries her through her patrol without remark. Mira's augmentative communication interface is simply how Mira talks. The school's adaptive stations are used by everyone as a matter of course. No one treats any of it as noteworthy.

### Day Seven — Cross-Perspective Anchor Scenes

Two moments on Day Seven occur identically in every profession's campaign and serve as anchors that let the perspectives line up. The first is the 06:30 morning announcement, heard shipwide. The second is the midday gathering in the Commons, where every profession, wherever it begins its day, passes through the same room at the same time and hears the same crowd.

### Day Seven — Purpose, Goals, and End State

The narrative purpose of Day Seven is to establish the ordinary. Its gameplay purpose is to teach the player to read the ship by sound while nothing is at stake. Its emotional goal is comfort and competence: the feeling of a good, unremarkable day at a job among people who know your name. The end state of Day Seven is a ship that has gone to sleep exactly as it woke, with the player having learned the baseline sound of home.

## Day Six

Day Six is the second recorded day, six days before the loss. It repeats the rhythm of Day Seven closely enough to feel familiar, and changes just enough to reward a player who is paying attention. Its quiet event is the first time a single human being writes the anomaly down.

### Day Six — Before Dawn and the Night Shift

At 05:30 the night shift again nears its handover. The overnight watch in Engineering notes nothing unusual. In Research, however, the automated sweep that dropped a sample the previous day returns a reading that is not quite noise. The night analyst flags it for the day team without urgency, the way one flags a curiosity rather than a concern.

### Day Six — The Morning Announcement

At 06:30 the claxon sounds and Elian speaks, this time on the theme of community:

"Good morning, everyone. This is Captain Elian of the Vale System. It is now 0630 Ship Standard Time. Before the shift begins, I want to say something simple. A ship is only metal. What makes Hearth a place worth waking up on is the person across the table from you, the neighbor who saved you a seat, the crew that covers your shift when your child is sick. Look after each other today. That is the whole job, underneath all the other jobs. Morning shift begins in thirty minutes. Have a good morning, everyone."

### Day Six — Morning Routines Across Hearth

The habitation decks wake into their now-recognizable pattern. Breakfast, children, greetings, and the short communal commute fill the corridors. The Commons gathers its first wave. A player who spent Day Seven listening will notice how much of this they can already predict, and how good that predictability feels.

### Day Six — The Working Departments

In Research, Dr. Soren Qadir opens the flagged reading and decides it is worth watching. He does not raise an alarm. He opens a quiet log and records the first entry, files a request to widen a sensor sweep, and returns to his other work. This is the moment the anomaly first exists on paper.

Maintenance works its list, including the ongoing Deck Twelve pressure quirk. Engineering runs clean. Medical holds clinic hours. Security patrols, and Talia again notes, without enthusiasm, how the restricted Research zone keeps expanding. Command, through Gideon, reviews the day and sees only routine, because on Day Six routine is still all there is to see.

### Day Six — The Living Departments

Education runs its second day of the week, and the mural in the corridor gains its first color. Hydroponics thins and tends the Founding Day crop. Cargo moves the festival supplies that have begun arriving from stores. Transportation keeps the loops on time. Manufacturing fabricates parts for both daily needs and festival structures. Civilian Services fields a rising number of small festival requests.

### Day Six — Midday

The midday Commons is warm and full. Iris of the Vale System sits with the festival committee over the meal, and the plans for Founding Day take clearer shape. Rowan checks on Mira. Jonah keeps the room moving. The afternoon returns everyone to their departments, and the day proceeds without incident.

### Day Six — Evening and Night

The evening brings music, gardens, worship, and family. Founding Day is now close enough that its preparation is part of ordinary conversation. By 22:00 the ship quiets, and the night shift takes it onward.

### Day Six — The Anomaly

On Day Six the anomaly is documented for the first time. It is a single quiet log entry in Research and a single request to widen a sweep. No one else aboard is aware of it. It is still, by any reasonable measure, nothing.

### Day Six — Character Presence

Elian gives the announcement and moves through the day; Iris leads the festival committee; Gideon reviews the routine; Naomi keeps readiness current. Rowan briefs and roams Maintenance. Mara keeps Engineering singing true. Samira holds clinic. Jonah organizes. Talia patrols and grumbles about the restricted zone. Soren opens the anomaly log. Mira attends school and drifts toward the maintenance shop afterward. Faience remains silent and absent, a voice the ship does not yet have.

### Day Six — The Sound of the Day

The soundscape is Day Seven's, subtly enriched: a little more festival talk in the Commons, a few more crates moving in Cargo, the mural corridor busier with children. The night is unchanged. The familiarity is deliberate, and it is comforting.

### Day Six — Environmental Storytelling

The community board gains a Founding Day countdown. The garden crop is visibly taller. The mural has color. Festival supplies stack up in Cargo. Residents reference the previous day's conversations, so that the world remembers what the player did and said.

### Day Six — Accessibility in Practice

Accessibility continues as the ordinary infrastructure it is. The only new detail worth noting is that the festival committee, chaired by Iris, plans the celebration to be accessible by default, and no one frames this as a special accommodation. It is simply how Hearth throws a party.

### Day Six — Cross-Perspective Anchor Scenes

The Day Six anchors are again the 06:30 announcement and the midday Commons. A profession with access to Research, such as the Scientist perspective, additionally witnesses Soren opening the anomaly log; other perspectives pass the same shielded doors without knowing what is being written behind them.

### Day Six — Purpose, Goals, and End State

The narrative purpose of Day Six is to deepen familiarity and to plant the anomaly as a single quiet seed. Its gameplay purpose is to reward attentive listening with a ship the player can begin to predict. Its emotional goal is the comfort of routine becoming home. The end state of Day Six is a community one day closer to a celebration it is sure will come, and a single unread log entry that says otherwise.

## Day Five

Day Five is the third recorded day, five days before the loss. It is the day the anomaly first touches instruments other than a single sensor, and the day two very different people, working in two very different departments, feel something wrong without being able to name it.

### Day Five — Before Dawn and the Night Shift

At 05:30 the night shift approaches handover. In Flight Operations the overnight navigation watch records two small position inconsistencies that do not reconcile cleanly with the star fix. They are within tolerance, so the watch notes them and moves on. In Engineering the overnight watch hears nothing wrong, because on Day Five what is wrong is still too quiet for anyone but Mara to hear.

### Day Five — The Morning Announcement

At 06:30 Elian speaks on the theme of belonging:

"Good morning, everyone. This is Captain Elian of the Vale System. It is now 0630 Ship Standard Time. I met a family last night who came aboard only a month ago, and the youngest told me, very seriously, that this is home now. She's right. You do not have to have been born on Hearth to belong here. You belong here because you showed up, because you do your part, because someone would miss you if you were gone. Belong loudly today. Morning shift begins in thirty minutes. Have a good morning, everyone."

### Day Five — Morning Routines Across Hearth

The habitation decks wake in their familiar pattern, now with an unmistakable undercurrent of festival anticipation. Children talk about Founding Day. The commute fills the corridors. The Commons gathers its first wave, louder than the day before.

### Day Five — The Working Departments

This is the day Engineering feels the anomaly. During morning diagnostics Mara Chen notices a resonance drift in the ship's harmonics, a fractional wrongness in a note that has always been true. She cannot yet explain it, cannot yet reproduce it on demand, and cannot yet prove it is anything at all. She trusts her ears, opens her own quiet log, and mentions it to no one but her own records.

In Flight Operations the navigation inconsistencies from the night watch recur during the day, small and unexplained. In Research, Soren's widened sweep returns more of the reading he flagged. Two departments now hold pieces of the same puzzle without knowing the other department holds anything at all. Maintenance works its list, Medical holds clinic, Security patrols, and Command sees a routine day, because the pieces have not yet been laid side by side.

### Day Five — The Living Departments

Education's mural is half finished and clearly a picture of Hearth itself. Hydroponics reports the Founding Day crop on schedule. Cargo receives the largest festival shipment yet. Transportation adds extra loop runs to handle festival movement. Manufacturing builds the frames for the celebration's central gathering space. Civilian Services coordinates the growing web of festival volunteers.

### Day Five — Midday

The midday Commons hums with plans. Iris finalizes the festival's shape with the committee. Rowan checks on Mira, who has been unusually insistent, in her own way, that something in the ship "sounds funny." The adults treat it as a child's imagination, kindly and without dismissal. The afternoon returns everyone to work.

### Day Five — Evening and Night

The evening is music, gardens, worship, and family, all now threaded with festival rehearsal. By 22:00 the ship quiets. The night shift resumes, and in Engineering the overnight watch, listening for what Mara described, honestly cannot hear it.

### Day Five — The Anomaly

On Day Five the anomaly reaches instruments and ears beyond a single sensor. Navigation logs inconsistencies. Engineering, through Mara, detects a tiny resonance drift. Research gathers more of its unexplained reading. Three separate threads now exist. None of the three departments yet knows about the other two.

### Day Five — Character Presence

Elian gives the announcement and walks the day; Iris leads the festival committee; Gideon reviews routine reports; Naomi runs a scheduled readiness check. Rowan briefs Maintenance and quietly worries about Mira's certainty. Mara hears the drift and logs it alone. Samira holds clinic. Jonah organizes. Talia patrols the widening restricted zone. Soren gathers more anomalous data. Mira insists, in her own way, that the ship sounds wrong, and is gently reassured. Faience is still absent, but Mira's insistence is the first time anyone aboard listens hard for something they cannot name.

### Day Five — The Sound of the Day

The soundscape is fuller and happier on the surface, thick with festival preparation. Underneath it, for the first time, there is a note that does not belong, audible to Mara and, apparently, to Mira. A player replaying Day Five with sharp ears may catch it too, a fractional wrongness beneath a joyful day.

### Day Five — Environmental Storytelling

The countdown board reads five days. The mural is recognizably Hearth. The garden crop nears readiness. Cargo overflows with festival supplies. The frames of the celebration space rise in the Commons. The ship is visibly preparing for joy.

### Day Five — Accessibility in Practice

Accessibility remains ordinary. Notably, it is Mira's different way of perceiving the world, and the crew's habit of listening to her rather than correcting her, that first gives voice to the anomaly at all. The ship's willingness to take a disabled child seriously is, quietly, the first line of its defense, even though no one recognizes it as such.

### Day Five — Cross-Perspective Anchor Scenes

The Day Five anchors are the 06:30 announcement and the midday Commons. The Engineer perspective additionally lives Mara's discovery of the drift; the Scientist perspective lives Soren's growing data; a Flight Operations or Commissioned Officer perspective lives the navigation inconsistencies. Each perspective holds one true piece and none holds the whole.

### Day Five — Purpose, Goals, and End State

The narrative purpose of Day Five is to let the anomaly touch trusted instruments and trusted people, so that in hindsight the crew's failure to act looks like competence rather than negligence. Its gameplay purpose is to reward the player's trained ear with the first audible wrongness. Its emotional goal is the bittersweet tension of a joyful day with a shadow beneath it. The end state of Day Five is three departments each quietly holding a fragment of the truth, and a child who already knows.

## Day Four

Day Four is the fourth recorded day, four days before the loss. It is the day the fragments begin, casually and without alarm, to find one another. It is also the day the player, in the maintenance perspective, moves through the hidden body of the ship and comes to know Hearth not as a map but as a living thing.

### Day Four — Before Dawn and the Night Shift

At 05:30 the night shift nears handover having compared notes across two watches. A night engineer, aware that Mara mentioned a drift, listens for it and thinks, uncertainly, that perhaps it is there. A night analyst in Research, aware of Soren's log, adds another data point. The threads have not been joined, but the people holding them have begun, informally, to talk.

### Day Four — The Morning Announcement

At 06:30 Elian speaks on the theme of responsibility:

"Good morning, everyone. This is Captain Elian of the Vale System. It is now 0630 Ship Standard Time. Somewhere on this ship right now, someone is doing a job no one will ever thank them for, and Hearth is alive because of it. Responsibility is not glamorous. It is showing up for the boring, careful, unwitnessed work that keeps everyone else safe. If you carry that kind of responsibility, know that I see it, even when no one else does. Carry it well today. Morning shift begins in thirty minutes. Have a good morning, everyone."

### Day Four — Morning Routines Across Hearth

The decks wake into festival anticipation now at full strength. Children rehearse songs. The commute is cheerful. The Commons fills, and the celebration frames at its center are nearly built.

### Day Four — The Working Departments

Day Four is when observations begin to be compared. Mara mentions her drift to a trusted engineer over the diagnostics bench. That engineer has heard, secondhand, that Navigation logged something strange. Soren, widening his sweeps, hears from a colleague that Engineering thinks the ship's harmonics are off. None of them convenes a meeting. None of them believes there is danger. They simply begin, in ones and twos, to notice that their small anomalies might not be unrelated.

Maintenance works a fuller list, including the hidden-infrastructure route the player travels this day. Medical holds clinic. Security patrols, and Talia escorts a Research request through a restricted corridor, unaware she is walking beside the edge of the mystery. Command, through Gideon, notices the first faint pattern in the day's reports and files it away to watch.

### Day Four — The Living Departments

Education's mural is nearly finished. Hydroponics confirms the Founding Day crop will be ready in time. Cargo stages the festival supplies for distribution. Transportation plans the celebration-day loop schedule. Manufacturing completes the last of the celebration structures. Civilian Services locks in the festival program.

### Day Four — Midday

The midday Commons is at its most joyful yet. The celebration space is standing. Iris walks the committee through the final program. Rowan checks on Mira, who has stopped insisting the ship sounds wrong, not because it stopped, but because the adults could not hear it and she is a child who has learned when to stop. That detail will matter terribly in hindsight. The afternoon returns everyone to work.

### Day Four — Evening and Night

The evening is rehearsal, music, gardens, worship, and family. Founding Day feels imminent and certain. By 22:00 the ship quiets, and the night shift carries the growing, informal awareness of the anomaly into another dark.

### Day Four — The Anomaly

On Day Four multiple departments begin, quietly and independently, to compare observations. No one raises an alarm. No one believes there is danger. The anomaly is now a shared curiosity among a handful of professionals rather than a secret held by one, and that is exactly as far as it gets on Day Four.

### Day Four — Character Presence

Elian gives the announcement and walks the day; Iris finalizes the festival program; Gideon notices the faint pattern in the reports; Naomi keeps readiness current. Rowan leads the player through the hidden routes of the ship and checks on Mira. Mara compares her drift with a colleague. Samira holds clinic. Jonah organizes the festival logistics. Talia escorts a Research request. Soren connects his data to Engineering's rumor. Mira stops mentioning the sound. Faience remains absent, and the ship has quietly stopped listening for the thing Mira heard.

### Day Four — The Sound of the Day

The surface soundscape is at its warmest and most celebratory. Beneath it the anomalous note persists, and a very attentive player will notice it has grown just slightly since Day Five. The contrast between the joyful surface and the deepening wrongness beneath is the emotional engine of the day.

### Day Four — Environmental Storytelling

The countdown board reads four days. The mural is nearly complete. The celebration space stands finished in the Commons. The garden crop is heavy and close to harvest. Everywhere the player looks, the ship is finished preparing to celebrate and is simply waiting for the day.

### Day Four — Accessibility in Practice

Accessibility remains ordinary throughout. The quiet tragedy of Day Four is an accessibility failure that the ship does not recognize as one: a disabled child perceived something real, was gently not believed, and learned to stop saying it. The canon does not moralize about this. It simply records that Mira was right and was not heard.

### Day Four — Cross-Perspective Anchor Scenes

The Day Four anchors are the 06:30 announcement and the midday Commons. The Maintenance perspective additionally lives the hidden-route journey through the body of the ship. The Engineer, Scientist, and Command perspectives live the first informal comparing of notes. Each perspective adds one more thread to a rope no one has yet finished braiding.

### Day Four — Purpose, Goals, and End State

The narrative purpose of Day Four is to let competent people begin, reasonably, to connect the dots, so that their failure to act in time reads as human rather than foolish. Its gameplay purpose, in the maintenance perspective, is to teach the player the ship as a body through its hidden routes. Its emotional goal is intimacy with Hearth and a deepening unease. The end state of Day Four is a finished celebration waiting for its morning, and a mystery that has just begun to become a conversation.

## Day Three

Day Three is the fifth recorded day, three days before the loss. It is the day the anomaly earns its first real meeting, and the day a small emergency reminds the whole community how fiercely it protects its own.

### Day Three — Before Dawn and the Night Shift

At 05:30 the night shift hands over having, for the first time, written a joint note. An engineer and an analyst on the overnight watch, comparing their separate logs, agree to flag both to their day supervisors together. It is not urgency. It is diligence. But it is the first time the anomaly is treated as one thing rather than several.

### Day Three — The Morning Announcement

At 06:30 Elian speaks on the theme of hope:

"Good morning, everyone. This is Captain Elian of the Vale System. It is now 0630 Ship Standard Time. Three days until Founding Day. I have been thinking about the people who launched this ship, who packed their whole lives into her and pointed her at a future they would never see, because they hoped we would. We are the future they hoped for. Everything we build now is a message to the people who will stand where we stand generations from now. Hope forward today. Morning shift begins in thirty minutes. Have a good morning, everyone."

### Day Three — Morning Routines Across Hearth

The decks wake into open excitement. The countdown is short enough that children mark it aloud. The commute is bright. The Commons gathers around the finished celebration space.

### Day Three — The Working Departments

Day Three brings the first cross-department meeting. Engineering, Research, and Flight Operations sit down together, informally and briefly, to lay their three fragments side by side. The resonance drift, the sensor reading, and the navigation inconsistencies are, for the first time, discussed as possibly one phenomenon. The meeting reaches no conclusion and raises no alarm. Everyone present agrees it is worth watching and that there is no cause for concern. Gideon reviews the meeting's note and concurs.

Maintenance works its list. Medical holds clinic and, this day, responds to the small emergency. Security patrols. The ship functions, competent and calm, around a mystery it has decided to keep an eye on.

### Day Three — The Living Departments

This is the day of the little emergency. Mira Ives, drawn as ever toward the maintenance spaces, slips into a restricted passage, and a pressure door locks behind her. She is frightened. She is not in real danger, but she does not know that, and the community's response is immediate and coordinated. Maintenance and Rowan reach her. Medical, through Samira, stands ready. Security clears the way. Jonah coordinates. The child is freed, comforted, and returned to her father. Education, Hydroponics, Cargo, Transportation, Manufacturing, and Civilian Services all continue their festival preparation around the incident, because a healthy town handles a scare without stopping.

### Day Three — Midday

The midday Commons carries the emotional aftertaste of the morning's scare, resolved into relief and gratitude. People check on Mira. Rowan holds her a little longer than usual. Iris uses the committee meal to confirm the celebration is ready. The afternoon returns everyone to work.

### Day Three — Evening and Night

The evening is warm with the particular closeness that follows a shared fright with a happy ending. Music, gardens, worship, and family. By 22:00 the ship quiets. The night shift carries the newly joined anomaly threads onward.

### Day Three — The Anomaly

On Day Three the anomaly earns its first cross-department meeting. Three fragments become one topic. Nobody believes there is danger, and given the evidence, nobody reasonably could. The phenomenon is now something the ship is officially, if quietly, watching.

### Day Three — Character Presence

Elian gives the announcement and walks the day; Iris confirms the festival; Gideon reviews the cross-department note; Naomi keeps readiness current. Rowan reaches Mira in the locked passage and does not let his fear show until after. Mara brings her drift to the joint meeting. Samira stands ready during the emergency. Jonah coordinates the response. Talia clears the route. Soren brings his data to the meeting. Mira has her frightening morning and is safe by midday. Faience remains absent, though the passage Mira entered is the kind of forgotten, in-between place where, after the attack, the ship's new silences will live.

### Day Three — The Sound of the Day

The soundscape holds the sharp, coordinated sound of the emergency response inside an otherwise festive day, and resolves into the softer sound of relief. Beneath everything the anomalous note persists and has grown again. The day teaches the player what Hearth sounds like when it protects one of its own.

### Day Three — Environmental Storytelling

The countdown board reads three days. The mural is finished and signed by its young artists. The garden crop is ready for harvest. The restricted passage where Mira was trapped gains a new, more visible safety notice by evening, an environmental scar from the morning's scare.

### Day Three — Accessibility in Practice

The emergency response is a portrait of accessibility as ordinary competence. Samira communicates through her projector under pressure without friction. The evacuation and safety systems are designed to be usable by everyone. Mira's own interface lets her tell her rescuers what happened. Nothing about the response treats disability as a complication, because the ship was built by people who never assumed a standard body.

### Day Three — Cross-Perspective Anchor Scenes

The Day Three anchors are the 06:30 announcement, the midday Commons, and, uniquely, the little emergency, which every profession experiences from its own angle: Maintenance frees the child, Medical stands ready, Security clears the route, Command coordinates, and Civilian perspectives watch a neighbor's child be saved. The cross-department anomaly meeting is a further anchor for the Engineer, Scientist, and Command perspectives.

### Day Three — Purpose, Goals, and End State

The narrative purpose of Day Three is to make the community's love for its own members vivid and to let the anomaly become an acknowledged, un-alarming topic. Its gameplay purpose is to introduce urgency without combat through the little emergency. Its emotional goal is the fierce tenderness of a community that protects its children. The end state of Day Three is a ship three days from a celebration, closer than ever after a shared fright, quietly watching a phenomenon it has every reason to believe is harmless.

## Day Two

Day Two is the sixth recorded day, two days before the loss. It is the day the evidence grows without becoming proof, and the day the community's excitement for Founding Day becomes almost unbearable to read in hindsight.

### Day Two — Before Dawn and the Night Shift

At 05:30 the night shift hands over with the anomaly now on both watches' informal agenda. The overnight teams gather a little more data and confirm the phenomenon is persistent rather than a fluke. It is still, they agree, far too little to act on. It is simply no longer possible to dismiss.

### Day Two — The Morning Announcement

At 06:30 Elian speaks on the theme of memory:

"Good morning, everyone. This is Captain Elian of the Vale System. It is now 0630 Ship Standard Time. Two days until Founding Day. My grandmother served on the crew that first crewed this ship, and she told me the ship remembers everyone who ever cared for her, in the wear of the handrails and the paths worn into the decks. I believe her. Every repair, every harvest, every lesson taught in our schools becomes part of Hearth's memory. Be worth remembering today. Morning shift begins in thirty minutes. Have a good morning, everyone."

### Day Two — Morning Routines Across Hearth

The decks wake into near-festival fervor. Children can barely be kept to their routines. The commute is joyful. The Commons gathers around a celebration space fully dressed for the coming day.

### Day Two — The Working Departments

In Research, Engineering, and Flight Operations, the evidence grows. The reading is stronger, the drift more consistent, the navigation inconsistencies more frequent. The professionals watching it agree it is real and agree it is unexplained, and they still cannot say what it is or whether it matters. They decide the responsible next step is a proper interdisciplinary meeting, and they begin to arrange one. Gideon, reviewing the accumulated notes, supports the idea and asks to attend.

Maintenance works its list. Medical holds clinic. Security patrols the now-familiar restricted zone. The ship runs beautifully, two days from its biggest celebration, around a mystery it has finally decided deserves a real conversation.

### Day Two — The Living Departments

Education holds its last full school day before the festival, and the children's Founding Day songs are ready. Hydroponics harvests the festival crop. Cargo distributes festival supplies across the decks. Transportation publishes the celebration-day schedule. Manufacturing produces the last decorative pieces. Civilian Services confirms every element of the program.

### Day Two — Midday

The midday Commons is nearly a celebration in itself. Iris announces the finalized program. Rowan checks on Mira, who is entirely swept up in festival excitement now, the frightening morning of Day Three already a fading memory. The afternoon returns everyone to work, though the whole ship is leaning toward the day after tomorrow.

### Day Two — Evening and Night

The evening is a dress rehearsal in all but name. Music, gardens, worship, and family, all pointed at Founding Day. By 22:00 the ship quiets, humming with anticipation. The night shift carries the strengthening anomaly and the arrangements for tomorrow's meeting into the dark.

### Day Two — The Anomaly

On Day Two the evidence grows but remains insufficient for any conclusion. The phenomenon is now undeniably real to the handful of professionals tracking it, and still entirely unexplained. Its only consequence so far is a decision to hold a proper meeting. That decision is the most the crew ever manages, and through no fault of theirs it comes one day too late.

### Day Two — Character Presence

Elian gives the announcement and walks the day; Iris announces the festival program; Gideon asks to join the coming meeting; Naomi keeps readiness current. Rowan briefs Maintenance and shares Mira's excitement. Mara confirms the drift is consistent. Samira holds clinic. Jonah organizes the festival's logistics. Talia patrols. Soren helps arrange the interdisciplinary meeting. Mira is joyfully lost in festival anticipation. Faience remains absent, a silence about to be broken by something no one is expecting.

### Day Two — The Sound of the Day

The soundscape is the fullest and happiest of the entire week, saturated with rehearsal, song, and preparation. The anomalous note beneath it is now clearly grown for any player who has tracked it, a wrongness the joyful surface cannot quite drown. The dissonance between the two is at its sharpest here, the day before the last day.

### Day Two — Environmental Storytelling

The countdown board reads two days. The celebration space is fully decorated. The harvested crop is stored for the feast. The children's songs echo from the education decks. Every corner of the ship is dressed for a party. Nothing anywhere suggests an ending.

### Day Two — Accessibility in Practice

Accessibility remains ordinary and total. The festival itself, harvest, songs, decorations, program, and celebration space, has been built accessible from the ground up, so that on Founding Day every resident, regardless of body or mind or means of communication, would have been able to take full part. That the day never comes is the point.

### Day Two — Cross-Perspective Anchor Scenes

The Day Two anchors are the 06:30 announcement and the midday Commons. The Engineer, Scientist, and Command perspectives additionally live the strengthening evidence and the arranging of the interdisciplinary meeting. Every perspective lives the near-unbearable anticipation of a celebration one day closer.

### Day Two — Purpose, Goals, and End State

The narrative purpose of Day Two is to raise the community's hope to its highest sustainable point and to bring the crew to the exact edge of understanding the danger. Its gameplay purpose is to let the player feel a ship completely ready for joy. Its emotional goal is anticipation so warm it aches in hindsight. The end state of Day Two is a decorated ship, a stored harvest, a scheduled meeting, and one last ordinary night before the last ordinary day.

## Day One

Day One is the seventh recorded day and the last full day of ordinary life aboard CSV Hearth. It is the eve of Founding Day. It is written to be the absolute emotional peak of the week: the most community, the most friendship, the most hope, the most laughter, and the most certainty that Hearth has decades of life ahead of it. Everything about Day One is designed to convince the player, completely, that tomorrow will be the best day of the year. Tomorrow is Attack Day.

### Day One — Before Dawn and the Night Shift

At 05:30 the night shift hands over for the last time. The overnight teams complete the final preparations that the morning festival would have required and confirm, once more, that the anomaly is real, persistent, and unexplained. The interdisciplinary meeting is set for the following morning. The night crews go off shift looking forward to attending it, and to the celebration afterward.

### Day One — The Morning Announcement

At 06:30 the two-tone claxon sounds for the last time, and Captain Elian of the Vale System gives the last morning announcement anyone aboard Hearth will ever hear, on the theme of civilization:

"Good morning, everyone. This is Captain Elian of the Vale System. It is now 0630 Ship Standard Time. Tomorrow is Founding Day. Tomorrow we celebrate the simple, impossible thing we have made together out here in the dark: a civilization. Not a ship, a civilization, with schools and gardens and songs and neighbors and a place set at the table for everyone. Whatever you do today, do it knowing that this, all of this, is what people dreamed of when they first looked up. We are the dream. Rest well tonight. Tomorrow, we celebrate what we are. Morning shift begins in thirty minutes. Have a good morning, everyone."

This is the announcement the timeline is built to make devastating. On first hearing it is warm and hopeful. After the player has finished Operation One, it becomes almost unbearable, because tomorrow never comes, the celebration never happens, and this is the last time Elian's voice ever fills the corridors of Hearth.

### Day One — Morning Routines Across Hearth

The decks wake into the fullest joy of the week. It is the eve of Founding Day and the ship can barely contain itself. Children are impossible. Families make plans for tomorrow. The commute is a celebration in miniature. The Commons overflows.

### Day One — The Working Departments

Even on the eve of the festival, the ship runs. Maintenance completes its final checks so that nothing will break during the celebration. Engineering confirms every system is ready for a day of heavy communal use. Medical prepares for the ordinary scrapes and overindulgences of a festival. Security plans the celebration's crowd flow. Research, Engineering, and Flight Operations confirm tomorrow morning's interdisciplinary meeting, intending to settle the anomaly question before the festival begins. Command, through Gideon, signs off on the meeting and turns its attention to the celebration.

### Day One — The Living Departments

Education holds a half day and then releases the children into festival preparation. Hydroponics dresses the gardens for tomorrow's gatherings. Cargo makes its final festival deliveries. Transportation locks the celebration-day schedule. Manufacturing hangs the last decorations. Civilian Services completes the program and opens the eve-of-festival gatherings.

### Day One — Midday

The midday Commons is the warmest moment of the entire week. The celebration space is finished and glowing. Iris walks the committee through tomorrow one last time. Rowan and Mira eat together, and Mira tells her father, in her own way, everything she is looking forward to tomorrow. Every friendship in the timeline is visible in this room, at this meal, on this day. The afternoon eases the ship toward its last ordinary evening.

### Day One — Evening and Night

The evening of Day One is the emotional summit of Operation One's ordinary life. There is music everywhere. The gardens are full. Worship is joyful. Families make plans for a tomorrow they are certain will come. Neighbors who were strangers on Day Seven say goodnight as friends. Slowly, happily, the ship goes to bed early, to be rested for Founding Day. By 22:00 Hearth is quiet, full, and hopeful. The last night shift takes its posts. The interdisciplinary meeting waits for morning. So does the anomaly.

### Day One — The Anomaly

On Day One the anomaly does only one thing: it becomes the subject of a meeting scheduled for tomorrow morning. Everyone who has tracked it agrees that tomorrow they will finally sit down, compare everything, and decide what it is. Tomorrow never arrives. The meeting is the crew's final, reasonable, responsible act toward a danger that gives them no more time.

### Day One — Character Presence

Elian gives the last announcement and walks the last ordinary day; Iris readies the festival; Gideon confirms tomorrow's meeting; Naomi, unknowingly, spends the last quiet day before she will be needed most. Rowan finishes his checks and shares the meal with Mira. Mara confirms the ship is ready and plans to bring her drift data to the morning meeting. Samira prepares Medical for a festival. Jonah organizes the celebration logistics. Talia plans the crowd flow. Soren readies his anomaly data for the meeting. Mira spends a perfect eve-of-festival day with her father and her friends. Faience remains absent for one more night, the last night the ship is whole.

### Day One — The Sound of the Day

The soundscape of Day One is the richest, warmest, and most crowded of the week, a whole civilization audibly happy on the eve of its celebration. The anomalous note is still there beneath it, but the joy is so total that even an attentive player may let themselves stop hearing it. That is deliberate. The timeline wants the player, like the crew, to be looking the other way when morning comes.

### Day One — Environmental Storytelling

The countdown board reads one day. The celebration space is finished and beautiful. The gardens are dressed. The decorations are hung. The children's songs are ready. The mural of Hearth is complete. Everywhere the player looks there is a future being prepared, a message from a community absolutely certain of its tomorrow.

### Day One — Accessibility in Practice

Accessibility is total and invisible on Day One. Tomorrow's festival is designed so that every resident could take full part. The eve-of-festival gatherings are already accessible to everyone present. On the last full day of Hearth's life, the ship is at its most completely inclusive, a finished portrait of the civilization Elian names in the morning announcement.

### Day One — Cross-Perspective Anchor Scenes

The Day One anchors are the final 06:30 announcement, heard shipwide, and the midday Commons at the warmest moment of the week. Every profession lives both. Every profession spends the day certain of tomorrow. These two anchors are the last time every perspective stands in the same ordinary light before everything changes.

### Day One — Purpose, Goals, and End State

The narrative purpose of Day One is to raise ordinary life to its absolute peak so that its loss is total. Its gameplay purpose is to give the player one last, unpressured day fully at home in a ship they now know by sound. Its emotional goal is joy so complete that it becomes grief in memory. The end state of Day One is a sleeping, hopeful, finished civilization, one night from a celebration, one night from the attack, entirely unaware that it has just lived its last ordinary day.

## Attack Day

Attack Day is the last entry in Operation One's chronology and the morning CSV Hearth was lost. It is also Founding Day, the celebration the entire week has been preparing for. It is written minute by minute, tracking every department, every captain, and every primary character at once, because the horror of the day is precisely that it happened to everyone simultaneously and that no one could see all of it. The player lives only one thread of this morning. This timeline records all of them, so that every perspective can be placed exactly against the same clock.

### Attack Day — Before Dawn and the Last Ordinary Hours

At 05:30 the night shift begins its final handover. The ship is dressed for Founding Day. The celebration space stands ready. The gardens are prepared. The interdisciplinary anomaly meeting is set for shortly after the morning announcement, before the festivities begin. The night crews are tired and happy. Nothing on any instrument suggests that the last hour of Hearth's ordinary life is already running.

By 06:00 the earliest festival volunteers are awake and moving. Bakers in the Commons begin the feast. Hydroponics cuts the last fresh greens. Children who could not sleep for excitement are already up. For thirty more minutes, Hearth is exactly what it has always been.

### Attack Day — The Announcement That Never Finished

At 06:30 the two-tone claxon sounds. Captain Elian of the Vale System begins the Founding Day morning announcement, warm and joyful, wishing the ship a happy Founding Day.

At 06:32, partway through Elian's words, the anomaly stops being a mystery. Every instrument that had held a fragment of it registers the same thing at once. The announcement stops mid-sentence. For a few seconds there is only the open channel and the sound of the bridge realizing what it is seeing. It is the last time the ship hears Elian's daily voice.

### Attack Day — First Contact With the Phenomenon

At 06:33 the phenomenon reveals its true nature. What the week recorded as drift, noise, and inconsistency resolves into a single unprecedented event unfolding faster than any framework aboard can classify. Sensors across Research, Engineering, and Flight Operations saturate. The ship's harmonics, the notes Mara knows by heart, break.

At 06:34 the first hostile action strikes Hearth. The nature of the attack is beyond anything the crew has trained for. Systems that have never failed begin to fail together. No department misreads the situation through incompetence. The situation is simply without precedent, exactly as the anomaly thread across the week has established.

### Attack Day — General Quarters

At 06:35 command shifts. The Vale System moves from Elian to Naomi mid-crisis, the warm morning voice replaced by the lower, firmer emergency voice, and Captain Naomi of the Vale System calls General Quarters. The claxon changes from the gentle two-tone morning chime to the emergency pattern the whole ship knows and has drilled but never truly needed.

Naomi's orders are short and clear: damage control to their stations, medical to triage, security to the evacuation routes, all civilians to the escape assembly points, all non-essential personnel off the working decks. The drills the crew has practiced in calm now run under fire. Because they were practiced, they run.

### Attack Day — Department by Department

From 06:35 the timeline tracks every department at once.

- Command holds the bridge under Naomi, coordinating damage control and evacuation, while Gideon reads the failing systems for any pattern that can buy time and Iris takes the civilian channels to keep the assembly points calm. Elian does not front again; the office has become an emergency, and the emergency belongs to Naomi.
- Engineering, under Mara Chen, fights to hold power and life support long enough for evacuation. Mara works by sound in a bay where the displays mean nothing, hearing failures before they cascade and rerouting what can still be routed. She keeps the lights and the air alive far longer than the damage should allow.
- Maintenance, under Rowan Ives, does what Maintenance always does and no one ever sees: opening the hidden routes, freeing jammed pressure doors, and clearing the crawlspaces and service corridors so that evacuation paths stay open. Rowan's knowledge of the ship as a body saves people whose names he will never learn. His first thought, and his first radio call, is for Mira.
- Medical, under Doctor Samira Kade, runs triage as the wounded arrive. Samira directs a chaotic bay through her projector, sign and text cutting through noise that has stopped meaning anything, communication holding when voices cannot.
- Research, under Dr. Soren Qadir, abandons the investigation that is now moot and turns to preserving what can be preserved and evacuating its people. Soren refuses to leave until his team is away, his chair carrying him through corridors he helped restrict.
- Security, under Talia Rusk, runs the evacuation routes and the assembly points, moving civilians toward the pods, holding order where panic would kill, carrying those who cannot move themselves.
- Logistics and operations, under Chief Petty Officer Jonah Briggs, become the nervous system of the evacuation, tracking who has reached which pod and which routes are still open, turning Naomi's orders into a moving map of survival.
- Education, Hydroponics, Cargo, Transportation, Manufacturing, and Civilian Services empty toward the assembly points. Teachers carry and account for their students. The festival crowd becomes an evacuation. The celebration space, finished only yesterday, stands empty as the ship dies around it.

### Attack Day — The Civilians and the Children

From 06:37 the civilian evacuation is the heart of the tragedy and the proof of the ship's design. The accessible emergency systems built into every deck do exactly what they were built for. Haptic alarms, spoken and captioned instructions, accessible routes, and adaptive transport move a whole town toward the pods without leaving anyone behind for lack of a standard body. The civilization Elian named that morning is, in its last hour, still trying to save every single one of its members.

Mira Ives is with the other children when General Quarters sounds. Her teachers account for her. Rowan reaches her assembly point through the service corridors he knows better than anyone. For a moment, father and daughter are together in the chaos. Then the evacuation separates them, as it separates every primary member of the crew, each toward a different pod.

### Attack Day — Evacuation and the Escape Pods

From 06:40 the escape pods and survival craft launch in waves. This is the canon established in Chapter One: when Hearth is lost, each primary crew member escapes in a separate pod, scattered across the dark, which is why Chapter Two is the work of finding and rescuing each of them one by one.

The scatter is recorded here so that every later rescue is consistent with it:

- Rowan Ives launches from a maintenance-adjacent pod after holding an evacuation route open to the last safe moment.
- Mara Chen launches from Engineering only once life support can hold no longer, having kept the ship breathing for everyone who got out ahead of her.
- Doctor Samira Kade launches from near Medical after the last of her walking wounded are away.
- Chief Petty Officer Jonah Briggs launches only after his tracking says the routes he was responsible for are clear.
- Security Lead Talia Rusk launches from an evacuation route after pushing civilians ahead of herself.
- Dr. Soren Qadir launches from a research-section pod after his team is away.
- Mira Ives launches from a civilian pod, separated from her father in the evacuation.
- The Vale System, one body in the captain's chair, holds the bridge under Naomi through the evacuation and launches last among the primary crew, command staying with the ship until the ship can no longer be commanded.
- The player, in the maintenance perspective, is swept into a separate, damaged pod in the final moments, the pod Chapter Two opens inside.

### Attack Day — The First Voice in the Dark

At the edge of the evacuation, in a damaged pod, the presence the player will come to know as Faience stirs for the first time. Across the entire week Faience was absent, the silence where a voice would someday be. In the wreck of Founding Day, in a broken craft spinning away from a dying ship, that silence finally ends. This is the first faint stirring of Faience, and it is the seam where Operation One's ordinary life becomes the story of what comes after. Nothing about Faience is explained here. The timeline records only that the silence broke on Attack Day, and not one moment sooner.

### Attack Day — The Final Moments of CSV Hearth

In the last minutes, with the pods away, CSV Hearth ends. The ship that carried a whole civilization, that woke every morning at 06:30 to Elian's voice, that remembered its people in the wear of its handrails, is lost with the celebration still hung in its Commons and the feast still warm in its galley. The registry CSV-0101-H goes silent. The single timeline of Hearth as a living home ends here.

### Attack Day — The Player Wakes Alone

Operation One's chronology ends at the exact point Chapter Two begins. The player regains consciousness alone inside the damaged escape pod. The ship is gone. The crew is scattered across the dark in pods of their own. The morning announcement will not come. The only thing in the pod is the player, the damage, and the first faint presence of Faience. Everything the player does from this moment forward belongs to Chapter Two and to the long work of finding everyone again.

### Attack Day — Purpose, Goals, and End State

The narrative purpose of Attack Day is to destroy, in a single unprecedented morning, the home the week so carefully built, without letting a single character die of incompetence. Its gameplay purpose is to turn every ordinary skill the week taught, reading the ship by sound, knowing its hidden routes, trusting its people, into the tools of survival and evacuation. Its emotional goal is total loss made bearable only by the knowledge that the crew scattered rather than died, so that hope survives as the work of reunion. The end state of Attack Day, and of Operation One's chronology, is a single damaged pod, a single waking survivor, a scattered crew waiting to be found, and a new voice beginning to speak in the dark.

## End State

This document is the definitive historical record of Operation One: Exodus. It fixes the seven ordinary days aboard CSV Hearth and the morning they ended into a single, unchanging order.

Every future campaign depicts this week and this morning from a different profession's perspective, and none of them changes what happened. Every future operation treats this timeline as settled history. Every flashback, every cinematic, and every conversation that reaches back to Hearth reaches back to the events recorded here, in this order, with these people in these places.

Perspective changes. Knowledge changes. History does not.
