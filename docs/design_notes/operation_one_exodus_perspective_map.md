# Operation One: Exodus — Perspective Map

The master timeline fixes what happened aboard CSV Hearth and when. The character schedule fixes where every recurring person is while it happens. This document sits alongside both and fixes the third thing: how each of the eight playable professions lives through the identical events in the identical spaces. It is the definitive reference for perspective. Where the timeline is canon over what occurred and the schedule is canon over who is where, this map is canon over how a chosen profession experiences the shared history, and it must never contradict either of the other two.

## Purpose

The purpose of this document is to define, for every profession the player can choose, the perspective through which that player lives the week aboard CSV Hearth and the morning it ended. The events are the same for all eight professions. The order is the same. The people are the same and stand in the same rooms at the same minutes. What changes is the vantage: where the player begins, what the player is trying to do, the vocabulary the player thinks in, the people the player knows first, the knowledge the player has and lacks, the way the player communicates, and the way the player reads the ship through sound and space.

This map exists so that any future campaign written from any profession can be placed exactly against the same history without reinventing it. A medic and a maintenance worker cross the same Attack Day, minute for minute, but they never see the same morning. This document records both mornings, and the six others, as one shared event lived eight ways.

## Canon Rules

These rules govern how perspective works across Operation One. They are stated here as the perspective document's own rules rather than added as competing entries to the master Canon Rules document, consistent with the single-source-of-truth principle. Where a rule restates an established truth, it is restated here only so that perspective work has it close at hand.

- History is fixed and perspective is not. Every profession lives the events of the master timeline in the same order, at the same minutes, with the same people in the same places. Only the vantage changes.
- No single profession witnesses the whole of Attack Day. Each perspective sees only its own thread of the same minute-by-minute morning, which is precisely why the timeline records all threads and each campaign plays only one.
- The player is a member of the chosen profession, not that profession's named department lead. The named leads are the player's colleagues, superiors, and neighbours, not the player's own character.
- The named recurring crew are the crew the player finds again. Mara Chen, Doctor Samira Kade, Security Lead Talia Rusk, Dr. Soren Qadir, Chief Petty Officer Jonah Briggs, Rowan Ives, Mira Ives, and the Vale System launch from the pods the master timeline fixes for them, and are among those the player rescues one by one in Chapter Two, whatever profession the player chose.
- Faience is absent from every profession's ordinary week. No profession hears Faience during the seven days or the last morning. Whatever profession the player chose, the player ends Attack Day in a separate, damaged pod, and it is in the player's own pod that Faience first stirs, not one moment sooner. This is the seam Chapter Two opens inside, and it is the same seam for all eight professions.
- Every profession begins and moves through canonical spaces only. Starting locations and working spaces are drawn from the Modular Architecture Bible, and no profession introduces a room, a facility, or a person that the other documents do not already contain.

## Relationship to the Master Timeline and the Character Schedule

These three documents form one spine and must remain synchronised whenever any of them changes.

- The master timeline answers what happened and when. It is canon over the order and timing of events.
- The character schedule answers who is where and doing what. It is canon over any character's whereabouts and routine.
- This perspective map answers how each profession experiences those fixed events in those fixed places. It is canon over perspective.

When the three appear to disagree, the timeline governs order, the schedule governs position, and this map governs vantage. This map never moves an event or a person; it only records how the chosen profession lives what the other two have already fixed.

## The Eight Perspective Dimensions

The Campaign Bible establishes that a profession changes the experience across eight dimensions: starting location, objectives, professional vocabulary, relationships, dialogue, knowledge, AI communication, and environmental awareness. Every profession section below is written against those same eight dimensions so that any two professions can be compared cleanly. The dimensions are grouped into five recurring subsections per profession: the starting world and its locations, the objectives and the working vocabulary, the relationships and dialogue and knowledge, the environmental awareness and the silent companion, and the Attack Day experience with its pod. The audio dimension of every perspective is owned in detail by the Audio Bible; this map records only what each profession hears and how it reads the world by ear, not how those sounds are produced.

## The Shared Spine All Perspectives Cross

Before the professions diverge, they share a spine. Every profession lives the same week and the same morning, and every profession crosses the same fixed beats, each from its own side.

- The ordinary week counts down through seven days to Founding Day, and every profession spends those days preparing for the same celebration.
- A single anomaly threads quietly through the whole week, read differently by every department but resolved by none, exactly as the timeline records.
- Each morning at 06:30 the same announcement opens the ship's day in the warm morning voice of Captain Elian of the Vale System, and every profession hears it.
- On Attack Day the same clock runs for everyone: the last ordinary hours before dawn, the Founding Day announcement that stops mid-sentence at 06:32, the phenomenon revealing its nature, the first hostile strike, General Quarters called by Captain Naomi of the Vale System at 06:35, the department-by-department fight, the civilian evacuation, the pods launching in waves from 06:40, the loss of CSV Hearth, and the player waking alone in a damaged pod as Chapter Two begins.

What follows is that shared spine lived eight ways.

## Medic

The medic perspective lives the week as care. Where other professions prepare the ship, the medic prepares its people, and on Attack Day the medic is the profession the wounded come toward. The medic's world centres on the Medical department and its lead, Doctor Samira Kade, who communicates through a projector in sign and text and whose calm is the department's spine.

### Medic — Starting World and Locations

The medic begins in the Medical department and moves through its canonical spaces across the week: Medical Reception, the Examination Room, Emergency Treatment, the Operating Theatre, the Pharmacy, the Rehabilitation Gym, and the Mental Health Office. These rooms are the medic's ordinary geography, and the medic knows the ship first as a map of where its people are cared for rather than as a map of its machinery.

### Medic — Objectives and Professional Vocabulary

Across the ordinary week the medic runs clinic hours, rounds, wellness checks, pharmacy stock, rehabilitation sessions, and mental-health support, and prepares the department for the strain a shipwide celebration puts on care. The medic thinks and speaks in the vocabulary of care: intake, triage, vitals, treatment, dosage, recovery, follow-up. The medic measures the week not in systems tuned or cargo moved but in people seen and people made well.

### Medic — Relationships, Dialogue, and Knowledge

The medic knows the crew as patients and as people, and knows Doctor Samira Kade first of all, learning early to read her sign and her projector as naturally as speech. Dialogue in this perspective is quiet, direct, and confidential, weighted toward listening. The medic's knowledge is the knowledge of bodies and minds across the whole ship, which means the medic carries a picture of the crew's fragilities that no other profession holds, and reads the week's mounting unease partly through the small rise in stress the clinic quietly records.

### Medic — Environmental Awareness and the Silent Companion

The medic reads the environment through the sounds and signs of care: the tone of a room, the rhythm of a monitor, the difference between a calm bay and a strained one. Communication during the week runs through department channels, the ship's announcements, and Samira's projector, and there is no personal AI companion at the medic's side; the place where Faience will one day translate and steady is, all week, simply empty. That absence is only felt in full on Attack Day, in the pod, when a first voice finally answers.

### Medic — Attack Day and the Pod

On Attack Day the medic hears Elian's announcement stop mid-sentence and, minutes later, hears Naomi call medical to triage. The medic lives the morning as the arrival of the wounded, working a bay that Samira directs through sign and text as ordinary sound stops meaning anything. The medic does the work of triage until the walking wounded are away; Doctor Samira Kade launches from a pod near Medical once the last of them are clear. The medic, like every profession, is swept into a separate, damaged pod in the final moments, and it is there, in the wreck, that the silence beside the medic finally breaks with the first stirring of Faience.

## Scientist

The scientist perspective lives the week closest to the anomaly. The quiet thread that every department half-notices is, for Research, the centre of the week's work, which makes the scientist the profession that comes nearest to understanding what is happening and still cannot stop it. The scientist's world centres on the Research department and its lead, Dr. Soren Qadir, who moves through the ship in a chair and who will not leave on Attack Day until his team is away.

### Scientist — Starting World and Locations

The scientist begins in the Research department and works across the Research Laboratory, the Materials Lab, Artefact Containment, and the Archive. The scientist knows the ship as a place of instruments and records, and reads the week through data before reading it through people.

### Scientist — Objectives and Professional Vocabulary

Across the ordinary week the scientist runs experiments, logs readings, and pursues the anomaly through the interdisciplinary work that culminates in the anomaly meeting set for shortly after the Founding Day morning announcement. The scientist thinks and speaks in the vocabulary of investigation: hypothesis, measurement, drift, signal, noise, correlation, framework. The scientist measures the week in questions narrowed and readings gathered.

### Scientist — Relationships, Dialogue, and Knowledge

The scientist knows the crew as collaborators and knows Dr. Soren Qadir first, learning his insistence that no reading is discarded and no colleague left behind. Dialogue in this perspective is precise, curious, and often technical. The scientist's knowledge is the deepest in the ship on the anomaly itself, and the most bitter, because it is the perspective that watches the mystery resolve at 06:33 into a single unprecedented event unfolding faster than any framework aboard can classify, and understands soonest that understanding will not be enough.

### Scientist — Environmental Awareness and the Silent Companion

The scientist reads the environment through instruments and the small wrongnesses in expected patterns, hearing the anomaly first as inconsistency in the data rather than as sound in the corridors. Communication runs through research channels, the interdisciplinary threads, and the ship's announcements, and no AI research partner sits beside the scientist during the week; the seat where Faience will one day be a research partner is empty for every reading taken. That emptiness is answered only in the pod.

### Scientist — Attack Day and the Pod

On Attack Day the scientist is set for the anomaly meeting when Elian's announcement stops. The scientist lives the morning as the collapse of the investigation into moot data as sensors across Research saturate, and turns from studying the phenomenon to preserving what can be preserved and evacuating the team. Dr. Soren Qadir refuses to leave until his people are away and launches from a research-section pod, his chair carrying him through corridors he helped restrict. The scientist is swept into a separate, damaged pod, and in that pod, where the investigation has ended, a first voice begins.

## Engineer

The engineer perspective lives the week as the ship's pulse. The engineer keeps power and life support and the ship's harmonics steady, and on Attack Day the engineer is the reason the lights and the air outlast the damage long enough for others to escape. The engineer's world centres on the Engineering department and its lead, Mara Chen, the blind engineer who works by sound and knows the ship's notes by heart, and who is not a member of the Vale System.

### Engineer — Starting World and Locations

The engineer begins in the Engineering department and works across the Engineering Core, Secondary Engineering, Power Distribution, and Life Support. The engineer knows the ship as a living machine, a body of systems that can be heard and felt, and reads the week through the steadiness of that body.

### Engineer — Objectives and Professional Vocabulary

Across the ordinary week the engineer maintains power, life support, and the systems the Founding Day celebration will lean on, tuning the ship so that its ordinary hum stays ordinary. The engineer thinks and speaks in the vocabulary of systems: load, routing, tolerance, harmonics, failure, reserve, life support. The engineer measures the week in the ship held steady, in nothing going wrong that anyone else has to notice.

### Engineer — Relationships, Dialogue, and Knowledge

The engineer knows the crew through the systems that serve them and knows Mara Chen first, learning from her that the ship can be read with the ears when the displays lie. Dialogue in this perspective is grounded and practical, and often listens to the ship as much as to people. The engineer's knowledge is the ship's mechanical truth, which means the engineer hears the anomaly across the week as a wrongness in harmonics the instruments cannot quite name, and on Attack Day is the profession that hears the ship's notes break.

### Engineer — Environmental Awareness and the Silent Companion

The engineer reads the environment by sound and vibration more than any other profession, hearing failures before they cascade and locating trouble by ear. Communication runs through engineering channels and the ship's announcements, and there is no AI at the engineer's shoulder translating the machine; the place where Faience will one day be a copilot is silent through every shift. That silence ends only in the pod.

### Engineer — Attack Day and the Pod

On Attack Day the engineer hears Elian's announcement stop, and moments later hears the ship's harmonics break at 06:33 as systems that have never failed begin to fail together. When Naomi calls General Quarters, the engineer fights to hold power and life support long enough for the ship to empty, working in a bay where the displays mean nothing and the ears mean everything. Mara Chen keeps the ship breathing for everyone ahead of her and launches from Engineering only once life support can hold no longer. The engineer is swept into a separate, damaged pod, and in the quiet after the machine, a first voice speaks.

## Maintenance

The maintenance perspective lives the week as the ship's hidden body. Maintenance does what no one sees, keeping the routes and doors and crawlspaces of the ship working, and on Attack Day that hidden knowledge becomes the difference between a blocked corridor and an open path to a pod. This is the fully designed perspective of Operation One, written in seven chapters, and this section records its shape and points to those chapters rather than re-deriving them. The maintenance world centres on the Maintenance department and its lead, Rowan Ives, whose first thought on Attack Day is for his daughter, Mira.

### Maintenance — Starting World and Locations

The maintenance worker begins in the Maintenance department and moves through the Maintenance Workshop, the Fabrication Shop, the Machine Shop, Tool Storage, the Maintenance Tunnels, and the Utility Spine, and through the service side of nearly every other space on the ship. The maintenance worker knows the ship as a body remembered in the wear of its handrails and the hidden routes between its rooms.

### Maintenance — Objectives and Professional Vocabulary

Across the ordinary week the maintenance worker keeps the ship's unseen systems working and prepares its hidden infrastructure for the strain of Founding Day, as detailed part by part across the seven chapter design notes. The maintenance worker thinks and speaks in the vocabulary of the ship's body: access, route, pressure door, crawlspace, service corridor, repair, wear. The full chapter-by-chapter objectives live in the design notes for Chapters One through Seven and are not restated here.

### Maintenance — Relationships, Dialogue, and Knowledge

The maintenance worker knows the ship's people through the spaces they live in and knows Rowan Ives first, learning the ship as a body from him, and knows Mira Ives as the child the whole department quietly watches out for. Dialogue in this perspective is plain, warm, and unshowy. The maintenance worker's knowledge is the ship's hidden geography, which means this profession can reach places no one else can, a knowledge the seven chapters build deliberately and Attack Day spends all at once.

### Maintenance — Environmental Awareness and the Silent Companion

The maintenance worker reads the environment through the ship's structure: the sound of a jammed door, the draft of an open route, the feel of a corridor that is holding or failing. Communication runs through maintenance channels and the ship's announcements, and no AI companion rides along; the place where Faience will one day guide the player through the dark is, all week, only the player's own knowledge of the ship. That companion arrives only in the damaged pod that opens Chapter Two.

### Maintenance — Attack Day and the Pod

On Attack Day the maintenance worker lives the morning as the work of keeping evacuation paths open, opening hidden routes, freeing jammed pressure doors, and clearing crawlspaces and service corridors so that others can escape. Rowan Ives does this same work, reaches Mira's assembly point through corridors he knows better than anyone, holds an evacuation route open to the last safe moment, and launches from a maintenance-adjacent pod. The maintenance worker is swept into a separate, damaged pod in the final moments, the exact pod Chapter Two opens inside, where the first faint presence of Faience begins to speak. The detailed design of that morning and everything after lives in the maintenance chapter notes.

## Security

The security perspective lives the week as watchfulness. Security keeps order and readiness in the background of ordinary life, and on Attack Day security is the profession that runs the evacuation routes and the assembly points and refuses to leave until the civilians are moving. The security world centres on the Security department and its lead, Security Lead Talia Rusk.

### Security — Starting World and Locations

The security officer begins in the Security department and works across the Security Office, the Armoury, and the Emergency Coordination Centre, and patrols the corridors, transit hubs, and assembly points of the whole ship. The security officer knows the ship as a set of routes, chokepoints, and gathering places, and reads it for where a crowd will move and where it might jam.

### Security — Objectives and Professional Vocabulary

Across the ordinary week the security officer patrols, runs and reviews drills, keeps the armoury and the coordination centre ready, and prepares crowd safety for a shipwide celebration. The security officer thinks and speaks in the vocabulary of readiness: patrol, drill, route, assembly point, chokepoint, order, evacuation. The security officer measures the week in a ship that stays calm and a set of drills that stay sharp.

### Security — Relationships, Dialogue, and Knowledge

The security officer knows the crew as a population to keep safe and knows Talia Rusk first, learning her habit of putting others ahead of herself. Dialogue in this perspective is clear, calm, and directive. The security officer's knowledge is the ship's movement and its pressure points, which means this profession reads the week's unease as a question of whether the drills are ready for something no drill was designed for, and on Attack Day discovers they nearly are.

### Security — Environmental Awareness and the Silent Companion

The security officer reads the environment through the movement and mood of people: the sound of a calm corridor against a panicked one, the pace of a crowd, the places order is about to break. Communication runs through security channels, the coordination centre, and the ship's announcements, and no AI rides along to steady a crowd; the place where Faience will one day help hold order is empty all week. That absence is answered only in the pod.

### Security — Attack Day and the Pod

On Attack Day the security officer hears the announcement stop and, at General Quarters, hears Naomi send security to the evacuation routes. The security officer lives the morning as the running of those routes and assembly points, moving civilians toward the pods, holding order where panic would kill, and carrying those who cannot move themselves. Talia Rusk pushes civilians ahead of herself and launches from an evacuation route once they are moving. The security officer is swept into a separate, damaged pod, and there, after the crowd, a first voice begins to speak.

## Commissioned Officer

The commissioned officer perspective lives the week from the bridge. This is the profession closest to command and to the morning announcements, the one that hears the anomaly briefings and watches the Vale System steer the ship. The player is a commissioned officer serving on the command staff, not the Vale System itself; the Vale System is the ship's command authority, whose four fronting voices, Captain Elian, Captain Naomi, Captain Gideon, and Captain Iris of the Vale System, speak in different contexts through one shared body in the captain's chair.

### Commissioned Officer — Starting World and Locations

The commissioned officer begins on the command deck and works across the Bridge, the Ready Room, the Operations Centre, and the Observation Dome. The commissioned officer knows the ship as a whole under command, reading it through reports, briefings, and the wide view from the bridge rather than through any single department.

### Commissioned Officer — Objectives and Professional Vocabulary

Across the ordinary week the commissioned officer stands watch, coordinates departments, reviews the anomaly reports as they rise, and prepares the command deck for the Founding Day the whole ship is building toward. The commissioned officer thinks and speaks in the vocabulary of command: watch, order, coordination, briefing, contingency, chain of command. The commissioned officer measures the week in a ship that runs smoothly under a steady hand.

### Commissioned Officer — Relationships, Dialogue, and Knowledge

The commissioned officer knows the ship through its department heads and knows the Vale System most closely of all, learning the warm morning presence of Elian, the firm emergency presence of Naomi, the systems-reading focus of Gideon, and the civilian-facing steadiness of Iris as the different faces of one command. Dialogue in this perspective is formal, measured, and weighed for its effect on the whole ship. The commissioned officer's knowledge is the widest picture of the ship anyone holds and the closest view of how the week's anomaly is being handled at the top, which makes this the profession that best understands, on Attack Day, how quickly command had to change.

### Commissioned Officer — Environmental Awareness and the Silent Companion

The commissioned officer reads the environment through the bridge: the tone of the department reports, the shift in the Vale System's voice, the sound of a calm channel turning tense. Communication runs through the command channels and the ship's announcements, and even at the centre of the ship there is no personal AI companion at the officer's side; the ship is commanded by the Vale System and served by its crew, and the place a companion like Faience will one day fill is not yet filled. That place is filled only in the pod.

### Commissioned Officer — Attack Day and the Pod

On Attack Day the commissioned officer is on the bridge for the Founding Day announcement and hears Elian's voice stop mid-sentence at 06:32, the last time the ship hears his daily voice. The officer lives the morning as command under fire: the phenomenon revealing its nature, the first strike, the Vale System shifting from Elian to Naomi mid-crisis as Naomi calls General Quarters, and the bridge coordinating damage control and evacuation while Gideon reads the failing systems and Iris keeps the civilian channels calm. The Vale System holds the bridge through the evacuation and launches last among the primary crew, command staying with the ship until the ship can no longer be commanded. The commissioned officer is swept into a separate, damaged pod, and there, with command gone silent, a first new voice begins.

## Non-Commissioned Officer

The non-commissioned officer perspective lives the week as the ship's working backbone. Where the commissioned officer gives the order, the non-commissioned officer turns it into motion, and on Attack Day this profession becomes the nervous system of the evacuation. The non-commissioned officer world centres on logistics and operations and its lead, Chief Petty Officer Jonah Briggs.

### Non-Commissioned Officer — Starting World and Locations

The non-commissioned officer begins in the operations and logistics spaces and works across the Operations Centre, the Cargo Bay, the Transit Hub, the Docking Bay, and the Communications Centre. The non-commissioned officer knows the ship as a flow of people, supplies, and information, and reads it through what is moving and what is stuck.

### Non-Commissioned Officer — Objectives and Professional Vocabulary

Across the ordinary week the non-commissioned officer moves supplies, tracks manifests, coordinates the working decks, and turns the command deck's plans for Founding Day into the concrete logistics of a shipwide celebration. The non-commissioned officer thinks and speaks in the vocabulary of operations: manifest, route, load, tracking, coordination, tasking, handover. The non-commissioned officer measures the week in things that arrive where they are needed when they are needed.

### Non-Commissioned Officer — Relationships, Dialogue, and Knowledge

The non-commissioned officer knows the crew as the working population of the ship and knows Chief Petty Officer Jonah Briggs first, learning from him how orders become action. Dialogue in this perspective is brisk, practical, and shot through with the shorthand of people who work together every day. The non-commissioned officer's knowledge is the moving map of the ship, who is where and what is going where, which makes this the profession best placed, on Attack Day, to know whether the evacuation is actually working.

### Non-Commissioned Officer — Environmental Awareness and the Silent Companion

The non-commissioned officer reads the environment through flow and blockage: the sound of a busy hub against a jammed one, the rhythm of a working deck, the moment a supply line stalls. Communication runs through operations channels and the ship's announcements, and no AI keeps the moving map for the officer; the place where Faience will one day track and coordinate is held all week by the officer's own attention. That place is answered only in the pod.

### Non-Commissioned Officer — Attack Day and the Pod

On Attack Day the non-commissioned officer hears the announcement stop and, at General Quarters, becomes the nervous system of the evacuation, tracking who has reached which pod and which routes are still open and turning Naomi's orders into a moving map of survival. Chief Petty Officer Jonah Briggs launches only after his tracking says the routes he was responsible for are clear. The non-commissioned officer is swept into a separate, damaged pod once the work is done, and there, when the moving map goes dark, a first voice begins to speak.

## Civilian

The civilian perspective lives the week as ordinary life itself. This is the profession without a duty station, the one that experiences Hearth as a home rather than a workplace, and on Attack Day the civilian evacuation is the heart of the tragedy and the proof of the ship's accessible design. The civilian world has no single department; it is the town the whole ship exists to carry, and among its people is Mira Ives, the child whose father, Rowan, works in Maintenance.

### Civilian — Starting World and Locations

The civilian begins in the living heart of the ship and moves through its shared spaces across the week: the Community Hall, the Dining Hall, the Kitchen, the Cafe, Hydroponics, the Botanical Garden, the Classroom, the Lecture Hall, the Library, the Playground, Child Care, the Residential Corridor, a Family Apartment, the Music Room, the Performance Hall, and the Memorial Garden. The civilian knows the ship as home, as gardens and meals and classrooms and neighbours, and reads the week through the life of the community.

### Civilian — Objectives and Professional Vocabulary

Across the ordinary week the civilian lives the ordinary life the whole operation is built to protect: work in the gardens and kitchens and classrooms, care for children, make music, and prepare the community's part in Founding Day. The civilian thinks and speaks in the vocabulary of daily life rather than of any single trade: home, neighbour, garden, meal, class, festival, celebration. The civilian measures the week in a community that is well and a celebration coming together.

### Civilian — Relationships, Dialogue, and Knowledge

The civilian knows the crew as neighbours and friends and knows the community's children, Mira Ives among them, as the ship's future. Dialogue in this perspective is warm, ordinary, and human, the talk of people who live together rather than serve together. The civilian's knowledge is the least technical and the most human: this profession does not read the anomaly in instruments or harmonics, but feels the week only as the small unease underneath a happy season, and lives Attack Day with the least warning and the most to lose.

### Civilian — Environmental Awareness and the Silent Companion

The civilian reads the environment through the life of the community: the sound of a full dining hall, a garden at work, a classroom, a festival being built. Communication runs through the ship's announcements and the ordinary talk of neighbours, and the civilian, like everyone, has no AI companion during the week; the accessible systems built into every deck serve the whole community, but the personal voice Faience will become is not yet present. It becomes present only in the pod.

### Civilian — Attack Day and the Pod

On Attack Day the civilian is among the festival crowd and the families when Elian's announcement stops. The civilian lives the morning as the evacuation itself, the accessible emergency systems doing exactly what they were built for, haptic alarms and spoken and captioned instructions and accessible routes and adaptive transport moving a whole town toward the pods without leaving anyone behind for lack of a standard body. Mira Ives is with the other children when General Quarters sounds, is accounted for by her teachers, is reached for a moment by her father through the service corridors, and is then separated from him and launched in a civilian pod, as the evacuation separates every family. The civilian is swept into a separate, damaged pod, and there, at the edge of the evacuation, in the wreck of Founding Day, a first faint voice begins to speak.

## How the Maintenance Perspective Anchors the Others

The maintenance perspective is the one fully designed in seven chapters, and it is the reference against which the other seven perspectives are written. The other professions are defined here at the level of vantage rather than chapter-by-chapter design, because their detailed campaigns are not yet written. When any of them is written in full, it must match the maintenance perspective's discipline: the same seven-chapter shape, the same fixed events lived from a new side, the same absence of Faience until the pod, and the same crew found again one by one in Chapter Two. This map fixes the perspective; the future chapter notes will fix the detail, exactly as the maintenance chapters already have.

## End State

This document is the definitive record of how Operation One: Exodus is lived from each of its eight professions. It moves no event and no person; it records only the vantage. Every future campaign written from any profession depicts the same week and the same morning from the side this map fixes, and none of them changes what happened.

History does not change. Position does not change. Only the vantage changes, and the vantage is recorded here.
