# Narrative Style Guide

This document is the definitive writing standard for the entire Resonance franchise and every future game, so that every writer, designer, and contributor renders the world in one consistent voice no matter which chapter, profession, or era they are working on.

## Foundations of Resonance narrative

Resonance is written to be understood completely without sight and completely without hearing, so no line of writing may carry information through one sense alone. Every scene is authored so that its meaning survives being heard, being read as captions, being felt as tactile output, and being read on a refreshable braille display. This is not a constraint added at the end; it is the ground the prose is built on, consistent with the accessibility-first foundation of the franchise.

The writing serves people rather than spectacle. The player is a resident of a living world, never its owner and never the chosen one, so the prose never flatters the player as the smartest person in the room and never lets a single character carry the whole truth. The world continues when the player logs out, and the writing reflects a civilisation that was living before the player arrived and keeps living after they leave.

These principles govern every category below.

- Meaning is never carried by one sense alone; every important beat has an auditory, a captioned, a tactile, and a braille-readable form.
- Writing shows through behaviour, sound, and environment before it ever explains through speech.
- Characters are complete people, and their identities inform how they speak without ever becoming the whole of what they say.
- The prose never resolves a deliberate mystery, especially anything concerning the Shawabti or Faience; it describes how understanding grows rather than declaring an answer.
- Where another document owns a topic, the writing references it by name rather than restating or overriding it.

## The craft of spoken dialogue

Dialogue is the primary place where character, information, and emotion meet, so it is written to sound like people rather than like a script delivering facts.

### Writing dialogue

Dialogue is written to be spoken aloud and to be read as captions with equal clarity, so it favours rhythm, plain human phrasing, and lines that reveal who is speaking through vocabulary and cadence rather than through a name tag. Each recurring character has a consistent speech rhythm and vocabulary the ear can learn, and the writing protects that consistency across every chapter. Characters speak from their own knowledge and their own stake in the moment, and no character narrates information they would have no reason to say.

- Write for the ear first and confirm it reads cleanly as a caption.
- Give each character a stable rhythm, vocabulary, and set of verbal habits so they are recognisable without a label.
- Let characters withhold, hesitate, and speak past one another the way real people do.
- Never use dialogue as a delivery pipe for lore the character would not naturally voice.

### Avoiding exposition

Exposition is avoided by making the player earn understanding through observation, action, and conversation rather than through a character explaining the world to them. When a fact must be spoken, it is spoken because a character needs it in that moment, not because the audience needs a briefing. The writing trusts the player to assemble understanding from many small, honest signals, consistent with the canon that history and understanding are earned separately.

- Prefer a character acting on knowledge to a character reciting knowledge.
- Break any necessary explanation into small pieces distributed across scenes and voices.
- Let the player ask, and let answers arrive through people who have a reason to give them.
- Treat any paragraph that exists only to inform the player as a fault to be redesigned.

### How characters interrupt one another

Interruption is written deliberately, because overlapping and broken speech communicates urgency, relationship, and emotional state. In audio it is layered so the player can still follow the essential line, and in captions and braille it is marked clearly so no reader loses the thread. Interruptions are authored, never accidental, and their meaning is always recoverable through every output channel.

- Use interruption to convey pressure, intimacy, or conflict, and give each interruption a purpose.
- Keep the load-bearing line audible in the mix and legible in captions even when voices overlap.
- Mark overlapping speech in captions and braille so a Deaf or DeafBlind player perceives the same overlap the hearing player perceives.
- Never let an interruption hide information that a player relying on text would need.

## Emotional register

Resonance carries real feeling, and its emotional writing is disciplined so that humour, grief, and hope each land honestly and never depend on the score alone.

### Writing humour

Humour grows from character and situation, from warmth, timing, and the comfort of people who trust one another, and it is never built on mocking a person's body, identity, disability, or way of communicating. It gives the world lightness and makes the community feel lived-in, and it reads through word choice and rhythm so a player who cannot hear the delivery still receives the joke.

- Let humour come from character, relationship, and situation rather than from cruelty.
- Never make a person's disability, identity, or communication method the punchline.
- Write the humour so it survives in captions and braille, not only in vocal delivery.
- Use warmth and gentle humour to show a community that is healing, in keeping with the tone of the franchise.

### Writing grief

Grief is written with restraint and honesty, because loss is real in this world and failure changes history rather than erasing it. The writing gives grief room and silence rather than forcing resolution, and it lets characters carry loss forward as part of who they become. Grief is never exploited for shock, and it is always accompanied by the quiet insistence that the community keeps building.

- Give grief space and time rather than rushing it toward comfort.
- Let loss become part of a character's continuing story rather than a moment they simply recover from.
- Convey grief through behaviour, ambience, and silence as much as through words.
- Hold to the canon that even in loss the community continues to build.

### Writing hope

Hope in Resonance is earned and concrete, measured by what people create together rather than by reassurance. It is written as the steady work of a community that keeps building through hardship, and it appears in small, tangible improvements to the shared home as often as in triumphant moments. Hope is honest about cost, and it never pretends the loss did not happen.

- Ground hope in something the community actually makes or mends together.
- Prefer quiet, tangible signs of recovery to grand declarations.
- Let hope coexist with grief rather than cancelling it.
- Keep hope collective, because in Resonance people, not individuals, are the source of it.

## The written voice of silence

Silence is a full member of the vocabulary of Resonance, and it is written on purpose.

### Writing silence

Silence is a deliberate storytelling tool, so it is scripted with the same care as dialogue and never left as an absence where content has not yet been made. Written silence communicates emotion, danger, absence, distance, or anticipation, and it always has a non-auditory form so a player with no auditory input perceives the pause as clearly as a hearing player. Silence is described in stage directions, marked in captions, and expressed through the tactile channel so it reads as meaning rather than as an error.

- Script silence intentionally and note what it is meant to communicate.
- Give every meaningful silence a captioned and tactile form so it is never invisible to a player who cannot hear.
- Never let silence stand in for content that simply has not been written.
- Treat the acoustic identity of quiet as owned by the Audio Bible and reference it rather than redefining it.

## Professional voices

Each profession hears and describes the world differently, and the writing gives each a distinct register so the player can recognise a speaker's work by how they talk.

### How scientists speak

Scientists speak with precision and curiosity, stating what the evidence supports and marking clearly where they are inferring rather than concluding. They distinguish between what happened and what is currently understood, consistent with the difference between objective history and historical knowledge, and they are comfortable saying that a question is still open. Their speech invites investigation rather than closing it.

- Favour careful, qualified statements over confident overreach.
- Separate observation from interpretation in the phrasing itself.
- Treat open questions as legitimate answers rather than as failures.
- Never let a scientist resolve a deliberate mystery; let them describe the method of finding out.

### How engineers speak

Engineers speak in terms of systems, load, tolerance, and consequence, describing the world as interlocking things that can fail and be repaired. Their speech is practical and grounded, often diagnostic, and it reveals expertise the player does not have. Engineers explain by pointing at behaviour and cause rather than by lecturing.

- Use concrete, physical, systems-oriented vocabulary.
- Let engineers reason aloud from symptom to cause when it serves the scene.
- Show engineering expertise as knowledge the player relies on, never as knowledge the player already holds.
- Keep the register practical, because engineers describe what is happening and what they will do about it.

### How maintenance workers speak

Maintenance workers speak plainly and from long familiarity with the hidden parts of the ship, the vents, ducts, panels, and backup systems that others overlook. Their voice carries the everyday texture of the home and often the first, quiet notice that something is wrong. They speak with the grounded confidence of people who know the place in their hands, and their perspective is treated as expertise equal to any other.

- Keep the register plain, direct, and rooted in hands-on familiarity.
- Let maintenance workers notice the small early signs others miss.
- Present their knowledge of the fabric of the home as genuine expertise.
- Reflect the canon that maintenance is developed first and carries the community's daily life.

### How command speaks

Command speaks with clarity and economy under pressure, allocating people to tasks and holding the operational picture together without wasted words. Because command aboard Hearth is carried by the Vale System, the writing of command is inseparable from the fronting member, and the register shifts with whoever leads the moment while the authority itself stays continuous. Command never becomes cold; its authority is expressed as responsibility for people.

- Write command as clear, economical, and calm, especially in emergencies.
- Match the command register to the fronting member of the Vale System, following the Character Identity Map.
- Keep the office of Captain continuous even as the voice leading it changes.
- Express command authority as care for the crew rather than as dominance.

### How civilians speak

Civilians speak from ordinary life, from family, work, fear, hope, and the daily business of a community that is more than its crew. Their voice grounds the stakes, because the community exists for the people who are not on duty, and their speech carries the announcements, worries, and small joys of everyday residence. Civilians are written as full participants in the world rather than as background.

- Root civilian speech in family, work, and everyday concern.
- Let civilians voice fear and confusion honestly in a crisis, and warmth and routine in calm.
- Treat civilian perspectives as essential rather than incidental.
- Show that the community continues its ordinary life whether or not the player is present.

## Community voices

Beyond professions, the community holds voices shaped by age and culture, and the writing keeps each distinct and respectful.

### How children speak

Children speak with directness, curiosity, and their own logic, and they are written as real people rather than as devices for exposition or sentiment. They notice things adults miss, ask the questions the player is thinking, and grow up inside the world, attending school and continuing their lives even while the player is away. Their speech is age-appropriate and never used to deliver a lecture.

- Write children with genuine curiosity and their own way of reasoning.
- Let children ask real questions rather than convenient ones.
- Never use a child solely to explain the world or to force emotion.
- Reflect that children continue attending school and living in the world in the player's absence.

### How different cultures speak

Different cultures speak with distinct rhythms, idioms, forms of greeting, and ways of showing respect, and each is written as a living culture rather than as a costume. Culture belongs to civilisations rather than to biology, so the writing never lets a people's speech collapse into a single stereotype, and it draws cultural detail from the Universe Bible rather than inventing competing lore. Human diversity is presented as an ordinary part of civilisation.

- Give each culture consistent, specific verbal texture rather than generic markers.
- Keep culture a property of civilisation, never a consequence of species or biology.
- Avoid stereotype by writing individuals within a culture rather than representatives of it.
- Reference the Universe Bible for cultural canon instead of creating parallel versions.

## Communication across species

Species communicate in ways that may not resemble human speech, and the writing respects that difference while keeping the world understandable.

### How different species communicate

Different species communicate through whatever their biology and history allow, which may include sound, gesture, light, tactile signal, or means humanity has not yet encountered, and the writing renders these so they are perceivable through every output channel. Where a species is the Shawabti, communication is known only through their preserved legacy and through Resonance, and the prose describes how meaning is gradually reconstructed rather than declaring what the Shawabti said or were. No species is written as a monolith or as an enemy by nature.

- Base a species' communication on its biology and history rather than on human assumptions.
- Render non-spoken communication so it reaches auditory, captioned, tactile, and braille channels.
- Preserve the Shawabti as a mystery; describe reconstruction and Resonance, never a settled translation.
- Never reduce a species to a single voice or to an inherent adversary.

## Language and translation

Resonance is multilingual and multimodal, and the writing keeps every conversation legible to every player.

### How multilingual conversations function

Multilingual conversations are written so the player always knows which language is being used and never loses meaning because of it, whether through Faience's translation, through captions that mark the source language, or through characters who move between languages as an ordinary part of life. The many languages of the community, spoken, signed, written, braille, tactile, and augmentative, are all treated as equally valid. Code-switching is written as natural behaviour rather than as a special event.

- Make the language in use clear in audio, captions, and braille at all times.
- Treat every human language and every communication method as equally valid.
- Write code-switching as ordinary rather than as remarkable.
- Ensure meaning is preserved across every output channel regardless of the language spoken.

### How translators work

Translation is written as a gradual, imperfect, human-and-Faience process rather than as instant, perfect comprehension, consistent with first contact as stewardship. Faience serves as translator, navigator, and research partner, but the writing never lets translation resolve a mystery the story means to keep, and it shows understanding growing through conversation over time. Translation is honest about its own uncertainty.

- Show translation building understanding gradually rather than delivering instant fluency.
- Let translation be imperfect and self-aware about what it cannot yet render.
- Keep Faience's translation within its established role and never let it settle a deliberate mystery.
- Reference the AI Bible and Universe Bible for Faience's translation role rather than redefining it.

### Representing sign languages

Sign languages are represented as complete, grammatical languages in their own right, never as gestured versions of spoken language, and they are rendered so their meaning, tone, and rhythm reach every player. In audio-led scenes, signing is conveyed through described action, spoken interpretation where a character interprets, and captions; in text it is written with respect for its own structure. Signing is an ordinary and equal way of communicating aboard Hearth.

- Treat each sign language as a full language with its own grammar and expression.
- Convey signed dialogue through description, interpretation, and captioning so no player misses it.
- Never write signing as mime or as a lesser substitute for speech.
- Present signing as an ordinary, equal communication method within the community.

## Identity and the Vale System

Identity is written with the same care as any other canon, and the Vale System is the franchise's central example.

### Portraying dissociative identity and the Vale System

Dissociative identity is portrayed through the Vale System as an ordinary medical reality accommodated as easily as any other, never as horror, villainy, instability, or spectacle. The Vale System is one shared body inhabited by four members, Captain Elian, Captain Naomi, Captain Gideon, and Captain Iris, who front in turn, and the writing never renders them as four separate people and never as a single character named Captain Mara Vale. Fronting is purposeful, driven by which member's temperament and expertise fit the moment, and the player comes to recognise the current member through voice, rhythm, and priorities rather than through announcement. Mara Chen is a blind engineer and is not a member of the Vale System.

- Write the Vale System as one body and four members who front in turn, following the Character Identity Map.
- Give each member a consistent, distinct voice, vocabulary, and register so the ear learns them.
- Present switching as ordinary and dignified, never as malfunction, danger, or drama.
- Keep the office of Captain continuous across fronting, and never confuse Mara Chen with the Vale System.

### Portraying disability

Disability shapes characters without defining them, so the writing gives disabled characters full lives, histories, flaws, and expertise, and never treats disability as a joke, a tragedy to be cured, or a qualification for existing. Accessibility is standard equipment, so characters customise their interfaces rather than asking permission to use them, and their access tools are ordinary parts of daily life. Representation informs identity but never replaces it.

- Write disabled characters as complete people first, with disability as one true part of who they are.
- Never write disability as a punchline, a tragedy, or a problem to be solved by cure.
- Treat access tools and accommodations as ordinary standard equipment aboard human vessels.
- Follow the Accessibility Bible and the Character Identity Map rather than inventing new disability canon.

### How Deaf characters communicate

Deaf characters communicate through sign language, writing, augmentative tools, and any method they choose, and the writing ensures every scene is fully understandable without sound. Deaf perspectives are written from the inside rather than as something explained to hearing characters, and Deaf identity is presented as culture and language as well as experience. No information a Deaf character needs is ever carried by sound alone.

- Centre Deaf characters' own communication choices rather than accommodating them as an afterthought.
- Ensure every scene involving a Deaf character is complete through captions, braille, and visible action.
- Present Deaf identity as language and culture, not only as absence of hearing.
- Never let a scene depend on a sound a Deaf character or player cannot access.

### How blind characters communicate

Blind characters communicate through speech, braille, tactile signing, and audio-first tools, and the writing renders the world so it is fully navigable and understandable through hearing, touch, and description. Blind perspectives are written from lived competence rather than as limitation, drawing on the way the world teaches location, identity, and event through sound and tactile cues. Mara Chen exemplifies this as a blind engineer whose expertise the crew relies on.

- Write blind characters from competence and expertise rather than from limitation.
- Ensure the surrounding world is legible through sound, touch, and description.
- Use tactile and braille communication as ordinary, equal methods.
- Keep blind characters' access tools as standard equipment they simply use.

### How AAC users communicate

Augmentative and alternative communication users communicate through their devices and systems as full, equal participants, and the writing gives their contributions the same weight, wit, and authority as spoken dialogue. AAC is never written as slow, lesser, or a barrier to be pitied; it is a valid voice, and other characters wait for and respond to it as they would any speaker. The rhythm of AAC is respected rather than rushed.

- Treat AAC output as a full, equal voice with the same range as speech.
- Never write AAC as inferior, and never have characters talk over or dismiss it.
- Give AAC users humour, authority, and complexity like any other character.
- Reflect the canon that AAC is standard, valid communication aboard the ship.

## Interior and documentary text

Beyond dialogue, Resonance is full of written and recorded text, and each form has its own voice.

### Writing thoughts

Thoughts are written sparingly and distinctly from spoken dialogue, marked clearly so a reader always knows an inner voice from an outer one. Because the player learns other people through observation rather than through access to their minds, interior thought belongs mainly to the player's own character and is used to colour rather than to explain. Thoughts never narrate information the player should discover through the world.

- Mark interior thought clearly and consistently apart from spoken lines.
- Use thought to convey feeling and perspective, not to deliver plot.
- Reserve mind-reading; other characters are known through behaviour, not through their thoughts.
- Keep interior narration brief so the world, not the head, carries the story.

### Writing logs

Logs are written as functional records with a consistent voice, timestamped in-world and shaped by the profession of whoever recorded them. They read as something a working person actually filed, terse where the moment was busy and fuller where reflection was possible, and they reward the player who reads across many logs with an understanding no single log states. Logs preserve honest first-hand record even when later discovery corrects it.

- Give logs a functional, in-world voice shaped by the recorder's profession.
- Keep each log honest to the moment it was recorded rather than to later knowledge.
- Distribute understanding across many logs rather than concentrating it in one.
- Reference the Knowledge Archive Bible for how logged knowledge becomes archive.

### Writing journals

Journals are more personal than logs, written as private reflection that reveals a character's inner life, relationships, and growth. They carry a distinct, human voice and are found rather than delivered, rewarding curiosity with intimacy and context. Journals show how a person understood events at the time, which may differ from what was later learned.

- Write journals in an intimate, personal voice distinct from official logs.
- Reveal relationship, doubt, and growth rather than plot mechanics.
- Place journals as discoveries the curious player earns.
- Let a journal preserve a person's honest, time-bound understanding.

### Writing scientific papers

Scientific papers are written with formal structure, careful qualification, and explicit distinction between evidence and interpretation, modelling the discipline of real research. They state methods, mark uncertainty, and treat open questions as legitimate, and they never resolve a deliberate mystery even when a character wishes they could. Papers are readable to a curious non-specialist through clear framing while remaining rigorous.

- Use formal structure and precise, qualified language.
- Separate method, evidence, and interpretation explicitly.
- Frame findings so a non-specialist can follow without diluting the rigour.
- Preserve mysteries; a paper may describe an investigation but never invents an answer to a canonical unknown.

### Writing reports

Reports are written as purposeful, structured communication from one part of the community to another, clear about what happened, what is recommended, and what remains uncertain. They carry the register of their author's profession and their audience's need, and they are concise, actionable, and honest about limits. A report exists to enable a decision rather than to impress.

- Structure reports around what happened, what it means, and what to do.
- Match the report's register to author and audience.
- Keep reports concise, actionable, and honest about uncertainty.
- Never pad a report with lore the situation does not require.

### Writing announcements

Announcements are written as public, community-facing communication, clear and calm, and always delivered through multiple channels so that no resident misses them. A spoken announcement is always accompanied by a captioned and, where relevant, a tactile or braille form, and its tone reflects the ordinary life of the community as much as its emergencies. Announcements are part of how the community's daily rhythm is heard.

- Write announcements to be clear, calm, and immediately understandable.
- Deliver every announcement through auditory, captioned, and tactile or braille channels together.
- Reflect ordinary community life, not only crises, in the announcement voice.
- Never let a public announcement depend on a single sense.

### Writing emergency communications

Emergency communications are written for maximum clarity under pressure, stripped to the essential action and delivered simultaneously across every channel so urgency reaches every player equally. Spoken urgency is matched by captions, visual indicators, and authored tactile patterns, and timed pressure is never conveyed by sound alone. The register is calm authority rather than panic, consistent with how command holds a crisis together.

- Reduce emergency messages to the essential action with no ambiguity.
- Convey urgency simultaneously through sound, captions, visual cues, and tactile patterns.
- Never make survival information depend on hearing or on sight alone.
- Keep the tone calm and authoritative, following the command voice of the Vale System.

## Discovery over exposition

The deepest principle of Resonance writing is that understanding is discovered, not delivered.

### Environmental storytelling instead of exposition

Environmental storytelling carries meaning that would otherwise be explained, letting a space, its sounds, its wear, and its arrangement tell the player what happened without a character narrating it. Because every location has an acoustic identity and every object communicates, the environment is authored as a text the player reads through hearing, touch, and description, and it is built so a player using any sense can read it. The world explains itself by being observed.

- Let places, sounds, and objects carry story that dialogue would otherwise dump.
- Author the environment as a readable text across auditory, tactile, and described channels.
- Ensure environmental meaning is accessible to a player using any single sense.
- Reference the Audio Bible for acoustic identity rather than restating it.

### How information is slowly discovered

Information is revealed gradually, so that the player finishes each expedition knowing more while realising how much remains, consistent with the canon that every expedition answers one question and raises at least two. Knowledge is corrected through further discovery rather than through retconning the past, and understanding grows as the player gains new perspective on events that already happened. The pacing of revelation is designed so curiosity always slightly outpaces certainty, and the deliberate mysteries of the Shawabti and Faience are revealed progressively through play and recorded where the campaign reveals them, never resolved in advance.

- Reveal understanding in earned increments rather than in single explanations.
- Let new evidence correct earlier understanding without rewriting honest earlier records.
- Keep curiosity slightly ahead of certainty at every stage.
- Preserve every deliberate mystery, describing the mechanism of gradual revelation and never the answer.

## Relationship to other documents

This guide is a craft standard for writing and holds no authority over any rule. Where its wording appears to differ from a governing document, that document governs.

- The Project Constitution is the governing authority for the whole project, and this guide serves the core philosophy it establishes.
- The Canon Rules document is the master rule list, and this guide follows its wording on accessibility, acoustic identity, silence, discovery, the Vale System, and representation.
- The Audio Bible defines sound as the primary renderer, acoustic identity, and the treatment of silence that this guide's writing relies on.
- The Accessibility Bible defines the captioned, tactile, braille, and multimodal paths that every accessible line in this guide depends on.
- The Player Identity and Character Presentation Bible defines how recurring characters are presented, including the Vale System, and this guide writes to those presentations.
- The Universe Bible is the definitive reference for species, civilisations, cultures, Faience, and the deliberate mysteries this guide preserves rather than resolves.
- The Knowledge Archive Bible defines how logged, journalled, and researched knowledge becomes archive and changes gameplay.
- The Campaign Bible defines the chapters, questions, and perspective structure that this guide's discovery pacing serves.
- The Decision Log records when this writing standard was adopted or amended.
