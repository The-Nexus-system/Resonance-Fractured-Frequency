# Visual Direction Track

This document is the visual production brief for Resonance: Fractured Frequency. It defines the emotional and visual target for the franchise and the design rules that govern every visual decision. Resonance is a sound-first game: visuals enhance the experience and are never required. Every visual decision made here must satisfy a parallel non-visual requirement before it ships.

This document is a focused production guide. It does not duplicate or override the character visual standards in the Player Identity and Character Presentation Bible, the spatial layout standards in the Modular Architecture Bible, the asset production constraints in the graphics and asset direction track, or the acoustic identity standards in the Audio Bible. Where those documents govern, this one refers to them.

## Visual ambition

Resonance is visually ambitious precisely because it never requires sight. The game must look meaningful to a sighted player while communicating every piece of meaningful visual information through parallel non-visual channels. Visual ambition and accessibility are not in tension here: both are satisfied only when the visual language is clear, purposeful, and legible.

The visual target is a lived-in future that feels genuine rather than polished. Human spaces carry the weight of long occupation: wear, repair, personalisation, and the quiet evidence of daily life. Shawabti spaces carry the weight of time and of a civilisation that thought differently about space, material, and light. Both must be immediately readable by sound and touch alone.

Every scene should be legible at a glance and fully navigable by ear. If a visual element communicates something the player needs to know, that information has a defined non-visual equivalent before the element is built.

## Colour philosophy

Colour in Resonance communicates state, identity, and history. It is never used as the sole carrier of critical information. Any colour-coded system — alert levels, crew sections, faction identity, Shawabti site condition — must pair its colour with a shape, a label, a haptic pattern, or an audio cue that carries the same meaning without the colour.

Human spaces use colour conservatively. Metal, fabric, and worn surfaces dominate. Colour appears in personal items, cultural markers, crew section wayfinding, and the subtle evidence of community life: a painted door, a fabric divider, a child's drawing fixed to a wall.

Shawabti spaces do not use colour to decorate. Where colour appears in a Shawabti site, it is material-inherent, structural, or meaningful in ways the player has not yet learned to read. The visual language of Shawabti colour is revealed through Chapter Five and later, consistent with the canon rule that the deliberate mysteries of the universe are revealed through play.

Alert and status colour follows a consistent system that pairs every colour with a non-colour signal. Critical alerts pair red with a distinct shape and a mandatory audio cue. Caution pairs amber with a different shape and a softer audio cue. Safe states pair green or neutral with the absence of warning shapes and the absence of alert audio. Players who cannot distinguish colours receive the same information through the paired signals.

## Lighting philosophy

Light in Resonance is a storytelling material, not a rendering requirement. The guiding question for every lighting decision is: what does this light say about the people and the history of this place?

Human spaces aboard CSV Hearth are lit for function and for life. Working areas are clear and even. Living areas are warmer and more personal. Spaces under repair or incomplete carry a rawness that the Environmental Sound Progression in the Audio Bible parallels acoustically. As the community grows, the light grows with it.

Shawabti sites carry a different relationship to light. Where human spaces push light outward to illuminate, Shawabti spaces may hold light within materials, direct it with structural precision, or leave spaces in a quality of dark that feels considered rather than absent. The specific visual character of Shawabti light is not fixed here: it is defined as Chapter Five's visual design brief is developed, consistent with the preservation of deliberate mystery.

Emergency and damage states change the lighting in ways that any player can read: emergency lighting has a distinct rhythm, damaged areas lose the warmth and regularity of working light, and restored areas regain it. These state changes are always paired with audio cues and haptic patterns from the Accessibility Bible.

No scene is lit in a way that requires the player to see in order to navigate it. The spatial audio of the Modular Architecture Bible's acoustic environments defines the navigable structure; light enhances it without replacing it.

## Material language

Every material in the Resonance universe has a visual identity that reflects its acoustic identity as defined in the Audio Bible's material library. Visual and acoustic material qualities are designed together rather than separately, so that a player who experiences both simultaneously receives a coherent world.

Human materials read as practical, maintained, and personal. Metal surfaces carry the evidence of use: scratches, repair patches, hand-worn edges. Fabric carries the character of the person who chose it. Glass and transparent panels are used where safety and spatial clarity justify them, not as decorative spectacle.

Shawabti materials read differently. Their surface qualities do not follow the human logic of wear and function. The visual grammar of Shawabti material — how it ages, how it responds to light, what it communicates about the civilisation that used it — is developed as the Chapter Five and Chapter Six design briefs are produced. This track establishes the principle: Shawabti materials have a consistent internal logic that the player learns to read, and that logic must be legible through the non-visual channels the Accessibility Bible defines.

New materials introduced by any chapter must define their visual identity, their acoustic identity, and their non-visual equivalence before they ship.

## Human versus Shawabti visual language

The visual grammar that distinguishes a human space from a Shawabti space is one of the franchise's primary design tools. A player entering a Shawabti site for the first time should feel the difference before any dialogue names it.

Human spaces are organised around people: scale, proportion, storage, sight lines, and traffic flow all reflect human bodies and human habits. The Modular Architecture Bible defines these layouts in full.

Shawabti spaces are organised around different principles. Their scale, proportion, and spatial logic reflect a civilisation that the player does not yet understand. The specific principles that govern Shawabti spatial organisation are revealed through play and are not fixed in this document. What is fixed here is the design requirement: the difference between human and Shawabti spaces must be immediately perceptible through visual, acoustic, and haptic channels, so that a player using any combination of those channels recognises the distinction.

Environmental artists working on Shawabti sites must brief with the narrative team before committing any design that reveals the logic behind Shawabti spatial organisation. The deliberate mysteries of the universe are preserved in the visual language as firmly as they are in the written canon.

## Information layer design

The information layer is every piece of interface that sits over the three-dimensional world: spatial audio indicators, waypoint markers, interaction prompts, status displays, and contextual labels. In Resonance, the information layer is designed last for sighted players and first for all other players.

The non-visual version of every information layer element is defined before the visual version is designed. If a spatial audio indicator tells a player that a workstation is interactable, that information is delivered through audio and haptics for players who need it that way. The visual indicator is then designed to be consistent with and legible alongside those channels, not to replace them.

Visual indicators in the information layer must be readable at the smallest text size the platform supports and must pass contrast requirements without colour as the sole distinguishing factor. Shape, position, and motion carry meaning independently of colour.

The information layer is always dismissible. No element is ever permanently obscured or permanently removed. Players who prefer minimal visual UI receive the same gameplay information through the non-visual channels that serve players who use no visual UI at all.

## Non-visual equivalence requirement

Every visual element in the game that communicates information the player needs must have a defined non-visual equivalent before the visual element is built. This requirement is not a post-production accessibility pass: it is a design gate.

The Accessibility Bible is the authority for what non-visual equivalents are required, how they are authored, and how they are tested. This document adds the visual design side of that requirement: no visual design is considered complete until the Accessibility Bible's corresponding non-visual requirement has been addressed.

Visual concept art, environment art, UI design, and portrait production all pass through the accessibility review gate defined in the Production Pipeline Bible before they progress to implementation.

## Relationship to other documents

This document is the visual production brief. It does not override any of the following; it works alongside them.

- The Player Identity and Character Presentation Bible is the authoritative visual standard for every character: face, body, silhouette, clothing, hair, animation, and portrait. Character visual design begins there, not here.
- The Modular Architecture Bible is the authoritative spatial reference for every environment: layout, function, and room design. Environmental visual design begins there, not here.
- The Audio Bible is the definitive reference for the acoustic identity that every visual element must have a non-visual counterpart to. Where this document says a material, a space, or a state has a visual character, the Audio Bible defines its acoustic character.
- The Accessibility Bible defines what non-visual equivalents are required for every visual decision, how they are authored, and how they are tested.
- The Franchise Asset Bible defines the asset categories and metadata standards that visual assets must satisfy.
- The graphics and asset direction track defines the production constraints for visual assets: polygon budgets, material channels, performance targets, and asset review criteria.
- The Production Pipeline Bible governs how visual design work passes through review gates and enters the repository.
- The Canon Rules document holds the master wording of the rules this document applies: everything has an acoustic identity; accessibility is standard equipment; the deliberate mysteries of the universe are revealed through play.
