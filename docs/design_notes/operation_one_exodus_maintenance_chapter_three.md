# Operation One: Exodus — Chapter Three: Community

This is a gameplay design document for the Maintenance perspective of Chapter Three. It maintains all previously established canon. It is not a screenplay and it is not code. It describes how the chapter plays, what the player decides, what each decision teaches, and what permanently changes as a result.

## Purpose

Chapter Three marks the transition from survival to civilisation.

Chapter One taught the player their profession. Chapter Two taught the player how to rescue people. Chapter Three teaches the player how communities function.

The player is no longer reacting to disaster. The player is now helping shape the future.

Every part of this chapter must satisfy three goals.

1. The player makes meaningful decisions.
2. The player learns a new gameplay system.
3. The player learns something from a crew member that permanently expands their understanding and abilities.

Every section should end with the player becoming more capable than when it began.

## Chapter Question

The chapter asks: can we survive together?

By the end of the chapter the answer is yes, if everyone contributes.

The chapter ends by asking a larger question: what are we willing to build together?

## Section Design Framework

Every major section in this chapter that presents a player decision answers the four questions established by the canon rule "Every Major Decision Answers Four Questions" in the Canon Rules document. Chapter Three is the first chapter authored to that rule.

1. What decision does the player make?
2. What gameplay system is learned?
3. What philosophy or life lesson is learned?
4. What permanently changes because of that decision?

## Part One: The First Morning

### Part One — Purpose

The first peaceful morning since the destruction of Hearth.

No alarms. No explosions. No emergency countdowns.

The player wakes to ordinary life. Ordinary life is the reward for surviving Chapter Two.

### Part One — Narrative Purpose

Demonstrate that the station is now a living community.

People have routines. People have responsibilities. Life continued while the player slept.

The world does not wait for the player. Instead, the player joins a community already beginning another day.

### Part One — Gameplay Purpose

Teach observation. Teach routine. Teach environmental awareness. Teach that important gameplay information is often discovered by paying attention to everyday life.

Combat is absent. Listening is the primary mechanic.

### Soundscape

The player wakes to a station that sounds alive.

- Ventilation.
- Coffee brewing.
- Tools being organised.
- Medical monitors.
- Someone humming while working.
- AAC notifications.
- Children laughing.
- Morning announcements.
- Engineering diagnostics.
- Maintenance drones.
- Footsteps.
- Conversations between crew members beginning their day.

### Player Agency

The player is free to decide.

- Who to visit first.
- Whether to immediately begin work or simply explore.
- Whether to listen to conversations.
- Whether to check reports before speaking to anyone.
- Whether to visit each workspace.

No choice is considered wrong. Each choice changes what the player learns first.

### Crew Learning

Every crew member teaches something simply by living their routine.

- Rowan teaches preventative maintenance. The player learns that fixing small problems early prevents larger failures later.
- Mara teaches active listening. She demonstrates identifying equipment problems entirely through sound.
- Samira teaches preventative medicine. She explains that healthy communities prevent illness rather than waiting to treat it.
- Jonah teaches organisation. The player learns that planning saves more time than rushing.
- Talia teaches situational awareness. Security begins with observation, not weapons.
- Soren teaches curiosity. He demonstrates recording questions before searching for answers.
- Mira teaches perspective. She notices details every adult has ignored.
- The Vale System teaches collaborative leadership. Leadership begins by listening, not speaking.

### Player Choice Structure

The player wakes naturally. There are no urgent objectives. Instead, Jonah has left a morning schedule.

The player may visit the crew in any order. The order chosen does not change canon. The order changes what the player learns first, what conversations occur later in the day, and which optional gameplay bonuses become available earliest.

Every first visit permanently unlocks one gameplay concept. The player is expected to eventually visit everyone before attending the community meeting. No crew member should ever become optional.

### First Visit: Rowan Ives

- Player learns: preventative maintenance. Rowan demonstrates inspecting systems before they fail.
- Gameplay unlock: Maintenance Inspection Mode. The player can now perform preventative inspections around the station.
- Benefits:
  - Reduced future equipment failures.
  - Small resource rewards.
  - Hidden maintenance problems become detectable.
- Future dialogue: Rowan later comments that the player is beginning to think like a maintenance engineer.

### First Visit: Mara Chen

- Player learns: active listening. Mara teaches identifying system health entirely by sound.
- Gameplay unlock: Engineering Diagnostics. The player begins recognising different machinery states through their acoustic signatures.
- Benefits:
  - Earlier warning of equipment failures.
  - Improved engineering puzzles.
  - Reduced repair time.
- Future dialogue: Mara begins occasionally asking the player what they hear before explaining the answer.

### First Visit: Doctor Samira Kade

- Player learns: communities survive because they prevent illness rather than simply treating it.
- Gameplay unlock: Crew Wellness. The player can begin checking overall crew wellbeing instead of waiting for medical emergencies.
- Benefits:
  - Improved morale.
  - Reduced long-term injuries.
  - Preventative medical projects become available.
- Future dialogue: crew members begin voluntarily discussing health concerns with the player.

### First Visit: Chief Petty Officer Jonah Briggs

- Player learns: planning saves time.
- Gameplay unlock: Mission Planning Board. The player can organise projects, prioritise work and schedule future tasks.
- Benefits:
  - Projects complete more efficiently.
  - Reduced idle time.
  - Additional cooperative projects become available.
- Future dialogue: Jonah increasingly trusts the player's organisational ability.

### First Visit: Security Lead Talia Rusk

- Player learns: situational awareness prevents danger.
- Gameplay unlock: Environmental Risk Assessment. The player can identify potential hazards before beginning projects.
- Benefits:
  - Fewer accidents.
  - Safer exploration.
  - Improved rescue planning.
- Future dialogue: Talia begins involving the player in security planning rather than only emergency response.

### First Visit: Dr. Soren Qadir

- Player learns: questions are as valuable as answers.
- Gameplay unlock: Research Notebook. Players can now record theories, unanswered questions and observations for later investigation.
- Benefits:
  - Knowledge Archive discoveries become easier to connect.
  - Hidden research opportunities become visible.
- Future dialogue: Soren begins asking the player for observations before giving explanations.

### First Visit: Mira Ives

- Player learns: curiosity reveals details expertise often overlooks.
- Gameplay unlock: Curiosity Prompts. The player occasionally notices optional environmental details that begin side conversations, research opportunities and accessibility improvements.
- Benefits:
  - Additional Knowledge Archive entries.
  - Optional discoveries.
  - Unique conversations.
- Future dialogue: Mira regularly asks the player questions that encourage exploration instead of objective chasing.

### First Visit: The Vale System

- Player learns: leadership begins by listening.
- Gameplay unlock: Crew Consultation. Major decisions now display who wishes to speak before the player commits to a course of action.
- Benefits:
  - Better informed decisions.
  - Additional dialogue.
  - Improved diplomatic outcomes later in the campaign.
- Future dialogue: members of the Vale System begin asking the player what they think before offering their own opinions.

### First Visit Completion Requirement

Before the community meeting begins, the player must have visited every primary crew member at least once.

Every first conversation permanently unlocks one gameplay system. Every first conversation permanently expands the player's understanding of how communities function.

The order changes the player's experience. The destination does not. Canon remains unchanged.

### Gameplay System Introduced

- Daily routines.
- Crew schedules.
- Environmental observation.
- Optional conversations.
- Routine reports.
- Morning briefings.
- Station orientation.
- Relationship building.
- Passive world discovery.

### Part One — Accessibility

Every piece of information available through environmental observation must also be available through accessible interaction.

Players may request:

- Today's schedule.
- Crew locations.
- Current projects.
- Unread reports.
- Morning announcements.
- Construction progress.
- Station orientation.

No player should miss important information because they cannot perceive one particular sensory channel.

### Part One — Emotional Goal

The player should feel:

- Safe.
- Welcomed.
- Useful.
- Part of something larger than themselves.

### Part One — End Condition

After exploring the station and speaking with crew members, the player receives an invitation from Jonah.

"The morning meeting is about to begin. Everyone's waiting."

The player chooses when to attend. The meeting does not begin until the player arrives. This becomes the transition into Part Two.

## Part Two: The First Community Meeting

### Part Two — Purpose

Teach collaborative leadership. Teach long-term planning. Teach that communities function because people solve problems together.

The player does not attend this meeting to receive orders. The player attends to become part of the discussion. The meeting should feel closer to a town council than a military briefing.

### Part Two — Narrative Purpose

This is the first time every primary crew member has occupied the same room since the destruction of Hearth.

The immediate emergency has ended. The future now depends upon planning rather than reacting.

Every person has different priorities. None of those priorities are wrong.

### Part Two — Gameplay Purpose

Introduce:

- Community planning.
- Long-term station projects.
- Project priorities.
- Shared decision making.
- Crew consultation.
- Mission planning.
- Station needs.
- Consensus building.

The player learns that planning itself is gameplay.

### Meeting Structure

The Vale System opens the meeting. Different members naturally front as different topics arise. No explanation is given. This is simply how command functions.

The meeting follows a predictable structure.

- Current status.
- Immediate concerns.
- Long-term projects.
- Open discussion.
- Community requests.
- Player input.
- Final decisions.

### Crew Reports

Each crew member's report reflects what the player did during the first morning.

- Rowan reports on structural integrity, the maintenance backlog, repair priorities, and fabrication capacity. Hidden maintenance concerns the player discovered earlier alter his report. If the player completed preventative inspections, Rowan has fewer urgent repairs to discuss.
- Mara reports on power reserves, engineering capacity, upgrade opportunities, and energy shortages. If the player visited Mara first earlier that morning, additional engineering options become available because the player already understands her diagnostic terminology.
- Samira reports on crew health, mental wellbeing, medical inventory, fatigue, and preventative care priorities. If the player visited Samira first, optional wellness projects become available and crew morale begins slightly higher.
- Jonah reports on supply inventory, mission readiness, construction schedules, and outstanding requests. If the player organised tasks earlier, Jonah's report reflects improved efficiency.
- Talia reports on security concerns, hazard assessments, emergency preparedness, and external risks. If the player previously completed environmental assessments, fewer unknown risks remain.
- Soren reports on research progress, questions requiring investigation, Knowledge Archive updates, and strange signal observations. If the player documented additional observations earlier, Soren incorporates those discoveries into future research.
- Mira does not report formally. Instead she interrupts. She asks questions. Those questions expose assumptions adults have made. At least one optional project should become available because of something Mira notices. Curiosity changes planning.

### Player Decisions

The player is asked to help establish the station's first major priorities. The player does not choose one correct answer. Instead the player determines what receives attention first.

The available priorities are as follows.

- Expand living space.
  - Immediate result: construction begins; crew comfort increases.
  - Future result: higher morale; additional community conversations; earlier access to optional social activities; construction projects progress more slowly.
- Strengthen engineering systems.
  - Immediate result: power reliability improves; engineering upgrades unlock sooner.
  - Future result: advanced equipment becomes available earlier; living conditions improve more slowly.
- Increase medical preparedness.
  - Immediate result: medical inventory expands; crew health improves.
  - Future result: future injuries recover faster; medical research becomes available sooner; other construction projects progress more slowly.
- Expand research capabilities.
  - Immediate result: the Knowledge Archive develops faster; new discoveries occur earlier.
  - Future result: additional dialogue options; earlier diplomatic advantages; engineering and construction improvements occur more gradually.
- Strengthen station defences.
  - Immediate result: security systems improve; future exploration becomes safer.
  - Future result: earlier defensive upgrades; reduced accidental damage; scientific progress slows slightly.
- Search immediately for additional survivors.
  - Immediate result: exploration projects begin sooner; additional optional rescue opportunities appear earlier.
  - Future result: the station develops more slowly because resources are shared with rescue operations.

### Canon Rule

None of these choices create a better or worse playthrough. Every priority eventually becomes available.

The player's choice determines what the community accomplishes first. History changes. Canon does not.

### Crew Reactions

Every crew member responds differently to each priority. Nobody is universally pleased. Nobody is universally disappointed.

Respectful disagreement is healthy. The player begins understanding personalities through discussion rather than exposition.

### Part Two — Gameplay Systems Introduced

- Station project board.
- Priority management.
- Community voting.
- Long-term planning.
- Crew consultation.
- Project dependencies.
- Project timelines.
- Community requests.

### Part Two — Accessibility

Every discussion topic is reviewable after the meeting.

Players may replay:

- The entire meeting.
- Individual reports.
- Player decisions.
- Crew recommendations.
- Meeting minutes.

No information should be permanently lost because a player became distracted or missed dialogue.

### Part Two — Emotional Goal

The player should leave the meeting feeling trusted. They are no longer simply another survivor. They have become one of the people helping shape the future.

### Part Two — End Condition

The first major community project officially begins. Construction starts. The station begins changing because of the player's decision.

The player is asked to report to Rowan, where practical work on the chosen project begins in Part Three.

## Part Three: Building Together

### Part Three — Purpose

Teach collaborative construction. Teach that no major project is completed by a single person. Teach the player how individual expertise combines into community.

The player should never feel like they are simply pressing a build button. Construction is a social activity.

### Part Three — Narrative Purpose

The station begins physically changing because of decisions made during the community meeting.

Construction should happen gradually. The player witnesses progress over time. Rooms slowly evolve. Equipment appears. Hallways expand. Temporary solutions become permanent.

The station grows because people are working.

### Part Three — Gameplay Purpose

Introduce:

- Collaborative construction.
- Large-scale fabrication.
- Multi-stage projects.
- Project dependencies.
- Resource logistics.
- Construction planning.
- Crew coordination.
- Station evolution.

### Construction Philosophy

Every major construction project requires multiple crew members. No single crew member can complete a major project alone.

Every completed project should visibly and audibly demonstrate cooperation. The player learns that expertise overlaps. No profession exists in isolation.

### Player Choice

The player reports to Rowan to begin the first community project.

Rowan presents three possible starting tasks based on the priority selected during the community meeting. The player chooses which task to begin first.

Each task teaches different mechanics. All tasks eventually become required. The order changes the player's experience. Canon remains unchanged.

### Construction Choice One: Expand Habitation

- Primary crew: Rowan, Mara, Jonah, and the player.
- Player learns: habitable spaces require planning rather than simply attaching modules.
- Gameplay introduced:
  - Module connection.
  - Interior planning.
  - Environmental comfort.
  - Storage allocation.
  - Life support expansion.
- Immediate result: living quarters become larger; crew begin spending more time together.
- Future result: additional conversations; community activities; better morale; crew recover from stress more quickly.

### Construction Choice Two: Expand Engineering

- Primary crew: Mara, Rowan, Soren, and the player.
- Player learns: reliable infrastructure allows every other system to grow.
- Gameplay introduced:
  - Power grids.
  - Redundant systems.
  - Engineering diagnostics.
  - Generator balancing.
- Immediate result: stable power; improved fabrication.
- Future result: advanced upgrades become available earlier; research equipment becomes more reliable.

### Construction Choice Three: Expand Medical Facilities

- Primary crew: Samira, Rowan, Jonah, and the player.
- Player learns: healthy communities are built before emergencies happen.
- Gameplay introduced:
  - Medical planning.
  - Treatment spaces.
  - Medical inventory.
  - Preventative healthcare.
- Immediate result: expanded treatment area; improved rehabilitation.
- Future result: crew recover more quickly from future injuries; new accessibility technologies become available sooner.

### Collaborative Gameplay

Every construction project includes moments where the player assists different crew members. These are not identical mini-games. Each crew member teaches their own approach.

- Rowan teaches fabrication and precision.
- Mara teaches listening for system health.
- Samira teaches preparation before action.
- Jonah teaches organisation before construction.
- Talia teaches identifying hazards before work begins.
- Soren teaches documenting discoveries while building.
- Mira notices practical improvements adults overlooked.
- The Vale System helps resolve disagreements when multiple good solutions exist.

### Meaningful Decisions

Construction frequently presents choices.

The first example is a corridor that can be built more quickly using salvaged materials, or reinforced with additional time.

- Quick construction: the project finishes sooner, but requires more maintenance later.
- Reinforced construction: construction takes longer, but reduces maintenance and increases safety.

The second example is whether to install permanent accessibility features now, or to delay installation until resources improve.

- Immediate installation: higher initial resource cost; earlier accessibility benefits; additional optional interactions become available immediately.
- Delayed installation: resources remain available for other projects; accessibility improvements arrive later.

No choice is considered morally correct. Every decision changes how the station develops.

### Environmental Progression

Construction should never happen instantly.

Players should hear:

- Metal cutting.
- Tools.
- Conversation.
- Planning.
- Laughter.
- Occasional disagreement.
- Power cycling.
- Fabrication equipment.

The player should revisit completed areas and notice:

- Fresh paint.
- New lighting.
- Additional signage.
- Improved navigation.
- Artwork.
- Plants.
- Personal belongings.

The station becomes increasingly personalised.

### Part Three — Accessibility

Construction automatically considers accessibility. The player never has to remember to build an accessible station. Instead the player chooses which accessibility improvements receive priority.

Examples include:

- Improved tactile navigation.
- Additional caption displays.
- Braille station maps.
- Adaptive workstations.
- Environmental beacon improvements.
- Wearable haptic support.
- AAC communication terminals.

Every accessibility improvement benefits the entire community, not only disabled crew members.

### Part Three — Gameplay Systems Introduced

- Construction phases.
- Project quality.
- Collaborative building.
- Accessibility planning.
- Long-term infrastructure.
- Environmental customisation.
- Station evolution.

### Part Three — Emotional Goal

The player should understand that they are no longer repairing wreckage. They are helping build a civilisation.

Every completed project should make the station feel more like a home than a survival shelter.

### Part Three — End Condition

The first major community project reaches completion. The player walks through the newly completed area. Crew members naturally begin using it.

No celebration is announced. Instead the player quietly witnesses ordinary life immediately filling the new space. Someone sits down with coffee. Someone starts working. Someone laughs. Mira immediately finds a new corner to explore.

Only after the player has experienced the completed space being lived in does the game acknowledge that construction is complete.

The next project is added to the station project board, leading into Part Four.

## Part Four: The First Tests

### Part Four — Purpose

The player has spent Chapter Three learning how a community functions. This section asks the player to demonstrate that learning.

The player must complete every test before Chapter Three can end. The order is chosen by the player.

Each completed test permanently reinforces a gameplay system introduced earlier in the chapter. Each test also demonstrates why every crew member remains essential. No crew member solves their own test alone. The player participates in every solution.

### Gameplay Philosophy

These are not examinations. These are ordinary problems experienced by a growing community.

Nothing is world-ending. Nothing is a boss battle. These moments teach that civilisation survives through countless small acts of cooperation.

### Test One: Rowan Ives

- Problem: a newly completed habitat module develops small structural problems.
- Player learns: preventative maintenance.
- Player decision: repair immediately, or continue observing before committing resources.
  - Immediate repair consumes resources now but prevents future failures.
  - Observation saves resources now but creates more repair work later.
- Neither choice is wrong. The player learns maintenance is about balancing risk.

### Test Two: Mara Chen

- Problem: power demand exceeds projections after recent construction.
- Player learns: power management.
- Player decision: reduce power to non-essential systems, reroute power and accept temporary instability, or delay expansion until additional generation is available.
- Each approach changes future engineering priorities.

### Test Three: Doctor Samira Kade

- Problem: several crew members are exhausted after continuous construction. No one is seriously injured.
- Player learns: communities require recovery.
- Player decision: continue construction, schedule mandatory rest, or rotate work shifts.
- Different choices alter project timelines and crew morale. The player learns that efficiency is not always measured by speed.

### Test Four: Chief Petty Officer Jonah Briggs

- Problem: three important projects now compete for the same resources.
- Player learns: prioritisation.
- Player decision: determine which project receives available materials first. The remaining projects wait. Nothing is cancelled. Only delayed.
- The player's priorities begin shaping the station's history.

### Test Five: Security Lead Talia Rusk

- Problem: routine inspection identifies several preventable safety hazards.
- Player learns: risk assessment.
- Player decision: correct every hazard before work resumes, or accept limited risk in order to maintain construction schedules.
- Future incidents reflect the player's philosophy toward safety.

### Test Six: Dr. Soren Qadir

- Problem: an unusual material recovered from Hearth cannot be identified.
- Player learns: scientific method.
- Player decision: experiment immediately, research existing records first, or ask other crew members for observations before forming conclusions.
- Different approaches unlock different Knowledge Archive entries. Curiosity is rewarded. Rushing assumptions is not.

### Test Seven: Mira Ives

- Problem: Mira notices something everyone else overlooked. The observation seems insignificant. The player chooses whether to investigate.
- Player learns: never dismiss curiosity.
- Player decision: investigate, or ignore.
  - If investigated, the player discovers an accessibility improvement, environmental shortcut, hidden resource or future story thread.
  - If ignored, the opportunity remains available later, but the community misses an immediate improvement.
- The lesson is that valuable ideas can come from anyone.

### Test Eight: The Vale System

- Problem: the previous seven tests produce conflicting priorities. Not everyone agrees.
- Player learns: leadership.
- Player decision: before making a final decision, the player must speak with every primary crew member. Every crew member explains their concern, their recommendation, and their reasoning. Only after hearing every perspective may the player make a final decision.
- The player's decision establishes the station's immediate direction moving into Chapter Four.

### Completion Requirement

All eight First Tests must be completed before the chapter can end. No crew member may be skipped.

Every completed test permanently expands the player's gameplay abilities. Every completed test reinforces one of the core philosophies of Resonance.

- Community.
- Accessibility.
- Curiosity.
- Adaptation.
- Shared knowledge.
- Hope.

## Chapter End

The station successfully overcomes its first ordinary challenges. The player has demonstrated competence in every core community system. The crew is no longer simply surviving together. They are functioning together.

As normal routines begin to resume, the unknown signal returns. This time it is stronger. Longer. Intentional.

Someone, or something, knows the station exists.

End Chapter Three.
