# Production Pipeline Bible

This document defines how new content enters the Resonance franchise, and it establishes that every future chapter, mechanic, character, species, civilisation, room and gameplay system follows the same workflow before it becomes part of the game.

## How to read this pipeline

The pipeline is a single ordered path from a first idea to finished, integrated content. Each stage below is a gate: work does not advance until the current gate is satisfied. The order matters, because early gates protect the identity of the franchise before any expensive production work begins, and later gates protect the quality and continuity of what has been built. Nothing skips a stage. A small change and a whole new campaign travel the same road; only the depth of scrutiny at each gate scales with the size of the change.

Every stage produces a written record. Because the repository is the single source of truth, a decision that lives only in conversation is not yet real. When a stage approves something, that outcome is captured in the relevant living document and, where it establishes a rule, in the Decision Log. This keeps the pipeline auditable, so anyone can see why a piece of content exists and which gates it passed.

The pipeline is itself accessibility-first. Its reviews, its records and its tools must be usable by contributors who work without sight or without hearing, and no stage may depend on a single sense to be completed. The workflow that builds an accessible game is built to be accessible in turn.

## Idea

The pipeline begins with an idea. An idea may be a new chapter, a new mechanic, a new character, a new species, a new civilisation, a new room, or a new gameplay system. Anyone may propose one, and the proposal is written down rather than left in conversation, because an unrecorded idea cannot be reviewed or remembered.

An idea is captured as a short design note in the design notes folder, where early concepts live before they are promoted to canon. The note states what the idea is, what problem it solves or what experience it creates, and which of the franchise's central themes it reinforces: community, accessibility, curiosity, adaptation, shared knowledge, or hope. An idea that reinforces none of these themes is reconsidered before it goes further.

At this stage the idea is deliberately cheap. It is not yet canon, nothing is binding, and it may be reshaped or abandoned freely. Recording it early means that later stages have something concrete to examine, and it means promising ideas are not lost simply because they arrived before the moment they were needed.

## Review

Review is the first shared read of the idea. A small group looks at the written note together and decides whether the idea is worth developing, whether it belongs in Resonance at all, and whether it duplicates or conflicts with work that already exists. This is a coarse filter, meant to catch problems while they are still inexpensive to fix.

The reviewers ask plain questions. Does the idea fit the purpose of the franchise? Does it respect the principle that technology exists to serve people rather than the reverse? Does it strengthen the world, or does it merely add volume? An idea may pass review unchanged, pass with requested revisions, be returned for more thought, or be declined with a recorded reason so the same ground is not walked again by accident.

Review never assesses an idea in isolation from the specialist gates that follow. Its job is to confirm that the idea is coherent and worth the attention of those gates. Passing review is permission to be examined seriously, not a promise of acceptance.

## Canon check

The canon check protects the continuity and the mysteries of the Resonance universe. Every idea that survives review is measured against the established record: the Project Constitution, the Canon Rules, the Universe Bible, the Campaign Bible, and the wider body of approved documents. The question at this gate is whether the idea can exist without breaking anything the franchise has already promised its players.

No established canon is retconned without explicit creator approval. If an idea would change, contradict, or quietly overwrite something that is already canon, it cannot proceed on its own authority. The conflict is raised openly, and only the creator may authorise a change to existing canon. When such a change is authorised, it is made by editing the relevant living document and recording the amendment and its reason in the Decision Log, so that the history of the change is preserved. Knowledge is corrected through discovery and formal amendment, never through silent rewriting.

The canon check also guards the franchise's deliberate mysteries. Some things are intended to remain unresolved, in particular anything concerning the Shawabti and anything concerning Faience. An idea may build around these mysteries, may deepen them, and may design how they are gradually revealed through play, but it may never supply an answer to a question the franchise has chosen to keep open. Where an idea touches such a mystery, the canon check confirms that it describes the mechanism of progressive revelation rather than the resolution. An idea that would resolve a protected mystery is refused at this gate.

## Representation review

The representation review ensures that every person, culture, species and community in Resonance is portrayed with respect and depth. Its standard is drawn from the Canon Rules: every recurring character is a complete person, representation informs identity but never replaces it, and no character exists solely to satisfy a representation, accessibility, gameplay or narrative requirement.

This gate rejects stereotype in all its forms. A character's ethnicity, gender identity, disability, communication style, faith or culture is never used as a shorthand for their personality, and no group is ever treated as the default against which others are measured. Human and non-human diversity is presented as an ordinary part of civilisation rather than as novelty or spectacle. Where an idea represents a specific real culture or a culturally specific identity, the review requires that the culture and community be portrayed thoughtfully and specifically, never as a generic label.

The representation review works closely with the narrative and accessibility gates, because how a character speaks, how they are recognised, and how their disability shapes them are all part of representing them honestly. The Player Identity and Character Presentation Bible governs how these people are presented, and this gate defers to it rather than inventing competing rules. An idea that reduces a person to a single trait, or that treats an aspect of identity as a joke, does not pass.

## Accessibility review

The accessibility review enforces the foundation of the franchise: accessibility is standard equipment, not a feature added at the end, and no player may ever be locked out because of disability. Every idea is examined against the Accessibility Bible, and an idea is not accepted at this gate until it meets that standard.

The review confirms that the idea can be experienced fully without sight and fully without hearing, that no important information ever depends on a single sense, and that every mechanic offers more than one input method and more than one output method. It confirms that any timed element can be removed or greatly extended when no auditory input is selected in single-player, that every movement mechanic has a stationary alternative, and that gestures and bindings remain customisable with no single mandatory gesture. It also confirms that accessibility in multiplayer stays individual, so that one player's settings never change another player's experience.

Accessibility is configured by experience rather than by diagnosis, and the review checks that new content honours this: it asks how a player wishes to interact with the world, never whether they have a condition. Because this gate defines an acceptance criterion rather than a preference, an idea that cannot be made accessible is not made at all until it can be. The specialist tools and metadata that carry accessibility through production are treated as first-class throughout the rest of the pipeline.

## Audio review

The audio review protects the acoustic identity of the world, because in Resonance audio is gameplay rather than decoration. Every object, creature, environment, vehicle, tool, article of clothing, building and technology possesses a deliberate acoustic identity, and this gate confirms that any new content arrives with one rather than being added silently.

The review, guided by the Audio Bible, checks that a player with their eyes closed could still learn from the new content where they are, what is happening, who is nearby, and what emotional state the moment carries. It checks that sound communicates information rather than merely accompanying it, that major locations remain recognisable by sound before dialogue confirms them, and that new audio fits the existing acoustic vocabulary of the franchise rather than contradicting it.

Silence is treated here as a deliberate acoustic identity in its own right. The review confirms that any quiet in the new content is authored to communicate emotion, danger, absence, distance or anticipation, and never exists merely because a sound has not yet been made. Because sound is progression, the review also considers how the new content changes the soundscape over time, so that growth in the world can be heard and not only seen.

## Narrative review

The narrative review examines how the content is written and how it fits the story of the franchise. Its standard is the franchise narrative style: dialogue that sounds like the person speaking it, exposition avoided in favour of discovery, and information revealed through lived experience and environmental storytelling rather than through lecture. This gate confirms that the new content tells its part of the story the way Resonance tells stories.

The review holds the content to the narrative rules in the Canon Rules. If the content is part of a chapter, the review checks that it grows gameplay and story together, answers a narrative question, and ends by asking a larger question than it answered. It confirms that Operation One is still shown as happening only once, seen through many perspectives, that no perspective contradicts another, and that the player remains a resident of the world rather than its owner or its chosen one. It confirms that any character voice matches who that character is, drawing on the representation review's findings.

The narrative review also protects tone. Grief, hope and humour each have a place, and each is written with care rather than convenience. Where the writing touches a deliberate mystery, this gate confirms, alongside the canon check, that the narrative reveals the mystery gradually and never resolves it. Content that reads as exposition, that flattens a character's voice, or that breaks the emotional truth of the world is returned for rewriting.

## Gameplay review

The gameplay review examines how the content plays. It confirms that the idea produces a real, operable experience and that this experience reinforces the central themes of the franchise. A mechanic that reinforces none of community, accessibility, curiosity, adaptation, shared knowledge or hope is reconsidered here rather than built.

This gate holds the content to the franchise rules about how players learn and progress. People introduce mechanics, so the review checks that any new ability is taught by a person before it becomes a player power, never handed over through a menu alone. It checks that the player is never made the smartest person in the room, that knowledge produces action rather than sitting as inert lore, and that failure changes history rather than simply restarting it. Where the content involves an expedition, the review confirms that it follows the rhythm of preparing, departing, exploring, recovering, and returning home changed, and that it returns with a meaningful contribution rather than resources alone.

The gameplay review also considers balance and continuity of experience across professions and perspectives, so that no profession is made obsolete and every recurring crew member keeps a meaningful role. Its findings feed directly into the technical and testing stages, which turn an approved design into a working, verified system.

## Technical review

The technical review examines whether and how the content can be built well. It measures the idea against the Code Standards: one shared codebase that produces native applications and a web build, correct semantics exposed to assistive technology, logical focus order, and announced state changes that matter to the player. The gate confirms that the content can be implemented without compromising these standards.

The review assesses feasibility, performance, and maintainability. It considers how the content fits the existing architecture and the reusable modules from which locations are assembled, so that new work extends the shared foundation rather than forking it. It confirms that the content will run acceptably across the supported targets, including mobile-only and touch-only play, and that it will not degrade the experience of players who rely on assistive technology. It also identifies the tooling, data and asset dependencies the content needs, so that nothing is discovered missing during implementation.

Crucially, this gate treats accessibility as an engineering acceptance criterion rather than an afterthought, in agreement with the accessibility review. A technically elegant implementation that cannot expose correct semantics, or that cannot be operated through more than one input method, is not technically acceptable. The technical review ends with an implementation plan that the next stage can follow.

## Implementation

Implementation is where the approved and reviewed idea is built. Only content that has passed the preceding gates reaches this stage, so implementation is production rather than experimentation. The work follows the implementation plan from the technical review and the designs approved by the earlier gates, and it builds on the one shared codebase rather than creating a competing version.

Implementation carries the earlier decisions into the actual content. Accessible input and output, acoustic identity, character voice, respectful representation and thematic purpose are built in from the first line of work, because they were designed in from the first gate. Accessibility is implemented as standard equipment, not bolted on afterwards, and every important action is given more than one activation method as it is built rather than later.

As the content takes shape, the people implementing it keep the written record current, noting decisions made during construction that the earlier stages could not foresee. Where such a decision changes something that other gates own, it is raised with the appropriate gate rather than settled quietly, so that the finished content still matches the reviews that approved it.

## Testing

Testing verifies that the built content works and that it works for everyone it is meant to serve. It follows the Playtesting Bible, which treats accessibility testing as a first-class development discipline equal to programming, audio and design. A feature is not finished until it has been validated with the players who will actually use it.

Testing checks the content against the things every test must verify. Every important action can be reached and performed through more than one input method. Important information is never conveyed by sound alone. Captions, braille, speech, haptics and the tactile language each carry the information they are responsible for. Timed actions are removed or greatly extended when no auditory input is selected in single-player. Accessibility settings stay individual in multiplayer. Gestures and bindings are fully customisable, with no single mandatory gesture.

Testing involves real players across a wide range of lived experiences and across the testing dimensions that cut through many players, rather than relying on assumption. Where testing reveals that a design must change, the change is made properly: the relevant living document is edited and the change is recorded, and findings still being explored may return to the design notes until they are ready. Testing continues until the content meets its standard, not merely until it runs.

## Localisation

Localisation prepares the content to be understood by players in every language and communication style the franchise supports. Because the world must be understandable without sight and without hearing, localisation covers far more than translated text: it includes spoken output, captions, braille output, sign-language representation, and the written material a player reads in logs, journals, reports and archive entries.

Localisation is planned from the design stages rather than retrofitted, so that text can expand or contract between languages without breaking layout, focus order or timing, and so that meaning survives translation intact. The gate confirms that culturally specific content is localised thoughtfully, in agreement with the representation review, and that nothing depends on a pun or idiom that cannot travel. It also confirms that the acoustic and tactile channels remain meaningful once localised, so that accessibility is preserved in every language rather than only in the original.

Localised content re-enters verification rather than being trusted on faith, because a translation can introduce errors that the original never had. This leads directly into regression testing, which confirms that localisation and every other change has left the wider game intact.

## Regression testing

Regression testing confirms that the new content, and everything done to prepare it, has not broken anything that already worked. New chapters, mechanics, characters, species, civilisations, rooms and systems all touch a shared world, and this gate checks that the shared world still behaves as its documents promise.

The gate re-runs the accessibility, audio, narrative, gameplay and technical checks against the surrounding game rather than only the new content. It confirms that existing accessible paths still function, that established acoustic identities are unchanged, that continuity across professions and perspectives still holds, and that the new content has not silently altered a mystery, a character, or a canon rule elsewhere. Localised builds are included, so that a change made for one language has not degraded another.

Automated tests in the tests folder support this stage, and accessibility behaviour is tested rather than assumed. When regression testing finds a fault, the content returns to the appropriate earlier stage to be corrected, and then travels forward through the gates again. Nothing advances toward approval while a known regression remains.

## Documentation

Documentation makes the finished content real in the single source of truth. Because an approved decision belongs inside the Markdown documentation rather than only in chat, this stage ensures that every living document affected by the content is edited to reflect what was built, so that the repository and the game agree.

Documentation follows the same standards as the content it describes. It uses proper Markdown headings in a logical hierarchy, never bold text in place of a heading, avoids unnecessary tables, prefers headings and lists, and remains navigable by screen readers. It maintains one living document per topic rather than creating competing versions, and it references other documents by name rather than restating or overriding what they own. Early or still-uncertain material stays in the design notes until it is ready to be promoted to canon.

This stage also records the reasoning behind the content, not only its result, so that a future contributor can understand why the content exists and which gates it passed. Where the content establishes a normative rule, that rule is written into the relevant Bible and prepared for the Decision Log, which the approval stage completes.

## Approval

Approval is the final decision that the content is ready to become part of the franchise. It is granted only after every preceding gate has been satisfied and every finding has been resolved, and it confirms that the content is coherent, accessible, respectful, true to canon, and technically sound as a whole rather than gate by gate.

Approval also confirms the handling of canon. Where the content changed existing canon, the approval stage verifies that the change carried explicit creator authorisation, that it was made by editing the relevant living document, and that deliberate mysteries remain unresolved. Where the content establishes a new rule or amends an existing one, approval ensures the change is recorded in the Decision Log with its date and reason, so the history of the decision is preserved. If the decision raises new uncertainties, they are added to Open Questions.

Approval is a deliberate act with a named authority behind it, not an automatic consequence of finishing the earlier stages. Content that is merely complete is not yet approved; content is approved when the responsible authority accepts it and the record of that acceptance is written down. Only approved content proceeds to integration.

## Repository integration

Repository integration places the approved content into the shared codebase and documentation, where it becomes part of the living game. Because the repository is the single source of truth, this is the moment the content stops being a proposal and becomes real for every contributor and every player.

Integration merges the built content, its assets, its localisation, its tests and its documentation together, so that the game, its accessibility, its audio, its record and its verification all arrive as one consistent whole. Nothing is integrated piecemeal in a way that would leave the documentation behind the game or the accessibility behind the feature. The integrated content extends the shared foundation and the reusable modules rather than forking them.

Once integrated, the content is part of the ordinary world and is maintained like everything else. Future ideas that touch it will pass through this same pipeline, and future regression testing will hold it to the promises it now makes. In this way the pipeline is continuous: every addition strengthens a living world that keeps growing without losing its identity.

## Relationship to other documents

- The Project Constitution is the governing authority for this pipeline, and it defines the principle that the repository is the single source of truth and that decisions are recorded rather than left in conversation.
- The Canon Rules document is the master rule list this pipeline enforces, especially at the canon check, and it is the standard against which continuity and deliberate mysteries are protected.
- The Code Standards document governs the technical review and repository integration, defining the one shared codebase, the semantic and screen-reader requirements, and the repository conventions this pipeline follows.
- The Playtesting Bible governs the testing and regression-testing stages, defining accessibility testing as a first-class discipline and the player groups and testing dimensions every test must involve.
- The Accessibility Bible governs the accessibility review and is the foundation no stage of this pipeline may violate.
- The Audio Bible governs the audio review and defines the acoustic identity, the role of sound as gameplay, and the deliberate use of silence that this pipeline preserves.
- The Decision Log records the adoption of this pipeline and of every rule, amendment, and canon change that passes through it, preserving the reason and date of each decision.
