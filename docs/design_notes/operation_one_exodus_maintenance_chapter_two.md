# Operation One: Exodus — Chapter Two: Roll Call

## Purpose

Chapter Two begins immediately where Chapter One ends.

There is no loading screen.

There is no mission complete screen.

The player experiences the destruction of CSV Hearth continuously.

The emotional goal of Chapter Two is to transform isolation into community.

The player begins alone.

By the end of the chapter the player has rebuilt the beginnings of a crew.

Unlike Chapter One, which teaches life aboard Hearth, Chapter Two teaches survival after losing Hearth.

The player is never rewarded simply for reaching an objective.

The player is rewarded when another human being comes home.

Every rescue changes both gameplay and the living soundscape.

## Emotional Themes

Chapter Two should reinforce:

Isolation

Shock

Hope

Responsibility

Community

Adaptation

Persistence

Every mechanic introduced during Chapter Two should reinforce one of these emotional themes.

## Gameplay Philosophy

Combat is not the focus.

Rescue is the focus.

Exploration is the focus.

Listening is the focus.

People are progression.

Every rescued crew member permanently expands gameplay.

The player should never feel as though they are collecting NPCs.

Instead they should feel they are rebuilding a community.

## Accessibility Philosophy

Accessibility remains invisible in the sense that it is fully integrated into society.

Nobody aboard Hearth comments on adaptive equipment.

Nobody explains accessibility technology.

It simply exists because this is how humanity has learned to build inclusive spacecraft.

Every interaction should reinforce that disabled people have always belonged aboard Hearth.

## Canon Rules

CSV Hearth cannot be repaired.

Hearth is gone.

Large pieces remain in space as debris.

Some sections remain pressurised.

Some remain powered.

Many become future exploration locations.

The player never rebuilds Hearth.

The player builds something new.

## Beginning State

The player is inside a damaged escape pod.

The pod is operational but unstable.

Power is limited.

Life support is functioning.

Communications are intermittent.

The cracked helmet survived.

Faience has not awakened.

The player has only basic emergency equipment.

Outside:

Fragments of CSV Hearth drift silently through surrounding space.

## Part One: The Silence After

### Part One — Purpose

Teach the player what survival sounds like.

Immediately establish that life aboard Hearth is over.

Contrast Chapter One's rich living soundscape with profound isolation.

### Opening

The chapter begins exactly where Chapter One ended.

General Quarters.

Impact.

Violent acceleration.

Metal tearing.

The escape pod launches.

For several seconds the player loses orientation.

No interface appears.

The player experiences confusion exactly as their character does.

### Part One — Sound Design

Initially the player hears:

Rapid breathing.

Heartbeat.

Emergency alarms.

Loose equipment striking the hull.

Rotational movement.

Then...

One by one...

The alarms begin shutting down.

The heartbeat slows.

Only ventilation remains.

Then the pod computer begins translating sensor information into audio.

Space is not silent.

The player hears translated sensor information.

Examples include:

Nearby debris.

Relative movement.

Radiation.

Beacon pulses.

Rotational drift.

Mass detection.

These are translated into meaningful sound rather than realistic vacuum audio.

This becomes one of Resonance's core mechanics.

### Part One — Mechanics Introduced

Player orientation.

Escape pod stabilisation.

Emergency battery management.

Life support awareness.

Basic thrust control.

Rotational control.

Orientation Layer activation.

Replay last spoken instruction.

Environmental audio balancing.

### Part One — Accessibility

If captions are enabled, every translated environmental sound should also appear descriptively.

If DeafBlind settings are enabled, important translated sounds should also be available through tactile output.

Players using headphones, hearing aids, cochlear implants or open speakers retain independent volume controls configured during onboarding.

### Part One — Emotional Goal

The player should feel:

Confused.

Small.

Alone.

But alive.

### Part One — End Condition

The pod becomes stable.

Emergency systems report operational status.

A prompt suggests activating the emergency beacon.

No NPC has yet answered.

## Part Two: Roll Call

### Part Two — Purpose

Teach communication.

Teach search.

Teach hope.

The player begins searching for survivors.

Not everyone answers.

The player must experience uncertainty.

### Part Two — Sound Design

Static.

Emergency beacons.

Automated distress loops.

Corrupted transmissions.

Broken voices.

Silence.

Occasional debris impacts.

The player should realise that many people aboard Hearth did not survive.

### Part Two — Mechanics Introduced

Emergency beacon activation.

Beacon listening mode.

Signal filtering.

Signal strength interpretation.

Bearing estimation.

Long-range scanning.

Replay previous transmission.

Bookmark signal.

Ignore signal.

Prioritise signal.

### Part Two — Gameplay

The player begins calling survivors.

Most channels remain silent.

Some transmit only automated emergency messages.

Some signals disappear before they can be reached.

Some are already too late.

The game should avoid confirming every outcome.

Uncertainty is emotionally important.

Eventually...

A familiar voice.

Not dramatic.

Not shouted.

Simply tired.

"...Maintenance..."

Pause.

"...Rowan..."

Long pause.

"...Anybody..."

The player immediately recognises Rowan because they spent an entire week working with him during Chapter One.

### Part Two — Emotional Goal

The first familiar voice should immediately reduce the player's sense of isolation.

Hope returns before safety does.

### Part Two — End Condition

The player locks onto Rowan's beacon.

Navigation begins.

## Part Three: Closing The Distance

### Part Three — Purpose

Teach movement through open space.

Teach momentum.

Teach precision.

Teach that rescue requires patience rather than speed.

### Part Three — Gameplay

The player must physically reach Rowan.

No automatic navigation exists.

The player learns:

Thrust.

Drift.

Momentum.

Relative velocity.

Collision avoidance.

Rotational alignment.

Safe approach.

### Environment

The journey passes through debris from Hearth.

Examples include:

Cargo containers.

Destroyed maintenance equipment.

Children's toys.

Medical supplies.

Pieces of hallway.

Emergency lights still blinking.

Hydroponic plants drifting through space.

Personal belongings.

These objects remind the player that Hearth was a home rather than merely a spacecraft.

### Part Three — Sound Design

Slow rotation.

Small debris impacts.

Beacon strength increasing.

Hull resonance.

Occasional emergency broadcasts.

Very distant explosions.

No music.

Allow isolation to dominate.

### Part Three — Accessibility

Orientation Layer continuously provides optional heading reminders.

Players may request destination information at any time.

Examples:

"Rowan Ives.

Bearing zero-eight-seven.

Distance three hundred twelve metres.

Relative closing speed safe."

Blind players should never lose orientation because of free movement.

The player should always be able to request current heading and destination without interrupting gameplay.

### Rowan Communication

As the player approaches, Rowan occasionally transmits.

His speech remains practical.

He is trying to solve problems while waiting.

Examples include discussing hull damage, checking life support, or reminding the player not to rush the approach.

He sounds like the same mentor the player worked with during Chapter One.

### Part Three — Emotional Goal

By the end of Part Three the player should no longer feel completely alone.

Someone is waiting.

Someone knows them.

Someone believes they are coming.

### Part Three — End Condition

The player reaches visual and sensor range of Rowan's escape pod.

Docking has not yet begun.

Part Three ends as the player prepares to make first contact.

## Part Four: First Dock

### Part Four — Purpose

Teach docking.

Teach trust.

Teach that rescue is a careful process rather than a quick interaction.

The player is rescuing a person, not collecting an objective.

### Beginning

The player reaches Rowan's pod.

The pod is rotating slowly.

Its emergency beacon remains active.

Life support is functioning but deteriorating.

The player must observe before acting.

Approaching too quickly can damage both pods.

### Part Four — Mechanics Introduced

Velocity matching.

Rotational matching.

Magnetic docking clamps.

Emergency EVA.

Hull inspection.

Manual hatch access.

Emergency cutting tools.

Pressure equalisation.

Emergency decompression procedures.

### Part Four — Accessibility

The Orientation Layer continuously announces:

Relative rotation.

Closing speed.

Dock alignment.

Safe approach angles.

Warnings should be descriptive rather than alarming.

Players may replay docking instructions at any time.

Blind players should be capable of docking independently.

### Environmental Audio

The player hears:

Metal creaking.

Air escaping.

Loose tools rolling.

Hull strain.

Emergency fans.

Occasional electrical pops.

Rowan moving inside.

The sounds become clearer as docking progresses.

### Rowan

Rowan does not panic.

He continues solving problems while waiting.

He occasionally reports:

Remaining oxygen.

Power status.

Hull condition.

He reminds the player:

"Easy...

Don't rush it.

We only get one chance."

His confidence reassures the player.

### Part Four — Emotional Goal

Docking should feel like helping a trusted mentor rather than completing a puzzle.

### Part Four — End Condition

Pods successfully dock.

Pressure equalisation begins.

The player prepares to enter Rowan's pod.

## Part Five: Bringing Rowan Home

### Part Five — Purpose

Teach boarding.

Teach environmental problem solving.

Teach that rescue continues after docking.

### Part Five — Gameplay

Rowan's pod remains dangerous.

Possible problems include:

Low oxygen.

Minor hull breach.

Jammed hatch.

Loose equipment.

Electrical hazards.

The player must stabilise the environment before Rowan can safely transfer.

### Part Five — Mechanics Introduced

Pod entry.

Interior scanning.

Portable repair tools.

Emergency environmental stabilisation.

Crew extraction.

Emergency inventory transfer.

### Part Five — Accessibility

All hazards should have identifiable audio signatures.

Players using tactile output receive hazard feedback through vibration.

Players may replay Rowan's last instructions.

### Dialogue Philosophy

Rowan continues behaving like a maintenance supervisor.

He naturally explains what he is doing.

He never lectures.

Teaching happens through conversation while working.

Example topics include:

Temporary hull patches.

Battery conservation.

Emergency repairs.

Tool organisation.

Salvage priorities.

### Part Five — Emotional Goal

The player should feel they are rescuing someone who immediately begins helping in return.

### Part Five — End Condition

Rowan transfers safely into the player's pod.

No reward appears.

No portrait lights.

Nothing celebrates yet.

## Part Six: The First Home

### Part Six — Purpose

Teach that people—not objectives—create progress.

Introduce Resonance's progression philosophy.

### Part Six — Gameplay

After Rowan enters the pod...

Nothing happens.

The player is free to move around.

Rowan immediately begins working.

He asks where emergency tools were stored.

He begins inspecting systems.

The player hears him organising equipment.

Opening storage compartments.

Sorting tools.

Checking seals.

Tightening fittings.

Repairing leaks.

Testing pressure.

Replacing damaged components.

The player is not asked to press a button.

The player simply lives with another person again.

### Environmental Progression

Over several minutes the environment changes naturally.

Examples:

Hull rattles become quieter.

Ventilation becomes smoother.

Electrical interference decreases.

Emergency lighting stabilises.

Storage becomes organised.

The pod simply feels safer.

The player hears these changes before anything appears on screen.

### Canon Progression Rule

A rescued crew member is not considered "home" when they enter the player's vessel.

They become home when they begin contributing to the community.

Only after the environment has changed...

Only after Rowan has begun repairing the pod...

A gentle confirmation tone sounds.

Rowan's portrait illuminates.

No text reads:

"NPC Acquired."

Instead the game quietly confirms:

Rowan is now part of the crew.

### Part Six — Mechanics Introduced

Crew contribution.

Passive NPC behaviour.

Living soundscape progression.

Community growth.

Crew abilities.

Long-term upgrades.

### Part Six — Emotional Goal

The player should understand:

People change places.

Places become home because of people.

### Rowan's First Lesson

Only after settling in does Rowan say something like:

"We've got enough to keep breathing...

Now let's see who else made it."

This naturally transitions into the search for the next survivor.

### Part Six — End Condition

Rowan activates a stronger search array using salvaged maintenance equipment.

A second emergency beacon is detected.

It belongs to Mara Chen.

The player hears something unusual before hearing her voice.

Electrical relay chatter.

Power fluctuations.

Transformer hum.

Intermittent arcing.

Rowan smiles quietly.

"That's Mara.

Only one engineer I know can make a pod sound like it's arguing with itself."

The player sets course toward Mara.

Chapter continues.

## Design Note: Rowan's Continuing Presence

Rowan should never become passive after rescue.

He should constantly be heard repairing, organising, improving and maintaining the growing station.

His presence should permanently alter the audio environment.

Players should come to associate the sound of tools, repairs and steady maintenance with safety.

This reinforces the central philosophy of Resonance:

Community is something you hear before you see.

## Part Seven: The Community Returns

### Part Seven — Purpose

Teach that every rescue permanently changes life aboard the player's growing station.

The player is no longer simply surviving.

The player is rebuilding civilisation.

### Structure

The remaining rescues should not feel repetitive.

Each rescue teaches a different system.

Each survivor changes the soundscape.

Each survivor changes daily life.

Each survivor changes conversations.

The player should begin hearing people checking on one another.

Meals.

Shift changes.

People laughing.

People arguing.

Maintenance requests.

Medical checkups.

The station slowly begins sounding alive.

## Rescue Order

The rescue order for the Maintenance campaign is canon.

### 1. Rowan Ives

Already completed.

Unlocks:

Repairs.

Salvage.

Hull maintenance.

Basic fabrication.

Emergency construction.

### 2. Mara Chen

Purpose:

Restore engineering.

Mara's pod is heavily damaged but still functioning.

She has already bypassed several failed systems before the player arrives.

The player is rescuing time, not competence.

Mara teaches:

Power routing.

Generator management.

Ship upgrades.

Engineering diagnostics.

Advanced audio navigation.

Environmental Change:

Electrical hum stabilises.

Power interruptions disappear.

Navigation sounds become cleaner.

Only after these changes...

Mara's portrait illuminates.

### 3. Doctor Samira Kade

Purpose:

Restore medical capability.

Her pod contains injured survivors.

The rescue introduces:

Medical triage.

Environmental contamination.

Medical inventory.

Crew health.

Long-term injuries.

Environmental Change:

Medical monitors begin operating.

Health alerts become available.

Crew breathing sounds healthier.

A treatment room gradually comes online.

Only then...

Samira's portrait illuminates.

### 4. Chief Petty Officer Jonah Briggs

Purpose:

Restore organisation.

Jonah has already begun coordinating other survivors over emergency radio.

He has lists.

Maps.

Beacon locations.

Supply estimates.

He immediately begins organising rescue priorities.

Gameplay unlocks:

Mission planning.

Search boards.

Crew scheduling.

Priority rescue.

Logistics.

Environmental Change:

Organised radio traffic.

Updated survivor boards.

Mission planning discussions.

Regular shift briefings.

Only then...

Jonah's portrait illuminates.

### 5. Security Lead Talia Rusk

Purpose:

Restore security.

Combat is introduced only now.

By this point the player understands what is worth protecting.

Gameplay unlocks:

Boarding actions.

Threat response.

Security patrols.

Weapon lockers.

Defensive systems.

Environmental Change:

Security patrol reports.

Door security checks.

Weapon safety routines.

Defensive planning.

Only then...

Talia's portrait illuminates.

### 6. Dr. Soren Qadir

Purpose:

Restore scientific understanding.

Soren introduces:

Artefact research.

Knowledge Archive.

Pattern analysis.

Signal decoding.

Historical investigation.

Research is not optional.

Knowledge changes gameplay.

Environmental Change:

Research equipment activates.

Artefact scanners begin operating.

Scientific discussions become audible.

Historical archive access becomes available.

Only then...

Soren's portrait illuminates.

### 7. Mira Ives

Purpose:

Restore hope.

Mira should be emotionally one of the hardest rescues.

The player has wondered about her throughout the chapter.

She has survived.

She is frightened.

She is still communicating.

Her neural AAC interface continues functioning.

Gameplay unlocks:

Advanced AAC interaction.

Alternative communication.

Adaptive interface customisation.

Patience.

Environmental Change:

AAC notifications.

Child laughter.

Questions.

Drawing.

Curiosity.

The station finally sounds like families live there again.

Only then...

Mira's portrait illuminates.

### 8. The Vale System

Purpose:

Complete the first crew.

This is the emotional climax of Chapter Two.

## The Last Signal

The player has rescued everyone else.

One signal remains.

Weak.

Broken.

Intermittent.

It repeatedly disappears.

No identification.

Eventually...

"This is...

...the Vale System..."

Static.

Silence.

Then...

"This is Captain Elian of the Vale System."

Pause.

"This is Captain Naomi of the Vale System."

Pause.

"This is Captain Gideon of the Vale System."

Pause.

"This is Captain Iris of the Vale System."

Long pause.

"...reporting from Escape Pod Seven."

The player assumes four people occupy the pod.

### Boarding

The pod is badly damaged.

Life support is nearly exhausted.

Power is failing.

The player docks carefully.

Boards.

Searches.

Eventually discovers:

One pressure couch.

One flight harness.

One environmental suit.

One person.

The player hears:

"Oh..."

A gentle laugh.

"You expected four."

Another voice.

"Happens all the time."

Another.

"We're the Vale System."

The story moves on.

No lecture.

No explanation.

No educational monologue.

Everyone aboard already understands.

The player simply catches up.

## The First Council

Once everyone has been rescued...

The player walks through the station.

Do not force objectives.

Allow exploration.

The player hears:

Medical.

Maintenance.

Engineering.

Research.

Security.

Crew conversations.

Someone cooking.

Someone laughing.

Someone complaining about coffee.

Someone asking for tools.

Life.

For the first time since Hearth...

It sounds like home.

## Naming The Ship

The player still does not have a ship.

They have an improvised station built from escape pods and salvaged modules.

The crew begins discussing what to call it.

Do not immediately open a naming interface.

Instead allow a natural conversation.

Examples:

"We can't keep calling it 'the pods.'"

"This thing needs a name."

"We're going to be living here awhile."

Crew members offer suggestions.

Some are serious.

Some humorous.

Some sentimental.

After the conversation naturally pauses...

The damaged helmet quietly speaks.

Not fully Faience.

Not yet.

Only a damaged interface.

It asks:

"Would you like to assign a designation to your vessel?"

Only then does the naming interface appear.

The player chooses the station's name.

This becomes the permanent name carried throughout the campaign unless the player later chooses to rename it through established game systems.

## Knowledge Archive

Immediately after naming the vessel...

Soren notices something.

The damaged helmet contains information it should not possess.

The first entry of the Knowledge Archive becomes available.

Players may ignore it.

Players may study it.

This choice matters.

Throughout Resonance:

Knowledge unlocks options.

Knowledge reduces difficulty.

Knowledge reveals hidden solutions.

Players who refuse to engage with history and research can still finish the game.

However...

Combat becomes more dangerous.

Negotiation becomes harder.

Certain rescue options disappear.

Knowledge is progression.

## Chapter End

The newly named vessel stabilises.

The crew finally rests.

The player hears normal life beginning again.

Then...

A signal.

Not human.

Not from Hearth.

Not emergency traffic.

Something...

Different.

Everyone falls silent.

End Chapter Two.

## Chapter Two End State

The player has rebuilt the first crew.

Every primary NPC is aboard.

The station has become a home.

The player has named it.

The Knowledge Archive has opened.

The mystery has expanded.

The player is no longer surviving alone.

The player is responsible for a community.

Chapter Three begins with that responsibility.
