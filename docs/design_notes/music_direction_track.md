# Music Direction Track

This document is the compositional brief for Resonance: Fractured Frequency. It defines the adaptive score architecture, motif construction method, leitmotif assignments, and the no-audio equivalents that ensure every musical beat is received by every player. It does not restate the Audio Bible's adaptive music section: that section is the philosophical foundation and must be read before this document.

The Audio Bible is the source of truth for the adaptive music philosophy, the emotional states music responds to, and the principle that the soundtrack tells part of the story rather than only setting a mood. This document is the compositional brief that implements those principles.

Shawabti music does not appear in this document. The acoustic identity of the Shawabti is revealed through play. No Shawabti motifs, scales, or compositional logic are defined here. When Chapter Five's design brief reaches the music team, that brief will be reviewed against the canon rule that deliberate mysteries are revealed through play before any Shawabti musical material is committed.

## Adaptive score architecture

The score is a layered system of stems and parameters rather than a library of discrete cues. Each situation is defined by a set of emotional parameters — drawn from the Audio Bible's list of emotional states music responds to — and the score responds to those parameters in real time. No two playthroughs produce an identical score.

The architecture has four layers.

The foundation layer is always present. It establishes the acoustic identity of the environment and carries the long-form emotional arc of the chapter. It is quiet enough that all other layers are audible above it and that its absence would be felt.

The character layer responds to which named characters are nearby and how those relationships currently stand. Leitmotifs from the assignments below appear in this layer, stated in full or in fragment depending on the relational state.

The situation layer responds to immediate gameplay states: danger, discovery, mission progress, community events, and the states the Audio Bible lists. This layer has the most direct relationship to haptic and caption output.

The profession layer provides the attentional framing each profession's mix applies. The stems are shared; the mix template shapes which layer is prominent.

## Motif construction

Motifs in Resonance are short, non-repetitive melodic ideas that are recognisable after two hearings and memorable without being intrusive. Each motif satisfies the following requirements before it is used in production.

- It is singable or tapable by a person who has heard it twice. If it cannot be reproduced by a listener without musical training, it is too complex.
- It carries a defined emotional meaning that holds across major, minor, and sparse restatements.
- It has a haptic equivalent: a rhythm and pressure pattern that communicates the same emotional meaning through touch, authored by the accessibility team in partnership with the composer.
- It can be stated in under eight seconds and recognised in under three.

Motif variants — reharmonisation, inversion, fragmentation, augmentation — must remain recognisable as the same motif to a non-musician. If a variant requires musical training to connect it to the source, it is too transformed.

## Leitmotif assignments

The following recurring characters and factions have assigned leitmotifs. Each assignment names the character or faction, the motif's emotional meaning, and the contexts in which it appears. The specific melodic content is composed against this brief; the brief governs the composition.

### CSV Hearth

The ship's motif represents home, continuity, and the community that lives aboard it. It appears in the foundation layer whenever the player is aboard Hearth and is felt as an absence when the player is away. As the community grows across chapters, the motif gains harmonic weight and textural density: a community of twelve sounds different from a community of two hundred.

### Faience

Faience's motif represents intelligence, partnership, and the question the franchise asks about what trust with an AI costs. It does not resolve: it ends on a note that asks rather than answers. It appears in the character layer whenever Faience speaks or acts with significant intent. As integration deepens across chapters, the motif becomes more harmonically integrated with the player character's own motif. As trust is damaged, it becomes more isolated.

### Doctor Soren Qadir

Doctor Qadir's motif represents competence, pragmatism, and the weight of institutional knowledge. It is grounded in rhythm more than melody: it feels like someone who solves problems. It appears in the character layer when Doctor Qadir is nearby and in scenes where his history becomes relevant.

### Mara Chen

Mara's motif represents community, persistence, and the ordinary courage of people who keep things running. It is warm and unspectacular. It appears in the character layer when Mara is nearby and in scenes of community recovery and daily life.

### The attack event

The attack is not scored with a combat motif. The score for the attack is built from fragments of Hearth's motif under structural duress: the same harmonic material heard in a state of damage. This communicates that the attack is not external action but harm done to something the player already knows.

### Human community factions

Each of the primary human communities aboard Hearth has a cultural sound identity as defined in the Audio Bible's cultural sound identity section. Their musical identity in the score reflects the cultural markers of that community — rhythm, instrument family, melodic character — without reducing any community to a single cultural stereotype. Cultural consultants review musical assignments for human communities before they are used in production.

## Silence as a compositional tool

Silence is defined in the Canon Rules as a deliberate storytelling tool. In the score, silence is used for three purposes.

Silence communicates absence. When a character or place that has been musically present becomes silent, that silence is heard as loss before dialogue names it.

Silence communicates danger. The withdrawal of the foundation layer before a threat arrives prepares the player without telegraphing the specific form of the threat.

Silence communicates weight. After a significant player decision, a beat of silence before the score resumes allows the choice to land. The score does not comment on the decision; it gives the player space to feel it.

Silence is never the result of a mix failing to reach a transition. Every silence is authored. If the score is silent, it is because the brief requires it.

## No-audio equivalents for every musical beat

The score communicates emotion and narrative information. Players who cannot hear the score must receive the same emotional and narrative information through other channels. This requirement is authored at the compositional level, not added during post-production.

For each significant musical moment — a leitmotif statement, a situational layer shift, a silence — the brief includes a no-audio equivalent specification. This specification is authored by the composer and the accessibility lead together before the moment is recorded.

No-audio equivalents take one or more of the following forms.

A haptic pattern communicates the emotional meaning of the musical moment through rhythm and pressure. The haptic pattern is authored against the motif's haptic equivalent as defined in the motif construction requirements above.

An environmental narration moment embeds the same emotional beat in a non-player character's spoken line, action, or presence in the scene. This is not an exposition line: it is a narrative beat that carries the same weight the music would carry.

A caption communicates the musical moment in plain language when the player's caption settings include score description. Score captions follow the Accessibility Bible's caption authoring standard: they communicate emotional meaning, not technical description.

The Accessibility Bible is the authority for which of these equivalents is required in each context. This brief names the moment; the Accessibility Bible defines the channel.

## Cultural music in human communities

Human communities aboard Hearth bring their own musical cultures. These appear in the cultural sound identity layer of the score and in the environmental ambience of community spaces. Each cultural musical element satisfies the following requirements.

It is developed with cultural consultation, as the Production Pipeline Bible's representation review gate requires.

It is authentic to the culture it represents in character, not in surface feature. A musical element that reduces a culture to a recognisable cliché does not pass the representation review gate.

It does not compete with the gameplay information the score is carrying. Cultural music in community spaces is mixed so that players who need to attend to the situation layer can do so without the cultural layer obscuring it.

## Relationship to other documents

This document is the compositional brief. It does not define the acoustic philosophy or the emotional states the score responds to.

- The audio production track covers the recording, editing, naming, integration, and testing workflow that brings this brief's musical content into the game.
