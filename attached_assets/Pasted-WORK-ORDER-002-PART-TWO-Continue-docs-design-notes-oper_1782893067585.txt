# Part Four

## First Dock

### Purpose

Teach docking.

Teach trust.

Teach that rescue is a careful process rather than a quick interaction.

The player is rescuing a person, not collecting an objective.

---

### Beginning

The player reaches Rowan's pod.

The pod is rotating slowly.

Its emergency beacon remains active.

Life support is functioning but deteriorating.

The player must observe before acting.

Approaching too quickly can damage both pods.

---

### Mechanics Introduced

Velocity matching.

Rotational matching.

Magnetic docking clamps.

Emergency EVA.

Hull inspection.

Manual hatch access.

Emergency cutting tools.

Pressure equalization.

Emergency decompression procedures.

---

### Accessibility

The Orientation Layer continuously announces:

Relative rotation.

Closing speed.

Dock alignment.

Safe approach angles.

Warnings should be descriptive rather than alarming.

Players may replay docking instructions at any time.

Blind players should be capable of docking independently.

---

### Environmental Audio

The player hears:

Metal creaking.

Air escaping.

Loose tools rolling.

Hull strain.

Emergency fans.

Occasional electrical pops.

Rowan moving inside.

The sounds become clearer as docking progresses.

---

### Rowan

Rowan does not panic.

He continues solving problems while waiting.

He occasionally reports:

Remaining oxygen.

Power status.

Hull condition.

He reminds the player:

"Easy...

Don't rush it.

We only get one chance."

His confidence reassures the player.

---

### Emotional Goal

Docking should feel like helping a trusted mentor rather than completing a puzzle.

---

### End Condition

Pods successfully dock.

Pressure equalization begins.

The player prepares to enter Rowan's pod.

---

# Part Five

## Bringing Rowan Home
 ### Purpose

Teach boarding.

Teach environmental problem solving.

Teach that rescue continues after docking.

---

### Gameplay

Rowan's pod remains dangerous.

Possible problems include:

Low oxygen.

Minor hull breach.

Jammed hatch.

Loose equipment.

Electrical hazards.

The player must stabilize the environment before Rowan can safely transfer.

---

### Mechanics Introduced

Pod entry.

Interior scanning.

Portable repair tools.

Emergency environmental stabilization.

Crew extraction.

Emergency inventory transfer.

---

### Accessibility

All hazards should have identifiable audio signatures.

Players using tactile output receive hazard feedback through vibration.

Players may replay Rowan's last instructions.

---

### Dialogue Philosophy

Rowan continues behaving like a maintenance supervisor.

He naturally explains what he is doing.

He never lectures.

Teaching happens through conversation while working.

Example topics include:

Temporary hull patches.

Battery conservation.

Emergency repairs.

Tool organization.

Salvage priorities.

---

### Emotional Goal

The player should feel they are rescuing someone who immediately begins helping in return.

---

### End Condition

Rowan transfers safely into the player's pod.

No reward appears.

No portrait lights.

Nothing celebrates yet.

---

# Part Six

## The First Home

### Purpose

Teach that people—not objectives—create progress.

Introduce Resonance's progression philosophy.

---

### Gameplay

After Rowan enters the pod...

Nothing happens.

The player is free to move around.

Rowan immediately begins working.

He asks where emergency tools were stored.

He begins inspecting systems.

The player hears him organizing equipment.

Opening storage compartments.

Sorting tools.

Checking seals.

Tightening fittings.

Repairing leaks.

Testing pressure.

Replacing damaged components.

The player is not asked to press a button.

The player simply lives with another person again.

---

### Environmental Progression

Over several minutes the environment changes naturally.

Examples:

Hull rattles become quieter.

Ventilation becomes smoother.

Electrical interference decreases.

Emergency lighting stabilizes.

Storage becomes organized.

The pod simply feels safer.

The player hears these changes before anything appears on screen.

---

### Canon Progression Rule

A rescued crew member is not considered "home" when they enter the player's vessel.

They become home when they begin contributing to the community.

Only after the environment has changed...

Only after Rowan has begun repairing the pod...

A gentle confirmation tone sounds.

Rowan's portrait illuminates.

No text reads:

"NPC Acquired."

Instead the game quietly confirms:

Rowan is now part of the crew.

---

### Mechanics Introduced

Crew contribution.

Passive NPC behavior.

Living soundscape progression.

Community growth.

Crew abilities.

Long-term upgrades.

---

### Emotional Goal

The player should understand:

People change places.

Places become home because of people.

---

### Rowan's First Lesson

Only after settling in does Rowan say something like:

"We've got enough to keep breathing...

Now let's see who else made it."

This naturally transitions into the search for the next survivor.

---

### End Condition

Rowan activates a stronger search array using salvaged maintenance equipment.

A second emergency beacon is detected.

It belongs to Mara Chen.

The player hears something unusual before hearing her voice.

Electrical relay chatter.

Power fluctuations.

Transformer hum.

Intermittent arcing.

Rowan smiles quietly.

"That's Mara.

Only one engineer I know can make a pod sound like it's arguing with itself."

The player sets course toward Mara.

Chapter continues.

---

# Design Notes

Rowan should never become passive after rescue.

He should constantly be heard repairing, organizing, improving and maintaining the growing station.

His presence should permanently alter the audio environment.

Players should come to associate the sound of tools, repairs and steady maintenance with safety.

This reinforces the central philosophy of Resonance:

Community is something you hear before you see.
